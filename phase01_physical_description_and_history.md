# Phase 1 — Physical Description & Historical Provenance

> Part of the [Shroud of Turin Research Plan](shroud_of_turin_research_plan.md). Researched: 2026-04-24.

---

## Part A: Physical Description

### A1. Dimensions and Material

The Shroud measures approximately **437 cm long × 111 cm wide × 0.34 mm thick** (roughly 14 ft 5 in × 3 ft 7 in). A precise 2000 survey by Barberis and Zaccone found slight asymmetry: long sides 437.7 cm and 441.5 cm; short sides 112.5 cm and 113 cm — the result of centuries of handling. A secondary longitudinal seam (~3.80 m × 0.08 m) runs along the left edge, attached by a single needle stitch, suggesting it was cut from a larger piece and later re-sewn. Official weight has never been published.

**Fiber:** Unbleached linen (*Linum usitatissimum*). Individual fibers are ~10–20 μm in diameter; each thread/yarn is ~0.15 mm in diameter and contains approximately **70–120 individual flax fibers**.

**Spinning:** Both warp and weft are spun with a **Z-twist** (clockwise), consistent with ancient and medieval linen. Spinning is irregular — neighboring warp threads can vary in diameter by a factor of three, consistent with hand-spinning.

**Weave:** A **3:1 chevron herringbone twill** — each weft thread passes over three warp threads and under one, in a diagonal that reverses direction at regular intervals to create the herringbone ("V") pattern. The cloth contains ~53.5 such chevron units, each ~80 warp threads wide. This weave requires a **four-shaft treadle loom**; textile expert **Gabriel Vial** (Technical General Secretary, CIETA) confirmed this from systematic errors in rib spacing that always occur as multiples of four (36 or 44 threads per rib instead of 40), consistent with a four-shaft loom.

**Thread count:** Warp ~38.6 threads/cm; weft ~35.5 threads/cm — averaging slightly over **35 threads/cm** (≈89 threads/inch). Comparable to the finest Egyptian mummy fabrics (~30 threads/cm).

**Key source:** Gabriel Vial, "The Shroud of Turin: A Technical Study," *Sindon* N.S. (Nos. 38–39), available on shroud.com.

### A2. Current Condition and the 2002 Restoration

**Pre-2002 state:** Since 1534 the cloth had borne **14 large triangular patches** and **8 smaller patches** sewn by Poor Clare nuns over the burn holes from the 1532 Chambéry fire, plus a full-length **Holland cloth backing** sewn onto the entire reverse face — also 1534 — to stabilize the cloth. The reverse had not been seen since.

**The 2002 restoration (June 20 – July 23, 2002):** Led by Swiss textile conservator **Mechthild Flury-Lemberg** and restorer **Irene Tomedi** in the New Sacristy of Turin Cathedral:
- All **30 patches** (14 large + 8 small + the Holland backing) were removed.
- The **reverse face** was exposed and fully photographed for the first time in 468 years.
- Organic residues were removed from the surface.
- A new, non-invasive support cloth was fitted, allowing inspection at any point.

**Controversy:** The work was done without full consultation with the international sindonological scientific community. Critics argued that the charred threads and patches — in contact with the Shroud for 468 years — were discarded, potentially destroying scientific evidence. Cardinal Severino Poletto authorized the work on conservation grounds (carbonized fragments were spreading carbon particles across the cloth).

### A3. The Double Body Image

The Shroud carries **two faint, brownish-yellow images**: one frontal (face to feet) and one dorsal (back of head to heels), oriented **head-to-head** at the center of the cloth. The arrangement implies the cloth was laid flat under the body (dorsal image), then the other half was folded over the front.

**Frontal image shows:**
- Face with closed eyes, beard and moustache, long hair to the shoulders
- Both arms crossed over the pelvis (right over left), fingers elongated, **no thumbs visible** — consistent with a flexion reflex caused by nail trauma to the median nerve at the wrist
- Torso with scourge marks across chest and abdomen
- A wound on the right side of the chest with apparent blood and watery fluid
- Legs and feet; large bloodstain at the right foot

**Dorsal image shows:**
- Back of head and hair
- Shoulders with abrasion marks consistent with carrying a heavy object
- Entire back with dense scourge patterns
- Buttocks, thighs, calves, and heels

**Body proportions:** Estimated height **1.70–1.80 m** (5 ft 7 in – 5 ft 11 in), well muscled, ~70–80 kg. The image is **monochromatic** — straw-yellow discolorations of individual linen fibrils against a pale-yellow background.

### A4. Why the Image Looks Like a Photographic Negative — and the VP-8 3D Relief

**The negative character:** On the cloth, areas closest to the body (nose, brow ridges, cheekbones) are **darker**; areas farther from the cloth (eye sockets, sides of the face) are **lighter** — the opposite of how light falls on a face in life, and the opposite of how a medieval painter would render a face. When Secondo Pia photographed the Shroud on **28 May 1898**, his photographic negative (which re-inverts tones) revealed a **natural, well-lit positive portrait**. The Shroud had been a negative all along; photography made it legible. This strongly implies the cloth recorded a **distance-related signal** (body-to-cloth proximity) rather than reflected light — something no medieval artist could have deliberately encoded centuries before photography.

**VP-8 Image Analyzer (1976):** In 1976, physicists **John Jackson** and **Eric Jumper** (U.S. Air Force Academy) fed the Shroud image into a **VP-8 Image Analyzer** — an analog NASA-derived device that converts pixel brightness into vertical height — at Sandia Scientific Laboratories with **William Mottern**. A normal photograph fed into a VP-8 produces a flat, distorted mess, because photographs encode reflected light, not spatial distance. The Shroud image, however, produced an **anatomically correct 3D topographic relief** of a human face and body. Image darkness varied precisely as a function of cloth-to-body distance: darker = closer. This spatial encoding is not found in any known painting or photograph. The discovery directly motivated Jackson and Jumper to organize STURP.

**Formal publication:** Jackson, J.P., Jumper, E.J., & Ercoline, W.R., "Correlation of image intensity on the Turin Shroud with the 3-D structure of a human body shape," *Applied Optics*, 23(14), 1984.

### A5. The Blood Marks

**Locations:**
- **Head/scalp:** Multiple small puncture wounds in an irregular crown pattern; a prominent "3-shaped" forehead stain
- **Wrists (not palms):** Large oval wound in the left wrist area; blood flows at two different angles along the forearms — consistent with a body alternately sagging and pushing up on a cross to breathe
- **Right side of chest:** Large elliptical wound with two distinct outflow streams — darker (blood) and clearer (serum/pleural fluid) — consistent with a postmortem lance wound into the right pleural cavity and pericardium
- **Feet:** Bloodstain at the right foot/ankle, consistent with a nail wound
- **Back:** Approximately **120 dumbbell-shaped scourge marks** in paired patterns, consistent with two individuals wielding Roman *flagra* (scourges with dumbbell-shaped lead weights, *plumbatae*)

**Why the blood is still reddish:** Normally blood on linen darkens to brown-black over centuries. STURP members **John Heller** (hematologist) and **Alan Adler** (chemist) identified the persistent redness as consistent with an abnormally high **bilirubin** content. In severe trauma and circulatory shock, massive hemolysis occurs; the liver converts the released hemoglobin into bilirubin (yellow-orange). Bilirubin mixed with blood degradation products maintains a reddish hue that resists normal darkening. Using spectroscopic and chemical tests — porphyrin conversion, protein tests, albumin and bile-pigment tests — Heller and Adler confirmed the presence of blood.

**Citation:** Heller, J.H. & Adler, A.D. (1980). "Blood on the Shroud of Turin." *Applied Spectroscopy*, 35(1).

**Serum halos under UV:** Under long-wave ultraviolet fluorescence, many blood marks show **fluorescent halos** surrounding the dark central stain — consistent with serum (albumin, immunoglobulins, bilirubin) from a blood clot that retracted, as occurs in a living or recently dead body. Blood marks showed no cracking or flaking typical of dried pigment; protease digestion altered their spectroscopic signature, confirming protein content.

**Important challenge:** Borrini, M. & Garlaschelli, L. (2018), *Journal of Forensic Sciences*, used blood-pattern analysis to argue that blood-flow angles on the wrists and forearms are not anatomically consistent with crucifixion posture (see Phase 7 for full evaluation).

### A6. The 1532 Chambéry Fire Damage

On the night of **3–4 December 1532**, a fire broke out in the sacristy of the **Sainte-Chapelle at Chambéry Castle**, Savoy. The silver-gilt reliquary housing the Shroud began to melt (>900°C); molten silver penetrated the folded cloth. At the time the Shroud was folded **48 layers deep** (folded lengthwise in half, then accordion-folded). The molten silver burned through the stacked layers at four points, leaving — when unfolded — **four symmetrical sets of holes and scorch marks** running in two diagonal lines down both sides of the full cloth length. The damage fell on the margins; the central image area was largely spared.

**Two sets of water stains are visible:**
- Smaller, more regular stains near the fire damage: water used to extinguish the 1532 fire.
- Older, larger, irregular stains (studied by Guerreschi & Salcito): predate 1532; proposed to have formed when the Shroud was folded and stored upright in an earthenware jar, with condensation wicking through the layers — consistent with ancient storage practices.

**1534 repairs:** Poor Clare nuns at Chambéry sewed 14 large and 8 smaller triangular patches over the holes, and attached the full Holland cloth backing. These remained until Flury-Lemberg's 2002 restoration.

## Part B: Historical Provenance

### B1. Geoffrey de Charny and Lirey, c.1354–1357 — First Undisputed Record

The documented history begins with **Geoffroi de Charny I** (c.1300–1356): a prominent French knight, royal counselor to King John II of France, and bearer of the *oriflamme* (the royal battle standard). Around 1353 he founded a **collegiate church** at his tiny fief of **Lirey**, near Troyes in Champagne. The Shroud was placed there and publicly displayed, probably beginning **c.1355–1357**, attracting pilgrims.

Critically, **de Charny left no record of how he obtained the Shroud**, despite being a well-documented figure. He died at the **Battle of Poitiers, 19 September 1356**, without disclosing the cloth's provenance. His widow **Jeanne de Vergy** and son **Geoffroi II de Charny** presumably knew but never revealed it.

**Physical evidence of the Lirey exhibitions:**
- A **lead pilgrim badge** (Musée de Cluny, Paris; found in the Seine, 19th century, dated to late 14th century) depicts two clerics holding out a full-length double body-image cloth with a herringbone weave, flanked by the coats of arms of the **Charny** and **Vergy** families.
- In 2009, metal detectorists found a **casting mold** at **Machy** (adjacent to Lirey) for a related badge with the arms in a different arrangement — corresponding to exhibitions initiated **c.1357 by Jeanne de Vergy** (de Charny's widow), making it the earliest physical evidence corroborating the Lirey display.

---

### B2. Bishop Pierre d'Arcis's 1389 Memorandum

When exhibitions resumed under **Geoffroi II de Charny** in 1389, **Pierre d'Arcis**, Bishop of Troyes (in whose diocese Lirey fell), dispatched a Latin memorandum to **Pope Clement VII** at Avignon. Key claims:

1. The Shroud was "**cunningly painted**" (*subtiliter depicta*) — "upon which by a clever sleight of hand was depicted the twofold image of one man."
2. His predecessor **Bishop Henri de Poitiers** (~1354–1370) had already investigated and found "**the truth being attested by the artist who had painted it**" — but d'Arcis did not name the artist, and no surviving record of Henri de Poitiers's investigation has been found.
3. The exhibitions were held "for the sake of gain" (*causa lucri*) and fraudulent miracles were claimed.

**Document status:** The memorandum survives as an **anonymous, unsigned, undated, unsealed rough draft** in Latin at the **Bibliothèque Nationale de Paris**. Translated into English by Rev. Herbert Thurston in 1903. Its draft status means it may never have been sent in this exact form.

---

### B3. Pope Clement VII's Response and Compromise

Clement VII issued **four papal bulls** between 1389 and 1390:

- **28 July 1389:** Granted indulgences to visitors of Lirey (effectively endorsing exhibitions).
- **6 January 1390:** Required exhibitors to declare publicly at each showing that the cloth was "*not the true Shroud of Our Lord Jesus Christ, but a painting or panel*," and ordered d'Arcis to be silent under pain of excommunication.
- **30 May / 1 June 1390:** Reversed course again — granted wide indulgences to visitors without requiring the disclaimer.

Net result: a **pragmatic compromise**. Clement sided with the de Charny family, silenced d'Arcis, neither declared the cloth authentic nor condemned it as a forgery. The exhibitions continued.

---

### B4. Transfer to the House of Savoy, 1453

By the mid-15th century the Shroud had passed to **Marguerite de Charny** (granddaughter of Geoffroi II), childless and twice-widowed. The canons of Lirey church pressed for its return, claiming legal ownership.

On **22 March 1453**, at Geneva, Marguerite formally transferred the Shroud to **Duke Louis I of Savoy** in exchange for the **Castle of Varambon** and revenues of the **Miribel estate** near Lyon — an explicit property transaction. The **canons of Lirey** protested vigorously; Marguerite was excommunicated in **1452** (for refusing to return the cloth) and again in **1457** (for having transferred it). She died in 1460, still excommunicated.

The House of Savoy kept the Shroud primarily at **Chambéry**, in the Sainte-Chapelle built to house it.

---

### B5. The Move to Turin, 1578

In **1563** the Savoy capital moved from Chambéry to **Turin**. In 1578, **Cardinal Carlo Borromeo** (Archbishop of Milan, later canonized in 1610) had vowed to walk on foot across the Alps to Chambéry in thanksgiving for Milan's deliverance from plague. Duke **Emmanuel Philibert of Savoy**, wishing to spare the elderly cardinal the Alpine crossing, had the Shroud brought from Chambéry to Turin instead.

The Shroud arrived in Turin in **September 1578**. On **10 October** there was a private showing for Borromeo; on **12 October** a public procession before an estimated crowd of **40,000**. It has remained in Turin ever since.

---

### B6. 1898: Secondo Pia's Photographic Discovery

In 1898 King **Umberto I of Savoy** authorized a public exhibition (25 May – 2 June 1898) and permitted photography. **Secondo Pia** (1855–1941), a lawyer and amateur photographer from Asti, made successful exposures on large glass plates on the evening of **28 May 1898**.

Developing the plates, Pia expected to see on his negative the usual inverted, darkened image. Instead he saw a **natural, well-modeled positive portrait** — light falling anatomically, features clearly readable. The Shroud image was a **photographic negative**: the cloth had all along been encoding a tonal inversion that only photography, invented in the 19th century, could reveal. Pia later wrote he nearly dropped the plate. The discovery launched modern scientific sindonology and reignited controversy (some contemporaries suspected manipulation; **Giuseppe Enrie** re-photographed the Shroud in 1931 with identical results, vindicating Pia).

### B7. The Ian Wilson "Mandylion Hypothesis"

**Source:** Ian Wilson, *The Shroud of Turin*, Doubleday, 1978.

**The claim:** The **Image of Edessa** (the *Mandylion*) — a famous cloth-imprint of Christ's face venerated in **Edessa** (modern Şanlıurfa, Turkey) from at least the 6th century AD — was the Shroud of Turin folded so only the face was visible. Folded in four (doubled twice), it became a "tetradiplon" (a word in the 6th-century *Acts of Thaddaeus* describing the Edessa cloth) mounted face-outward in a frame, hiding the body image. The proposed chain: Edessa → **Constantinople** (transferred 15 August 944 under Emperor Romanus I Lecapenus) → looted in the **Fourth Crusade sack of Constantinople, 1204** → via the **Knights Templar** → to **Geoffroi de Charny I** at Lirey by the 1350s.

**Evidence supporting the hypothesis:**

- **Gregory Referendarius's 944 sermon** (rediscovered 1997 in the Vatican Apostolic Archive by Wilson and Gino Zaninotto): the Archdeacon of Hagia Sophia, preaching on the cloth's arrival in Constantinople, described the image as showing **more than the face** — including bloodstains and a reference to the side wound — implying a full-length body image, not just a face.
- **Fold marks:** Analysis confirmed the Shroud bears fold marks consistent with the **tetradiplon configuration** (folded in eighths, face centered in the resulting rectangle) from an extended period.
- **Robert de Clari, 1203:** The Crusade chronicler described seeing a *sydoine* at the **Blachernae Church in Constantinople**, displayed every Friday, on which "*one could see the figure of Our Lord*."
- **The Templar name link:** Barbara Frale (Vatican archivist) and Wilson proposed the Templar **Geoffroi de Charney** (burned 1314) was a relative of Shroud-owner Geoffroi de Charny I.

**Evidence undermining the hypothesis:**

- **Separate relic entries:** Byzantine relic lists after 944 treat the *Mandylion* and the *Sindon* (burial shroud) as **two distinct relics**, both present in Constantinople simultaneously.
- **Terminological problem:** The Mandylion is consistently called a *napkin* or *facial cloth* in Byzantine sources — a burial shroud (*sindon*) would not be called a *mandylion*.
- **Iconographic mismatch:** All surviving Mandylion copies depict Christ with **eyes open and alive**; the Shroud face shows Christ eyes closed and dead.
- **Averil Cameron's objection** (Oxford Byzantinist): the Mandylion tradition most plausibly originated in Edessa in the 5th century for political/theological reasons; descriptions are too divergent from the Shroud for identification to be credible.
- **No genealogical proof** linking Templar Geoffroi de Charney to Shroud-owner Geoffroi de Charny I despite similar names.
- **Robert de Clari's ambiguity:** He was a secondary witness and noted that no one knew what happened to the cloth after the 1204 sack.

**Status:** Widely discussed, not demonstrated. The most elaborate attempt to bridge the missing centuries; rests on circumstantial rather than documentary evidence.

---

### B8. The "Missing Centuries" Problem

**The gap:** There is no securely documented record of the specific cloth now in Turin before **c.1354–1357 at Lirey** — and if the Wilson/Mandylion hypothesis is rejected, no record before the 14th century at all.

**The three leading explanatory frameworks:**

1. **The Constantinople → Templar → de Charny chain** (Wilson/Frale): as above. Plausible but unproven; rejected by most historians as insufficiently evidenced.

2. **The Louis IX / Sainte-Chapelle route** (Daniel Scavone): relics documented in the Sainte-Chapelle (built 1248 by Louis IX to house the Crown of Thorns and passion relics) overlap with those listed at Lirey. The Shroud may have passed from Constantinople to Louis IX via the Latin Empire diplomatic channel and been kept quietly in royal custody before passing to de Charny. Entirely undocumented.

3. **Medieval manufacture:** If the 1988 C14 date (1260–1390 AD) is accepted and the reweave hypothesis rejected, there is no gap — the cloth was simply manufactured in the 13th–14th century, possibly as a devotional object or deliberate forgery. On this view, de Charny or someone close to him commissioned or obtained it.

**Consensus view:** "The surviving source references are fragmentary and inaccurate, which makes a reconstruction of the history of the Shroud of Turin before the mid-14th century not fully possible; the proposals put forward by scholars are only more or less plausible hypotheses." (Digital Sindonological Lexicon)

### B9. The Pray Codex (Hungary, c.1192–1195)

The **Pray Codex** (Codex Pray) is a collection of ecclesiastical manuscripts compiled at the **Boldva monastery**, northern Hungary, between **1192 and 1195**. Held in the National Széchényi Library, Budapest. Named after Jesuit historian **György Pray**, who identified it in 1770. It contains the oldest known Hungarian-language text.

A two-panel miniature depicts the **Entombment of Christ** (upper) and the **Marys at the Tomb** (lower). Proponents identify five specific parallels with the Turin Shroud:

1. **Nude body** — Christ completely unclothed, unusual in medieval art (normally shows a loincloth), matching the Shroud's nude figure.
2. **Crossed hands over pelvis** — right over left, fingers elongated, same posture as the Shroud.
3. **No thumbs visible** — four fingers but no thumbs, matching the Shroud (possibly due to median-nerve flexion from wrist nail wounds).
4. **Herringbone weave pattern** — the burial cloth shows a prominent zigzag/herringbone pattern, not a common artistic motif, matching the Shroud's unusual 3:1 twill.
5. **L-shaped cluster of burn holes** — in the lower scene the cloth on the sarcophagus shows small circular marks in an **L-shaped arrangement** that matches exactly the location, spacing, and grouping of a specific set of **pre-1532 burn holes** on the actual Shroud (the "poker holes").

**Why it matters:** The Pray Codex is reliably dated to **1192–1195** — nearly two centuries before the radiocarbon date of 1260–1390 AD assigned to the Shroud. If the artist had seen the actual Shroud (or a faithful copy), it would constitute physical evidence of the Shroud's existence before the medieval C14 date. The five specific parallels — especially the pre-existing burn holes and herringbone weave — are regarded by authenticity proponents as too specific to be coincidental.

**Sceptical counter-arguments:**
- The zigzag pattern may be a conventional decorative border or generic textile motif.
- The L-shaped marks could be decorative roundels common in manuscript illumination.
- Similar "poker hole" patterns appear in other contemporary manuscripts with no Shroud connection.
- No other manuscript depicts the shroud-body combination so closely, which could mean it derives from a now-lost artistic tradition rather than the Turin Shroud itself.

**Scholarly status:** Suggestive but not conclusive. The parallels could result from shared artistic tradition rather than direct acquaintance with the Shroud. (Casabianca et al., 2021, *philarchive.org*)

### B10. Nicole Oresme's *Problemata* — Oldest Written Challenge (2025 Discovery)

**Publication:** Nicolas Sarzeaud, "A New Document on the Appearance of the Shroud of Turin from Nicole Oresme: Fighting False Relics and False Rumours in the Fourteenth Century," *Journal of Medieval History*, Vol. 51, No. 5 (published online **28 August 2025**; DOI: 10.1080/03044181.2025.2546884).

**Who was Nicole Oresme?** (c.1320–1382) Norman philosopher, mathematician, economist, Bishop of Lisieux, tutor to the future King Charles V of France — one of the most intellectually distinguished scholars of 14th-century Europe. His death in 1382 predates d'Arcis's memorandum (1389) by seven years.

**What the *Problemata* says:** In a treatise on *mirabilia* (wondrous phenomena), composed between approximately **1355 and 1382**, Oresme cites the Shroud as a **"patent example"** of clerical fraud to elicit offerings: "*There is manifest experience of that in a church in Champagne, where it was said that there was the shroud of the Lord Jesus Christ.*"

**"A church in Champagne"** almost certainly refers to Lirey — the only church publicly displaying a cloth claiming to be Christ's shroud in that era and region.

**Significance:**
- Predates d'Arcis's 1389 memorandum (previously the oldest written rejection) by **at least 7 years**, potentially by up to **34 years** (if dated to c.1355, coinciding with the first Lirey exhibitions).
- Comes from one of the most respected intellectuals of 14th-century France — lending it greater weight than a locally aggrieved bishop.
- Oresme's phrasing implies the Shroud's fraudulent character was already so well-known in learned circles he could cite it as a self-evident, "manifest" example without elaboration.

**Caveats:** Oresme does not explain *how* the fraud was perpetrated, does not name an artist, and does not describe the cloth in detail. His is the characterization of a skeptic making a general point about the manipulation of popular piety, not a forensic investigation.
