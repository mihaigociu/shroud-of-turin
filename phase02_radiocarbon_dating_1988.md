# Phase 2 — The 1988 Radiocarbon Dating: Original Study and Systematic Challenges

## Part A: The 1988 Study — Damon et al. 1989

**Full citation:** Damon, P.E., Donahue, D.J., Gore, B.H., Hatheway, A.L., Jull, A.J.T., Linick, T.W., Sercel, P.J., Toolin, L.J., Bronk, C.R., Hall, E.T., Hedges, R.E.M., Housley, R., Law, I.A., Perry, C., Bonani, G., Trumbore, S., Woelfli, W., Ambers, J.C., Bowman, S.G.E., Leese, M.N., and Tite, M.S. (1989). "Radiocarbon dating of the Shroud of Turin." *Nature*, 337(6208), 611–615.

---

### A1. Background: The 1986 Turin Workshop Protocol

In September–October 1986, a **Turin Workshop** convened seven radiocarbon laboratories — five AMS (Accelerator Mass Spectrometry) and two small gas-counter — and developed a formal recommended protocol for dating the Shroud. The original protocol called for:

- Seven laboratories participating
- Two complementary dating techniques (AMS and gas-counter proportional counting)
- Samples to be taken from **multiple locations** on the cloth
- Control samples of known age distributed blind

In **October 1987**, the Archbishop of Turin (Cardinal Anastasio Ballestrero), acting on instructions from the **Holy See** (which owns the Shroud), reduced the laboratory count to three AMS facilities only: the University of Arizona, the University of Oxford, and ETH Zurich. The four laboratories not selected — including Rochester — were notified by letter. The director of the Rochester Laboratory wrote a seven-point letter to *Nature* criticizing the protocol reduction:

> "The involvement of seven laboratories has been reduced to three. This eliminates the possibility of detecting a mistake made in the measurements by one or more of the three laboratories."

In **April 1988**, the final protocol was published by **Michael Tite** (then Scientific Director, Research Laboratory, British Museum). Key changes from the 1986 protocol:

- Three AMS laboratories only
- Each laboratory to receive **one sample weighing ~40 mg**, from **a single location** on the cloth
- Two control samples clearly distinguishable from the Shroud sample
- The British Museum invited to provide oversight, certify sample distribution, and conduct independent statistical analysis

The shift from multiple sampling locations to a single location was the most consequential departure from the original protocol. It concentrated the entire dating result on one ~10 × 70 mm strip of cloth.

---

### A2. Sampling Day: 21 April 1988

The sampling took place in **Turin Cathedral** at the start of the day's proceedings. Those present:

- Cardinal Anastasio Ballestrero (Pontifical Custodian of the Shroud)
- Luigi Gonella (scientific adviser to the Cardinal; Turin Polytechnic)
- Michael Tite (British Museum)
- Representatives of the three laboratories
- Franco Testore (textile expert, Turin Polytechnic — performed weighing)
- **Giovanni Riggi di Numana** (made the actual cut)
- Four priests, photographers, and a camera operator

**Location:** The strip was cut from the **lower left-hand corner** of the Shroud, **just above** the area from which Gilbert Raes of Ghent had received a sample in 1973. This corner became known as the "Raes corner" or "C14 corner." The location was chosen for several reasons: it had already been minimally disturbed by the 1973 sampling, it was away from the central body image, and it was not in one of the charred or patched areas from the 1532 fire.

**The cut strip** measured approximately **81 mm × 21 mm** and weighed ~**300 mg**. As a precautionary reserve, approximately half was set aside, sealed, and placed in Vatican custody. The remainder — **81 mm × 16 mm, ~150 mg** — was divided into sub-samples.

**Division into sub-samples:** Riggi cut a sliver from one edge, divided it, then further divided to create three pieces for the three laboratories. In a separate room (the Sala Capitolare), with the video camera no longer recording, Cardinal Ballestrero and Michael Tite placed the three sub-samples in **sealed metal canisters** (the contents of which were certified by Tite on behalf of the British Museum). Control samples from three artefacts of known age — an Egyptian linen cloth (about 3,000 years old), a Peruvian mummy cloth (approximately 650 years old), and a French medieval tapestry — were also prepared and distributed blind to the laboratories.

Sub-sample weights as distributed:
- Arizona: **~39.6 mg** (slightly smaller than the others; they were later given an additional 14.1 mg "Riserva" piece)
- Oxford: **~52 mg**
- Zurich: **~52.8 mg**

---

### A3. Pre-Treatment (Cleaning) Protocols

Each laboratory used its own independently chosen cleaning procedure before converting linen to graphite for AMS measurement. Three different protocols were employed:

**Arizona (University of Arizona, NSF-AMS Facility, Tucson):**
- Sub-samples 1 and 2: standard **Acid-Alkali-Acid (AAA)** treatment (dilute HCl, NaOH solution, HCl again)
- Sub-samples 3 and 4: experimental protocol — rinsed with **HCl** and **commercial detergents**, followed by **Soxhlet extraction in ethanol** (a continuous extraction method that removes hydrophobic organic contaminants)

**Zurich (Institut für Mittelenergiephysik, ETH):**
- All sub-samples treated by standard **Acid-Alkali-Acid (AAA)** procedure

**Oxford (Research Laboratory for Archaeology and the History of Art):**
- Sub-samples pre-treated in **petroleum ether** (to remove lipid/organic wax contaminants) before AAA treatment
- Two sub-samples additionally **bleached in sodium hypochlorite** (to oxidize residual organic matter)

The variation in pre-treatment protocols was intentional — designed to test whether different cleaning regimes produced consistent results. The fact that the protocols differed would later become relevant to the Casabianca et al. (2019) statistical heterogeneity finding.

---

### A5. Calibration Curve and Final Date Calculation

The three laboratory means were combined using a **weighted average**: overall mean of **690 ± 14 BP** (the *Nature* paper quotes the combined result before and after the t₅ distribution correction).

**Calibration procedure:** The calendar-age range was derived from the **high-precision calibration curve of Stuiver and Pearson** (1986), based on **dendrochronological dating** (tree rings). The University of Washington Quaternary Isotope Laboratory's **CALIB program revision 2.0** was used. Specifically, Method A — the "intercept method" — was applied: the error in the calibration curve itself was first incorporated into the radiocarbon measurement error, widening the limits slightly, before reading off calendar-age ranges from the curve.

Because the calibration curve is not monotonically decreasing between about AD 1250 and 1400 (it has a "plateau" and minor wiggles), the probability distribution of the calibrated dates produced **two separate sub-ranges**:
- **1262–1312 AD**
- **1353–1384 AD**

Combining these gives the reported range of **1260–1390 AD at 95% confidence** (rounded to the decade). The paper states: "These results therefore provide conclusive evidence that the linen of the Shroud of Turin is mediaeval."

---

### A6. The Role of the British Museum

Michael Tite (British Museum Research Laboratory) served as the **independent overseer** of the sampling and sample distribution. His specific functions were:

1. Certifying and sealing the containers in the Sala Capitolare
2. Receiving the raw data from each laboratory
3. Conducting an independent statistical analysis to verify agreement between labs
4. Co-authoring the final *Nature* publication (Tite is the 20th listed author of Damon et al.)

The British Museum retained the official archive of **raw data** from all three laboratories. These data — not released publicly in 1989 — would later become the subject of Casabianca et al.'s legal request, which resulted in their release in 2017.

Tite's role was purely supervisory and statistical; he had no influence over how each laboratory measured their samples. His presence gave the study institutional credibility and provided a chain of custody for the raw data.

## Part B: The Statistical Heterogeneity Argument

**Full citation:** Casabianca, T., Marinelli, E., Pernagallo, G., and Torrisi, B. (2019). "Radiocarbon Dating of the Turin Shroud: New Evidence from Raw Data." *Archaeometry*, 61(5), 1223–1231. DOI: 10.1111/arcm.12467.

**Authors:** Tristan Casabianca (researcher in epistemology and sindonology, Aix-Marseille University); Emanuela Marinelli (Italian sindonologist); Giuseppe Pernagallo (statistician, University of Catania); Benedetto Torrisi (economist/statistician, University of Catania).

---

### B1. How the Raw Data Were Obtained

Damon et al. (1989) published only **combined laboratory means** and their standard errors. The full individual sub-sample measurements — all 12 data points from the three laboratories — were not included in the *Nature* paper and were retained by the British Museum. In **2017**, following a **legal request (Freedom of Information)**, the British Museum released all raw data to Casabianca et al. This was the first public release of the individual sub-sample measurements since 1988.

Importantly, Casabianca et al. also obtained raw data from the Oxford laboratory via a separate channel, giving them 12 data points (8 from Arizona, not all of which were published in Damon et al., plus Oxford and Zurich means and sub-sample values).

---

### B2. Key Statistical Findings

Casabianca et al. applied several formal statistical tests to the raw data:

**1. Test for inter-laboratory homogeneity:**
- Unweighted ANOVA: **p = 0.0400** (significant at 5%)
- Weighted ANOVA: **p = 0.0043** (significant at 1%)
- Modified weights ANOVA: **p = 0.0497** (significant at 5%)

All three tests reject the null hypothesis that the three laboratories measured the same underlying age. The laboratories produced statistically different results, even though they were all supposed to have measured the same cloth strip.

**2. Ward & Wilson χ² test:** The standard test used in radiocarbon dating to check that multiple measurements of the same material agree before averaging them — the test that Damon et al. themselves applied. Casabianca et al. note that the published Damon et al. analysis also fails this test when the full raw data are examined, a fact not fully transparent in the *Nature* paper.

**3. Monotone spatial variation (the gradient finding):**
Riani, Atkinson and colleagues (2004, *Applied Statistics*; extended in 2010, *Statistics and Computing*) had previously applied **robust regression analysis**, examining all **387,072 plausible spatial configurations** of the 12 sub-samples (since the exact position of each sub-sample within the strip is not recorded). They found that a substantial majority of plausible configurations show a **negative linear gradient of approximately −50 BP years per centimetre** as one moves along the sample strip.

In concrete terms: sub-samples from one end of the strip give dates roughly **100 radiocarbon years younger** (more recent) than sub-samples from the other end of the same 7 cm strip. This systematic spatial trend is not expected if all sub-samples contain the same carbon; it implies that the composition of the sampled strip varies continuously along its length — consistent with either a gradual contamination gradient or a material transition.

Casabianca et al. (2019) confirm and extend this finding, stating: "The statistical analyses highlight an inter-laboratory heterogeneity of the means and a monotone spatial variation of the ages of subsamples that suggest the presence of contaminants unevenly removed by the cleaning pretreatments."

---

### B3. What This Implies — and What It Does Not

The heterogeneity finding does **not** prove that the Shroud is ancient. The mean of all three laboratories still falls squarely in the medieval range. What the heterogeneity demonstrates, according to Casabianca et al., is that the 1988 dating **did not meet basic statistical requirements** for validity:

- If sub-samples from the same piece of cloth give dates that vary systematically with position by ~50 years/cm, they are **not repeated measurements of a single unknown quantity**
- The basic assumption of radiocarbon inter-laboratory dating — that sub-samples are exchangeable — is violated
- The 95% confidence interval (1260–1390 AD) depends on the legitimacy of combining these heterogeneous measurements, which is questionable

Casabianca et al.'s conclusion: "These analyses suggest that the 1988 radiocarbon dating does not match the current accuracy requirements" and that a new study is needed. Crucially, they do **not** claim to know the "true" age of the Shroud.

---

### B4. Mainstream Statistical Responses

The heterogeneity finding is **real and undisputed** among statisticians who have examined the raw data. It was noted independently by:

- **Marco Riani** (Università di Parma) and **Anthony C. Atkinson** (London School of Economics), published in *Applied Statistics* (2004) and *Statistics and Computing* (2010), well before Casabianca et al. obtained the full raw dataset. Riani and Atkinson's key observation: "The dating which comes from a piece at the top edge [of the uncut sample] is very different from the date which comes from a piece taken from the bottom edge." They applied the **forward search regression method** to identify the spatial gradient.

The mainstream response is not to deny the heterogeneity but to **dispute its interpretation**. Two positions are taken by non-sindonologically-aligned statisticians:

1. **The heterogeneity is real but insufficient to shift the date out of the medieval range.** Even if the sub-samples are not perfectly exchangeable, the weighted mean of all measurements still falls firmly in the medieval period (690 ± 14 BP ≈ AD 1260). The observed gradient shifts individual sub-samples by ~100 radiocarbon years across the 7 cm strip — far too small to reach a first-century date.

2. **The design was not optimally randomized, and Riani & Atkinson note this as a lesson.** In a 2020 paper (published in *Entropy*), they explicitly state: "The difficulties in interpretation of the results of the age of the Shroud show forcibly the difficulties that can be caused by inadequate experimental design. Laboratory scientists are trained, perhaps subconsciously, to trust that they control all sources of variation. Statisticians, on the other hand, are aware of the possibility of unexpected sources of heterogeneity — here the 'lurking variable' of location — which have to be guarded against by suitable randomization."

The mainstream consensus is: the heterogeneity is a **methodological imperfection** that warrants a new study, but it does not by itself invalidate the medieval conclusion. A 2024 systematic review (Casabianca, T., "Systematic Evaluation of Recent Research on the Shroud of Turin," *Theology and Science*, 23(1), December 2024) found the evidence against the 1988 dating to be insufficient to overturn it, but recommended extracting new samples from multiple locations.

## Part C: The Rogers 2005 Vanillin Finding

**Full citation:** Rogers, R.N. (2005). "Studies on the radiocarbon sample from the Shroud of Turin." *Thermochimica Acta*, 425(1–2), 189–194. DOI: 10.1016/j.tca.2004.09.029.

**Author:** Raymond N. Rogers (1927–2005), physical chemist at Los Alamos National Laboratory, member of STURP (1978), initially sceptical of the Benford-Marino hypothesis.

---

### C1. The Threads Rogers Analyzed

Rogers' key empirical claim rests on a set of threads whose provenance is contested:

On **12 December 2003**, Rogers received from Luigi Gonella (former scientific adviser to Cardinal Ballestrero) what Gonella claimed were two tiny fragments — one warp thread, one weft thread — that had been **excised from the radiocarbon sample before it was distributed to the labs**. Rogers also retained threads he had personally sampled from STURP's 1978 sticky-tape sampling of the main Shroud body.

**Chain of custody problem:** Gonella was not an authorized custodian of Shroud materials. The Holy See and the custodians of the Shroud were not informed that Gonella had retained fragments. There is no independent verification that these threads were genuinely from the 1988 C14 sample area rather than another part of the Shroud or another cloth entirely. Critics (including Nickell and others) argue this provenance uncertainty undermines the entire study.

---

### C2. The Vanillin Finding

**Background:** Vanillin (4-hydroxy-3-methoxybenzaldehyde) is a component of **lignin**, the structural polymer found in the cell walls of plant fibers. In linen, lignin is present in the growth nodes of flax fibers. Under normal conditions, vanillin undergoes thermally driven degradation reactions over time; it is consumed progressively and eventually disappears entirely from aged linen.

Rogers used a **standard spot test** (Feigl's reagent and the Gibbs reaction with phloroglucinol/HCl), both qualitative color-change tests, to detect vanillin in lignin.

**Results reported by Rogers:**
- Main Shroud body threads (STURP 1978 samples): **negative for vanillin** (no detectable lignin vanillin)
- Dead Sea Scrolls linen (known to be ~2,000 years old): **negative for vanillin**
- Other very old linens: **negative for vanillin**
- Raes/C14 corner threads (Gonella samples): **positive for vanillin** (retained residual vanillin)

Rogers concluded: the corner threads contain vanillin consistent with cloth of **medieval age**, while the main body threads have lost all vanillin, consistent with cloth of **much greater age**.

**Kinetic calculation:** Rogers used activation energy data for vanillin degradation kinetics to estimate the age at which a linen cloth stored at average room temperature would lose detectable vanillin. He concluded the main Shroud is "between **1,300 and 3,000 years old**" — a range of 1,700 years, which he acknowledged as imprecise. His estimate was that "even allowing for errors in the measurements and assumptions about storage conditions, the cloth is unlikely to be as young as 840 years."

---

### C3. The Coating Chemistry Finding

Rogers also reported chemical differences between the corner threads and main body threads:

**Corner threads:**
- Yellow-brown coating on the fiber surface
- Chemical tests suggesting **plant gum** (specifically tentatively identified as **gum Arabic**, based on furfural evolution on pyrolysis, water solubility, yellow color with iodine, and ready hydrolysis)
- The gum contained **madder root dye** (identified as alizarin and purpurin by fluorescence and other microchemical tests)
- **Aluminium mordant** used to fix the dye (detected by SEM as high aluminium concentration; also consistent with alum mordanting)
- Rogers interpreted the madder/alum combination as a medieval textile dye applied to the repair thread to match its color to the aged appearance of the main Shroud

**Main body threads:**
- No plant gum coating
- No madder dye
- No elevated aluminium

---

### C4. Pyrolysis-Mass Spectrometry

Rogers ran **pyrolysis-mass spectrometry (pyrolysis-MS)** on both thread types, in which fibers are flash-pyrolyzed and the resulting volatile breakdown products characterized by mass spectrometry.

Rogers reported spectral differences between corner threads and main body threads, particularly in the **low-mass-number volatile products** (early thermal evolution of furfural and related degradation products). He interpreted these differences as evidence of fundamentally different cellulose chemistry — specifically, that the corner threads had undergone different aging chemistry, consistent with different age and different initial composition.

**Rogers's overall conclusion:** "The radiocarbon sample contains both a gum/dye/mordant coating and cotton fibers. The main part of the shroud does not contain these materials." He concluded that the 1988 C14 sample was "not part of the original cloth of the Shroud of Turin, and the radiocarbon date was thus not valid for determining the true age of the shroud."

---

### C5. Specific Criticisms of the Rogers 2005 Paper

Several significant problems have been raised:

**1. Chain of custody and sample provenance:**
The Gonella threads were obtained informally from a person not authorized to retain Shroud material. Joe Nickell (*Skeptical Inquirer*, 2005) pointed out that Rogers "relied on two little threads allegedly left over from the sampling" with no official chain of custody. The threads cannot be definitively proven to be from the C14 sample strip, let alone from the specific zone tested.

**2. Vanillin test is qualitative, not quantitative:**
Rogers used colorimetric spot tests, which provide a yes/no (presence/absence) result, not a precise concentration. The vanillin degradation kinetics calculation (which produced the 1,300–3,000 year range) requires quantitative concentration data that the method cannot provide. Multiple critics noted that a 1,700-year uncertainty range is nearly useless for chronometric purposes.

**3. The Shroud was subjected to the 1532 fire:**
Vanillin is thermally labile — it decomposes rapidly at elevated temperatures. The 1532 fire at Chambéry subjected much of the cloth to temperatures sufficient to melt silver (>960°C in the reliquary). Even if the corner was not directly scorched, the entire cloth was exposed to significant heat stress. Loss of vanillin from the main Shroud could reflect **fire damage** rather than great age. Rogers acknowledged storage temperature matters but used a simple average temperature assumption.

**4. Cotton found elsewhere on the Shroud:**
The cotton fibers Rogers cited as evidence of a medieval repair in the corner were also reported by **Walter McCrone** (who examined STURP 1978 sticky-tape samples) and noted by Raes (1973) as potentially attributable to cross-contamination from the loom. Multiple investigators have found cotton elsewhere on the Shroud, making cotton presence in the corner non-diagnostic of a repair.

**5. Madder dye and gum found elsewhere on the Shroud:**
Both the madder and gum-type materials have been found on other parts of the Shroud. McCrone's STURP samples included madder pigment observations from image areas. If these materials are distributed across the cloth, their presence in the corner does not exclusively point to a localized medieval repair.

**6. Rogers died in March 2005** — the same month the paper was published, which means he could not respond to criticisms. His co-workers who allegedly participated in the vanillin test (including Stanley T. Kosiewicz) were not listed as co-authors, raising further questions about the study's verification.

---

### C6. The Bella, Garlaschelli & Samperi 2015 Rebuttal

**Full citation:** Bella, M., Garlaschelli, L., and Samperi, R. (2015). "There is no mass spectrometry evidence that the C14 sample from the Shroud of Turin comes from a 'medieval invisible mending'." *Thermochimica Acta*, 617, 169–171. DOI: 10.1016/j.tca.2015.08.033.

Published in **the same journal** as Rogers (2005), nine years later. The authors are: Marco Bella (University of Messina), **Luigi Garlaschelli** (University of Pavia, known sceptic), and Roberto Samperi (University of Messina).

**Method:** Bella et al. conducted a close re-examination of the **pyrolysis-MS spectra** Rogers published in his 2005 paper — the specific data Rogers cited as the most objective chemical evidence for a compositional difference between corner and main-body threads.

**Finding:** The spectral differences Rogers identified between the two thread types were "just due to the presence of a **contaminant with a long aliphatic chain**" — that is, a hydrocarbon wax or oil coating on one of the sample types. Removing the signal attributable to this contaminant from the spectra, Bella et al. found:

> "Except for the presence of the contaminant, the two pyrolysis-mass spectra look alike rather than different."

**Conclusion:** "No diagnostic peak in the pyrolysis mass spectra indicates a significant difference in the two samples, besides hydrocarbon-derived contamination. Therefore, none of the presented data supports the conclusion by Rogers."

In other words: the pyrolysis-MS data Rogers took as the strongest chemical evidence of material difference is entirely explained by contamination of one sample — the very thing the pre-treatment procedures were designed to remove. Remove the contaminant signal and the two threads look chemically identical.

---

### C7. The Latendresse 2015 Counter-Rebuttal

**Full citation:** Latendresse, M. (2015/2016). "Comments on the mass spectrometry analysis of a sample of the Shroud of Turin by Bella et al." *Thermochimica Acta*, 624, 55–58. DOI: 10.1016/j.tca.2015.11.014.

Mario Latendresse (a pro-authenticity researcher at SRI International) disputed Bella et al.'s technical identification of the "contaminant":

- He argued that Bella et al.'s identification of the long-chain aliphatic signal as a "contaminant" was incorrect — that Bella et al. misinterpreted the spectral peaks
- He claimed the spectral differences Rogers identified are **not fully attributable** to the aliphatic contaminant and that some real chemical differences between the two thread types remain after accounting for any contaminant signal
- His conclusion: Bella et al.'s "main conclusion is unconfirmed"

The technical debate over spectral peak attribution remains unresolved in the peer-reviewed literature. Neither Bella et al. nor Latendresse conducted new pyrolysis-MS experiments on newly sampled threads — both were reanalyses of Rogers's published spectra. Independent replication with properly provenanced threads has not been conducted.

## Part D: The Freer-Waters & Jull Studies

Two related studies, ten years apart, directly examined textile fragments retained by the University of Arizona.

### D1. Freer-Waters & Jull 2010

**Full citation:** Freer-Waters, R.A. and Jull, A.J.T. (2010). "Investigating a dated piece of the Shroud of Turin." *Radiocarbon*, 52(4), 1521–1527. DOI: 10.1017/S0033822200056277.

In December 2010, Timothy Jull (director of the Arizona AMS lab and one of the original 1988 team, and then editor of the journal *Radiocarbon*) co-authored this study with Rachel Freer-Waters. The study examined a **remnant fragment** of the sample originally used at Arizona in 1988 — a piece never consumed in the AMS measurement.

**Methods:** Low-magnification stereomicroscopy; high-magnification transmitted and polarized light microscopy; epifluorescence microscopy.

**Findings:**
- The fragment is consistent with the main Shroud cloth — same herringbone 3:1 twill structure, same Z-twist spin
- **No evidence of dyeing, coatings, or applied treatments**
- **Only low levels of contamination** — specifically, "a few cotton fibers"
- No evidence the sample "was dyed, treated, or otherwise manipulated"
- The cotton fibers present were assessed as minor surface contamination, not evidence of a woven-in repair thread

**Conclusion:** "We find no evidence to contradict the idea that the sample studied was taken from the main part of the shroud."

---

### D2. Freer-Waters & Jull 2026

**Full citation:** Freer-Waters, R.A. and Jull, A.J.T. (2026). "Analysis of textile fragments from the 1988 radiocarbon samples of the Turin Shroud." *npj Heritage Science* (Nature Publishing Group). DOI: 10.1038/s40494-026-02530-7.

This more recent study — published in *npj Heritage Science*, a Nature Portfolio journal — examined **two specific fragments** (designated A1A and A1B) that had been retained by the University of Arizona since the 1988 dating.

**Methods:** Digital microscopy (Keyence VHX-7000), fiber documentation, weave structure analysis; comparison to control textiles distributed to the laboratories in 1988.

**Findings:**
- Both A1A and A1B fragments are **consistent with the main body of the Shroud** in weave structure and fiber characteristics
- **No evidence of rewoven material** in either fragment
- **No evidence of contamination** that would affect the radiocarbon date
- The fragments' textile characteristics match the control samples distributed for the 1988 dating exercise

**Significance:** This 2026 study is the most technically rigorous and highest-profile textile analysis of the actual Arizona C14 fragments. Published in a Nature Portfolio journal (as opposed to the pro-authenticity shroud.com circuit), it represents the most direct and credentialed refutation of the Benford-Marino/Rogers hypothesis from textile evidence.

## Part E: The Invisible Reweave Hypothesis

**Principal paper:** Marino, J.G. and Benford, M.S. (2002). "Evidence for the Skewing of the C-14 Dating of the Shroud of Turin Due to Repairs." Presented at the Ohio Shroud Conference, 2002. Available online at shroud.com/pdfs/marben.pdf.

**Authors:** Joseph G. Marino (Benedictine monk and shroud researcher); M. Sue Benford (medical geneticist). The hypothesis was first presented at the **Sindone 2000 World Congress** in Orvieto, Italy (August 2000).

---

### E1. The Core Claim

Marino and Benford proposed that the corner from which the 1988 radiocarbon sample was taken had been **invisibly rewoven** at some point after the cloth's original manufacture — likely in the **16th century**, by skilled textile workers in the employ of the House of Savoy (who had owned the Shroud since 1453). The repair was needed to mend damage — possibly the 1532 fire damage, or earlier wear at a corner that may have been handled for display.

The proposed repair technique is **"invisible mending"** (also called French weaving or French reweaving): a tedious thread-by-thread replacement of damaged threads with new threads, interwoven so precisely that the repair is invisible from the front face of the cloth. Done by master weavers, such repairs can be undetectable without cutting and examining threads.

**The quantitative claim:** If approximately **60% of the threads** in the dated sample were replacement (16th-century) threads (~AD 1530, i.e., ~420 BP) and ~40% were original (1st-century) threads (~0 AD, i.e., ~1950 BP), the weighted mixture would produce a blended C14 date of approximately **AD 1200–1260** — close to the 1988 result of 1260–1390 AD.

The Beta Analytic calculation: 60% × 450 BP + 40% × 1875 BP = 270 + 750 = **1020 BP** ≈ **AD 1210** — within the 1988 calibrated range.

---

### E2. Textile Evidence Cited by Benford and Marino

Marino and Benford cited several lines of evidence:

1. **Blind weave-examination:** They sent photographs of the Raes corner to three textile experts without identifying the cloth. They claimed all three noted **weave pattern misalignment** consistent with a repair patch rather than original cloth.

2. **Cotton fibers:** Gilbert Raes (1973) had found cotton fibers in the corner — specifically, he noted that **one part** of his sample contained cotton (Gossypium herbaceum) while the part on the other side of a seam did not. Marino and Benford argued the cotton indicated medieval repair thread (the original Shroud having been woven in the ancient Near East on a linen-only loom; medieval European repair thread being cotton-linen blend).

3. **Color anomaly:** The Raes/C14 corner appears **slightly darker** in color and different in texture from adjacent cloth areas, visible in high-resolution photographs.

4. **Historical precedent:** The House of Savoy employed skilled weavers; the practice of invisible mending on valuable tapestries and cloths was documented in 15th–16th century Europe.

---

### E3. What Textile Experts Said

**Mechthild Flury-Lemberg** (the Swiss textile conservator who led the 2002 restoration, and one of the world's most experienced experts in ancient textiles):

- Flatly rejected the invisible-reweave theory
- Stated that an invisible reweave of this type would be **"technically impossible"** to execute without leaving traces
- Crucially: when the 2002 restoration removed the Holland backing cloth (in place since 1534), the **reverse face** of the Shroud was examined for the first time in 468 years. Flury-Lemberg examined it closely: "I have absolutely not seen any sign of added textile." A medieval invisible reweave visible through the backing cloth from the reverse would show as a different thread pattern, thread splices, or texture changes — none were found.

**Franco Testore** (textile expert, Turin Polytechnic; present at the 1988 sampling):

- Examined the corner on sampling day and found no evidence of reweaving or thread splicing

**Gabriel Vial** (Technical General Secretary of CIETA, the International Centre for the Study of Ancient Textiles; one of the foremost authorities on historic European textiles):

- Present at the 1988 sampling
- Found no evidence of reweaving at the sampling site
- His detailed textile analysis of the Shroud (published in *Sindon*) documented the entire cloth as consistent with a single original weave, with only the minor side-seam as a secondary addition

**No independent textile expert with recognized credentials has published peer-reviewed evidence supporting the reweave hypothesis.** All expert testimony from non-partisan textile specialists — including those present during the sampling — rejects it.

## Part F: The Garza-Valdes Bacterial Bioplastic Coating Hypothesis

**Origin:** Leoncio Garza-Valdes, a microbiologist and pediatrician at the University of Texas Health Science Center (San Antonio), first reported his findings in presentations beginning in 1993, formalized in his book *The DNA of God?* (1996; Doubleday).

**The claim:** Garza-Valdes claimed to have discovered a **bioplastic coating** on Shroud fibers — a transparent, continuous coating produced by colonies of bacteria and fungi (specifically *Leobacillus rubrus* — a genus he named, not previously known to the scientific community). He claimed:

1. Shroud linen fibers are encased in a transparent polymer produced by these organisms
2. The coating is similar to a "bioplastic" he had first discovered on Mayan artifacts
3. The organisms are Gram-positive bacteria forming a film that resists normal cleaning procedures (including AAA pre-treatment)
4. This organic carbon coating, being younger than the cloth itself, would incorporate modern (post-1950) or relatively recent carbon into the AMS measurement, pulling the date forward
5. He claimed the coating was thick enough that it could explain the entire 1988 medieval date as a contamination artifact

---

### F1. Scientific Assessment and Rejection

The bioplastic hypothesis was assessed by multiple independent scientists and rejected on both theoretical and empirical grounds:

**Quantitative mass argument (Rodger Sparks and others):**
To shift a true first-century date (AD 30, ~1920 BP) to the 1988 result (~690 BP), a modern organic coating would need to constitute a **very large fraction of the total carbon in the sample**. Calculations show that to shift the date by approximately 1,300 years, the contaminating organic carbon would have to constitute roughly **60–70% of the total carbon** in the sample — meaning the coating would have to be roughly **double the mass of the linen fibers themselves**.

Sparks: "An error of thirteen centuries stemming from bacterial contamination in the Middle Ages would have required a layer approximately doubling the sample weight."

AMS measurement is based on carbon isotope ratios; adding 60–70% by mass of modern carbon is equivalent to adding a thick visible crust, not a thin transparent film.

**Failed detection:** When STURP members and other researchers examined Shroud fiber cross-sections microscopically, no continuous bioplastic coating was found. **Pyrolysis-mass spectrometry** of Shroud fibers at Los Alamos (by Rogers and colleagues) failed to detect any bioplastic polymer. **Laser-microprobe Raman spectroscopy** on fiber cross-sections also failed to detect any bioplastic polymer.

**Timothy Jull** (University of Arizona, 1988 dating team): "In my opinion, our sample of the shroud was very clean, and there was no evidence of any coating."

**R. Ervin Taylor** (University of California Riverside): "At the present time, the 'bioplastic theory' has many of the characteristics of cold fusion."

**The pre-treatment problem:** Even if a bioplastic coating existed, the AAA and Soxhlet pre-treatment procedures used by the three labs in 1988 are specifically designed to dissolve and remove organic surface coatings. A bioplastic robust enough to survive AAA pre-treatment would be chemically unusual and easily detectable.

**Credibility issues:** Garza-Valdes's *Leobacillus rubrus* was never published in a peer-reviewed microbiology journal, never independently isolated, and the species name was not submitted to the standard nomenclature databases. "The only people who have ever seen these bacteria are Drs. Mattingly and Garza-Valdes," said Jull.

**Consensus assessment:** The bioplastic hypothesis is considered scientifically untenable on quantitative grounds alone, regardless of whether a coating exists.

## Part G: The 1532 Fire Contamination Argument

**The claim:** The 1532 fire at Chambéry subjected the Shroud to extreme heat and subsequent water dousing. The argument proposes that combustion gases (primarily carbon monoxide, CO, and carbon dioxide, CO₂) produced by the fire reacted with the cellulose in the linen, incorporating **"younger" carbon** from the fire's combustion products into the cloth's molecular structure, thus pulling the radiocarbon date forward.

---

### G1. The Kouznetsov Experiment (1996) and Its Rejection

The most detailed version of the fire contamination argument came from a Russian team:

**Kouznetsov, D.A., Ivanov, A.A., and Veletsky, P.R. (1996).** "Effects of fires and biofractionation of carbon isotopes on results of radiocarbon dating of old textiles: The Shroud of Turin." *Journal of Archaeological Science*, 23(1), 109–121.

Kouznetsov et al. claimed experimental evidence that CO and CO₂ produced in combustion can react with hydroxyl groups (-OH) in cellulose molecules via **carboxylation**, incorporating combustion carbon. They argued the presence of silver (from the reliquary), water (from fire-fighting), and heat would have catalyzed this reaction.

**Rebuttal: Jull, Donahue, and Damon (1996):**
Published in the same issue of the journal, the Arizona AMS team attempted to **replicate Kouznetsov's experiments** exactly and could not reproduce the claimed carbon incorporation. They concluded: "The proposed carbon-enriching heat treatments are not capable of producing the claimed changes in the measured radiocarbon age of the linen."

**Christopher Ramsey** (Oxford, head of radiocarbon dating): "Carbon monoxide does not undergo significant reactions with linen which could result in an incorporation of a significant number of CO molecules into the cellulose structure."

Testing by Ramsey and others with **very high concentrations of CO** produced **no significant reaction detectable by AMS**.

**Quantitative problem:** Even accepting Kouznetsov's scenario at face value, to shift the date by ~1,300 years requires that **over 80% of the total carbon** in the sample be fire-derived. The scorched areas of the Shroud are clearly visible as black burn marks — they were not used as the C14 sample. The C14 corner was selected precisely because it showed no visible fire damage. Even in the worst-case scenario of subtle smoke penetration into apparently undamaged cloth, the quantity of deposited combustion carbon would be orders of magnitude too small.

**Kouznetsov's subsequent discrediting:** Kouznetsov was arrested in the United States in 1997 on allegations of research fraud (accepting payments to produce falsified data in unrelated studies), further undermining confidence in his shroud-related work.

## Part H: Could a New C14 Dating Be Done?

### H1. What Sampling Strategy Would Be Needed

For a new radiocarbon study to effectively address the reweave critique and the single-location criticism, the minimum requirements are:

1. **Multiple sample sites** — ideally 5–7, distributed across the full length of the Shroud, well away from the Raes corner, including:
   - At least two sites from the **central body area** (between dorsal and ventral images, i.e., the fabric-only zones)
   - One site from the **mid-cloth area** well away from any edge or patch
   - One site from the **charred cloth** adjacent to a burn hole (as a confirmatory date for the fire damage — this should post-date the main cloth date, providing an internal consistency check)
   - One site from the **Holland backing cloth** (sewn in 1534 by the Poor Clares — a known-age built-in control)
   - One site from the **side strip** (a separate narrow strip running the length of the Shroud, attached by a single-needle stitch, possibly a different period of manufacture)

2. **Blind distribution** to at least five laboratories, with randomized coding so no lab knows which sample is from which location

3. **Pre-sampling characterization** at each proposed site: before any removal, each proposed sampling zone should be examined by:
   - SEM (scanning electron microscopy) for fiber morphology and coatings
   - XRF (X-ray fluorescence) for elemental composition
   - Micro-Raman spectroscopy for organic composition
   - High-resolution photography in multiple wavelengths (UV, visible, IR)
   This characterization ensures that any anomalous site is identified and excluded before dating

4. **Full raw data publication** from all laboratories simultaneously, with standardized individual sub-sample reporting, to prevent the opacity problem of the 1988 study

5. **Modern pre-treatment protocols** — the 1988 protocols (AAA, Soxhlet, NaOCl bleaching) are still the basis of modern practice, but have been refined:
   - More rigorous **solvent extraction sequences** (hexane, methanol, acetone) to target hydrophobic and polar organic contaminants
   - Possible **plasma oxidation** pre-treatment as an additional decontamination step
   - **Stable isotope (δ¹³C, δ¹⁵N) monitoring** of each pre-treatment stage to track the removal of non-cellulosic material
   - **Combustion and graphitization quality control** — checking that each AMS preparation has consistent stable isotope ratios, as a proxy for sample purity

### H2. Modern Pre-Treatment vs. 1988

The core AAA procedure remains standard in 2026 and is well-validated. Key differences from what was used in 1988:

| Aspect | 1988 Protocol | 2026 State-of-Art |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-treatment | AAA (all labs), Soxhlet at Arizona, NaOCl bleaching at Oxford | AAA + additional solvent extraction stages; SEM characterization of residue |
| Sample quantity | 40–52 mg required for gas combustion-based AMS | Modern AMS requires only **0.5–5 mg** — far less destructive |
| Stable isotope QC | Not systematically used | δ¹³C routinely measured as contamination check |
| Pre-sampling characterization | None | SEM, XRF, Raman spectroscopy available |
| Blind distribution | Partial (control samples only) | Full randomization possible |
| Data transparency | British Museum archive (not released for 29 years) | Commitment to full raw data publication before submission |

The dramatic reduction in sample size requirement (from ~50 mg to ~2 mg for modern AMS) is particularly significant: it means that five to seven samples could be taken from a cloth the size of the Shroud with a combined total destruction of ~15–20 mg of linen — smaller than the 1988 single sample.

### H3. Church Position on New Sampling (as of April 2026)

The Shroud is owned by the **Holy See** and held under the authority of **Cardinal Roberto Repole**, Archbishop of Turin (appointed 2022).

The Church's stated position has consistently been that the Shroud is venerated as a "relic of devotion" — no formal declaration of authenticity has ever been made by the Catholic Church. Testing authorization has historically required Vatican approval.

As of April 2026:

- **No new radiocarbon dating has been authorized**
- A **scientific congress in Turin (March 2025)** convened to update the Archdiocese on new research proposals; no testing programme was announced
- For **Jubilee 2025**, the Archdiocese chose a **digital initiative** (the "Avvolti" project — a full-scale interactive digital replica deployed at Piazza Castello, Turin, April 28–May 5, 2025) rather than a physical exposition or scientific access
- Cardinal Repole has expressed caution about some recent studies (specifically the De Caro 2022 WAXS paper and the 2026 Moraes geometric simulation study), publicly questioning their methodology and claiming the custodians "cannot recognize any serious value" to some results due to chain-of-custody issues

**Practical obstacles to new sampling:**
1. Any C14 study requires **destructive sampling** — even 2 mg per site represents permanent removal of relic material
2. **Multi-site sampling** increases the logistical and symbolic weight of the sacrifice
3. The Church has shown no urgency — the 1988 result has not created pastoral crisis, and the Shroud remains a major object of veneration regardless
4. **Political sensitivity:** a new medieval result confirming 1988 could be seen as unnecessary damage to the cloth; a new first-century result would have enormous theological implications

**Scientific community recommendation:** A 2024 systematic review (Casabianca, *Theology and Science*, 23(1), 2024) and the Casabianca 2019 *Archaeometry* paper explicitly recommend a properly designed new multi-sample C14 study. The 2025 Shroud of Turin International Conference & Symposium (St. Louis, July 30–August 3, 2025; 425 attendees, 85 presentations) included presentations arguing for new testing. The $1 million **Rolfe Challenge** (offering $1M to anyone who replicates the Shroud image using medieval techniques only) remains unclaimed, but is irrelevant to the dating question.
