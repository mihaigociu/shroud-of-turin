# Phase 3 — The 1978 STURP Investigation: A Systematic Evidence-Based Study

> Part of the [Shroud of Turin Research Plan](shroud_of_turin_research_plan.md). Researched: 2026-04-22.

---

## Overview

The Shroud of Turin Research Project (STURP) was a multidisciplinary American scientific team that carried out the first systematic, direct scientific examination of the Shroud of Turin in October 1978. For 120 continuous hours (five days and nights), the team operated in a large room of the Royal Palace adjoining Turin Cathedral. They brought an unprecedented array of scientific instruments and returned with thousands of photographs, miles of spectral data, and a set of sticky-tape samples from the cloth surface. The analysis and publication process extended from 1979 through the mid-1980s, with the official summary statement issued in October 1981. No subsequent team has been granted comparable access.

---

## Part A: Organization, Personnel, and Timeline

### A1. Origins and Leadership

The origins of STURP trace to 1976, when three scientists — physicist **John P. Jackson** (U.S. Air Force Academy), thermodynamicist **Eric J. Jumper** (U.S. Air Force Academy), and radiographer **William R. Mottern** (Sandia National Laboratories) — fed a 1931 Enrie photograph of the Shroud into a **VP-8 Image Analyzer** at Sandia Scientific Laboratories on 19 February 1976. The VP-8 converted the photograph's gray-scale density into a topographic surface relief. Where any normal photograph produces a flat, distorted result, the Shroud image produced an anatomically correct three-dimensional relief of a human body. The discovery galvanized Jackson and Jumper to form an organized scientific team.

In **March 1977**, Jackson, Jumper, and Mottern convened the first organizing meeting in **Albuquerque, New Mexico**, and began recruiting specialists. Jackson became STURP's **President**; Jumper its **Vice-President**.

**Key source:** Jackson, J.P. (2011), "John P. Jackson and the Shroud of Turin Research Project," Academia.edu (biographical account). Also: Jumper, E.J. & Mottern, R.W., "Scientific Investigation of the Shroud of Turin," *Applied Optics*, Vol. 19, No. 12, 1980, pp. 1909–1912.

## Part B: Instruments and Methods

STURP brought approximately **seven tons of scientific equipment** to Turin, transported in specially constructed cases. The following methods were employed.

### B1. Photography

- **Normal visible-light photography** (front-lit, raking-light from multiple angles): Vern Miller and Barrie Schwortz, using large-format view cameras and 35 mm systems. Raking-light photography at very low angles reveals surface texture and any raised or recessed materials (a sensitive test for the presence of brush-applied paint).
- **Ultraviolet fluorescence photography:** Vern Miller and Samuel Pellicori. UV excitation at 365 nm (long-wave, Wood's lamp) and 248 nm (short-wave). Recorded on both film and electronic detectors. Published: Miller, V.D. & Pellicori, S.F., "Ultraviolet Fluorescence Photography of the Shroud of Turin," *Journal of Biological Photography*, Vol. 49, No. 3, 1981, pp. 71–85.
- **Transmitted (back-lit) photography:** Illumination from behind the cloth to reveal internal weave structure and any superimposed material.
- **Infrared photography:** Near-infrared bands to distinguish materials with different IR absorption.

### B2. Visible and Ultraviolet Reflectance Spectroscopy

Samuel Pellicori and the Gilberts (Oriel Corporation) used a **portable spectrophotometer** to measure reflectance spectra from 250 nm (near UV) to 750 nm (near IR) at multiple points: body image areas, blood areas, scorch areas (1532 fire), water stain areas, and off-image linen background. The purpose was to characterize the spectral signature of the image and compare it with known materials (pigments, dyes, blood, scorched linen).

Key published paper: Pellicori, S.F., "Spectral Properties of the Shroud of Turin," *Applied Optics*, Vol. 19, No. 12, 15 June 1980, pp. 1913–1920.

### B3. X-Ray Fluorescence Spectrometry (XRF)

Roger Morris, Larry Schwalbe, and Ron London used a portable **X-ray fluorescence spectrometer** to map the elemental composition of the cloth at multiple locations. XRF is sensitive to trace heavy elements — if paint pigments (which contain lead, manganese, iron oxides, copper, zinc, or mercury in verifiable concentrations) had been applied, XRF would detect them. Spectra were collected in image areas, blood areas, scorch areas, water stain areas, and blank linen.

Key published paper: Morris, R.A., Schwalbe, L.A. & London, J.R., "X-Ray Fluorescence Investigation of the Shroud of Turin," *X-Ray Spectrometry*, Vol. 9, No. 2, 1980, pp. 40–47.

### B4. X-Ray Radiography

Full-length X-ray radiographic passes along the cloth. William Mottern was the principal radiographer. Radiography detects any embedded material (metallic particles, heavy pigments) within the cloth structure, and reveals internal weave condition and patching.

### B5. Infrared Reflectance Spectroscopy and Thermography

Joseph Accetta (Lockheed) and Steven Baumgart (Air Force Weapons Laboratory) used infrared equipment to record:
- **Reflectance spectra** in the 3–5 μm and 8–14 μm bands.
- **Thermographic imaging** using enhanced-contrast external-illumination technique.

Key published paper: Accetta, J.S. & Baumgart, J.S., "Infrared Reflectance Spectroscopy and Thermographic Investigations of the Shroud of Turin," *Applied Optics*, Vol. 19, No. 12, 15 June 1980, pp. 1921–1929.

### B6. Direct Microscopy and Photomicrography

High-magnification examination of individual linen fibers and fibrils. This allowed assessment of the depth and distribution of image coloration at the single-fiber level (diameters ~10–20 μm). Samuel Pellicori and Mark Evans collaborated on systematic microscopy documented in: Pellicori, S.F. & Evans, M.S., "The Shroud of Turin Through the Microscope," *Archaeology*, January/February 1981, pp. 34–43.

### B7. Sticky-Tape Adhesive Sampling

Raymond Rogers and Robert Dinegar pressed specially prepared **adhesive tapes** (which leave no residue on the cloth) onto selected areas of the Shroud surface, including: body image fibers, blood areas, scorch areas, water stain areas, and background linen. The tapes lifted free-lying microparticles and the outermost surface fibrils. A total of approximately **32 tape samples** were collected and subsequently shared among STURP chemists. This was the primary means of obtaining material for off-site chemical and microscopic analysis.

### B8. VP-8 Image Analysis (Pre-Examination Discovery)

Though the VP-8 analysis predated the 1978 examination (first performed 1976; formalized in 1984 publication), it directly motivated STURP. The VP-8 Image Analyzer (manufactured by Interpretation Systems Inc.) converts pixel brightness values into mechanical vertical displacement, producing a three-dimensional topographic surface. A laser-flat photographic print fed into the VP-8 produces a distorted surface because photographs encode reflected light, not spatial distance. The Shroud image, by contrast, produced a coherent 3D body relief, indicating that image density encodes body-to-cloth distance. No photograph, painting, or rubbing has replicated this property when analyzed in the same way.

Key published paper: Jackson, J.P., Jumper, E.J. & Ercoline, W.R., "Correlation of Image Intensity on the Turin Shroud with the 3-D Structure of a Human Body Shape," *Applied Optics*, Vol. 23, No. 14, 15 July 1984, pp. 2244–2270.

### B9. Digital Image Processing

Donald Lynn and Jean Lorre of the Jet Propulsion Laboratory applied digital image enhancement algorithms developed for NASA planetary mission photography. Published: Avis, C., Lynn, D., Lorre, J., Lavoie, S., Clark, J., Armstrong, E. & Addington, J., "Image Processing of the Shroud of Turin," *IEEE 1982 Proceedings of the International Conference on Cybernetics and Society*, October 1982.

## Part C: Key Findings — The Image

### C1. The Image is Chemically a Modified Linen Surface, Not an Applied Substance

The central physical finding of STURP was that the body image is **not produced by any added material** (paint, dye, powder, stain, photographic emulsion, or acid etching in the conventional sense), but by a **chemical alteration of the outermost cellulose of the linen fibrils themselves**.

The STURP Summary of Conclusions (1981) states:

> "No pigments, paints, dyes or stains have been found on the fibrils. X-ray, fluorescence and microchemistry on the fibrils preclude the possibility of paint being used as a method for creating the image. Ultra Violet and infrared evaluation confirm these studies. Computer image enhancement and analysis by a device known as a VP-8 image analyzer show that the image has unique, three-dimensional information encoded in it."

The chemical basis of the image color was identified as the **oxidation and dehydration of cellulose** in the linen microfibrils. Oxidized, dehydrated cellulose takes on a yellow-to-brown color through conjugated carbonyl groupings that act as chromophores — the same chemistry responsible for the yellowing of aged linen and paper. The STURP 1981 summary states:

> "The scientific consensus is that the image was produced by something which resulted in oxidation, dehydration and conjugation of the polysaccharide structure of the microfibrils of the linen itself."

This finding was established by:
- Microspectrophotometry (Pellicori, 1980): the image shows monotonically increasing absorption toward the UV, consistent with conjugated carbonyl compounds, inconsistent with iron oxide or other inorganic pigments.
- Microchemical tests on fiber samples (Heller & Adler, 1981).
- XRF (Morris, Schwalbe & London, 1980): no enrichment of any heavy element in image areas compared to off-image areas.

**Key citation:** Heller, J.H. & Adler, A.D., "A Chemical Investigation of the Shroud of Turin," *Canadian Society of Forensic Science Journal*, Vol. 14, No. 3, 1981, pp. 81–103.

**Note on Rogers's later hypothesis:** Raymond Rogers initially accepted the cellulose-oxidation explanation. He later proposed (2002–2005) that the color resides not in the bulk cellulose but in a thin carbohydrate **impurity layer** (residual starch, pectin, reducing sugars) coating the individual fibrils — a legacy of retting and processing. This layer, Rogers suggested, could have undergone a **Maillard reaction** with amino compounds from a body to produce the image. Neither model has yet been definitively confirmed by independent synthesis.

### C2. Depth of Image Penetration: The Superficiality Finding

Under direct microscopy, Pellicori and Evans established that the image coloration resides exclusively on the **outermost surface fibrils** of each thread — specifically, on the **topmost ~200 nanometers** (0.2 micrometers) of the fibril surface. Colored fibrils show yellow coloration on their convex outer surface; where one fibril passes beneath another and is shielded from the cloth exterior, the underlying fibril is **uncolored**. This half-tone micro-structure — individual fibrils are either fully colored or fully uncolored; there is no gradation within a single fibril — means the image "tonality" seen macroscopically is produced by the **density of colored fibrils per unit area**, not by varying depths of color within individual fibers.

This is the "superficiality" finding. The image:
- Does not penetrate to the inside of threads.
- Does not penetrate through the cloth.
- Is confined to the very first molecular layers of the surface cellulose.
- Is fully consistent with a process that affected only the surface and could not have been an applied liquid (which would wick by capillary action into the thread interior).

**Depth estimate:** approximately 200–600 nm (broadly cited range; the lower bound of ~200 nm for the coloration itself is most precisely supported by microscopy).

**Key citation:** Pellicori, S.F. & Evans, M.S., "The Shroud of Turin Through the Microscope," *Archaeology*, January/February 1981, pp. 34–43. Also: Schwalbe, L.A. & Rogers, R.N., *Analytica Chimica Acta*, Vol. 135, 1982, pp. 3–49 (comprehensive summary).

### C3. Absence of Brushstrokes and Directionality

Raking-light photography — illuminating the cloth at an extremely acute angle to reveal surface texture — detected no raised deposits, no brush-applied material, and no evidence of the microscopic ridges and depressions that a bristle brush leaves in even the most thinly applied paint. Under high magnification, the individual colored fibrils show no preferential orientation, no directional pattern suggesting mechanical application, and no pigment particles sitting on top of the surface.

The 1981 Summary confirms:

> "There are no indications of how the image was produced. There are no brush marks."

This finding eliminates not only conventional painting but also "light contact" methods (rubbing, squeezing, or pressing) since these would produce directionality and pressure variations visible at the fibril level.

### C4. UV Fluorescence: The Image Does Not Fluoresce

Under ultraviolet illumination at 365 nm, the 1532 scorch marks from the Chambéry fire **fluoresce** (emit visible light), as do typical contact-heat scorches on linen. The body image, however, **does not fluoresce** — it is UV-dark. This is a critical diagnostic. A thermally produced contact scorch, a chemical scorch from an acid, and most organic dyes all produce characteristic UV fluorescence. The non-fluorescence of the Shroud image rules out all conventional scorch mechanisms and the majority of known chemical coloring agents.

Conversely, the blood marks **do fluoresce** under UV, specifically showing characteristic porphyrin fluorescence — consistent with blood degradation products (see Part D). The serum halos surrounding some blood marks also fluoresce (albumin/bilirubin fluorescence).

**Key citation:** Miller, V.D. & Pellicori, S.F., "Ultraviolet Fluorescence Photography of the Shroud of Turin," *Journal of Biological Photography*, Vol. 49, No. 3, 1981, pp. 71–85.

### C5. The VP-8 3D Analysis: Cloth-to-Body Distance Encoding

The 1984 paper by Jackson, Jumper & Ercoline constitutes the most mathematically detailed analysis of the 3D properties. The authors demonstrated that across the full extent of the frontal image, pixel brightness (image density) varies as a **monotonically decreasing function of the inferred distance between the cloth and the body surface** beneath it. The relationship is not merely qualitative — it can be characterized by a single mathematical transfer function that applies globally. The paper concluded:

> "The frontal image on the Shroud of Turin is shown to be consistent with a body shape covered with a naturally draping cloth in the sense that image shading can be derived from a single global mapping function of distance between these two surfaces."

This "distance-encoding" property means:
- Prominent features (tip of nose, chin, cheekbones, knuckles) are darker.
- Recessed features (eye sockets, neck, valleys between fingers) are lighter.
- The image encodes spatial information rather than reflected light.
- When fed into a VP-8 or processed with any image-to-topography algorithm, the result is an anatomically coherent human figure.

No ordinary photograph, painting, or rubbing produces this property. A photograph encodes reflected light intensity; raking light across a painted face would encode the relief of the brushwork, not the underlying body geometry. The 3D property has been independently verified by multiple researchers using different image-analysis platforms.

**Key citation:** Jackson, J.P., Jumper, E.J. & Ercoline, W.R., "Correlation of Image Intensity on the Turin Shroud with the 3-D Structure of a Human Body Shape," *Applied Optics*, Vol. 23, No. 14, 15 July 1984, pp. 2244–2270.

### C6. Image Resolution and Spatial Detail

The finest resolvable spatial detail in the Shroud image corresponds roughly to the scale of individual linen threads (~0.15 mm diameter, ~35 threads/cm). Image information at the sub-thread level (individual fibril scale, ~10–20 μm) reflects only the binary on/off coloring of individual fibrils — not continuous tonal variation within a thread. For practical imaging purposes, the spatial resolution is therefore approximately **0.3 mm or slightly finer** — comparable to a moderately high-resolution analog photograph.

The combination of high spatial resolution, 3D encoding, superficial chemical alteration, and no directional brushwork defines a set of constraints that no single known imaging or artistic process simultaneously satisfies.

## Part D: Key Findings — The Blood

### D1. Heller and Adler's Spectroscopic and Chemical Analysis

John Heller (hematologist, New England Institute) and Alan Adler (chemist, Western Connecticut State College) conducted the most exhaustive chemical analysis of materials lifted on the sticky-tape samples. Their work was published in two papers:

1. Heller, J.H. & Adler, A.D., "Blood on the Shroud of Turin," *Applied Optics*, Vol. 19, No. 16, 1980, pp. 2742–2744. *(Note: some sources cite this as Applied Spectroscopy Vol. 35 — cross-check preferred; the Applied Optics version is confirmed by the STURP papers list.)*
2. Heller, J.H. & Adler, A.D., "A Chemical Investigation of the Shroud of Turin," *Canadian Society of Forensic Science Journal*, Vol. 14, No. 3, 1981, pp. 81–103.

**Specific tests performed and their results:**

| Test | Method | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Porphyrin fluorescence | UV excitation of fibril material | **Positive** — characteristic porphyrin ring fluorescence of heme degradation products |
| Hemochromogen formation | Addition of hydrazine to dissolve non-heme iron, then pyridine reagent | **Positive** — generation of hemochromogen (ferroprotoporphyrin IX) spectrum |
| Cyanmethemoglobin | Addition of neutralized cyanide solution | **Positive** — characteristic cyanmethemoglobin absorption spectrum |
| Bilirubin detection | Microspectrophotometry; chemical tests | **Positive** — bilirubin (bile pigment) identified in blood areas; indicator of massive hemolysis from extreme trauma |
| Protein/albumin | Biuret reaction and protein stains | **Positive** — albumin (serum protein) and globulins identified |
| Protease digestion | Treatment with pronase, trypsin (proteolytic enzymes) | **Positive** — "blood" material on fibrils dissolved upon enzyme treatment, leaving a colorless fibril; iron-oxide paint does not dissolve in proteases |
| Acid dissolution | Treatment with hydrochloric acid | Blood particles **resistant** (unlike inorganic iron oxide, which dissolves cold in HCl); blood dissolves in enzymes but not in cold HCl — opposite of iron oxide |
| Birefringence / refractive index | Microscopic optical measurements | Blood particles and image-area particles have **different refractive indices and birefringences**, showing they are chemically distinct species |
| Microspectrophotometry | Visible absorption spectra of individual particles | Unmistakably identified **hemoglobin in the acid methemoglobin form** (consistent with great age and denaturation) |

Adler's decisive demonstration was the **hydrazine test**: when hydrazine was added to dissolve any free iron (iron oxide would remain as a residue), the red particles in blood areas turned the characteristic hemochromogen color, confirming he was looking at heme derivatives, not iron oxide.

Adler's reported conclusion to his colleagues: *"This, ladies and gentlemen, is not iron oxide; it is blood."*

The bilirubin finding deserves emphasis. Normally, blood on ancient linen darkens to brown-black over centuries as hemoglobin oxidizes to methemoglobin and then breaks down further. The blood on the Shroud remains reddish. Adler proposed — and the spectroscopic data support — that the blood contains abnormally high levels of **bilirubin**, produced when massive physical trauma causes extensive hemolysis (rupture of red blood cells). The liver converts the released hemoglobin to bilirubin at a rate that overwhelms normal clearance. Blood with elevated bilirubin retains a persistent reddish hue consistent with the Shroud stains' appearance.

### D2. The Serum Halo Finding Under UV

Under ultraviolet fluorescence photography (Miller & Pellicori, 1981), many blood marks on the Shroud are surrounded by **fluorescent halos** that are invisible under normal light. These halos, brighter and wider than the dark blood core, fluoresce with an emission characteristic of **albumin and bilirubin**. In living or recently deceased tissue, when blood coagulates and a clot forms and subsequently retracts (the normal physiological process of clot retraction, driven by platelet contraction), the serum (the non-cellular fluid component of blood) is expelled and spreads laterally around the clot. The resulting halo of dried serum contains albumin, globulins, and bile pigments — which fluoresce under UV.

This serum-halo morphology:
- Is characteristic of real wounds and does not occur when paint or pigment is applied.
- Is consistent with wounds that bled, clotted, and dried while in contact with the cloth.
- Provides independent UV-based corroboration of the chemical blood identification.

The serum halos were documented photographically by the STURP team during the October 1978 examination, primarily under the direction of Vern Miller and Samuel Pellicori.

### D3. Blood Before Image: Stratigraphic Evidence

One of the most forensically important STURP findings is the stratigraphic relationship between blood marks and image coloration. Under microscopy, where blood marks and image areas overlap:

- Image fibrils **under** a blood clot are **not colored** — they show no image toning.
- The blood mark has **blocked** whatever process produced the image coloration from reaching those fibers.

This implies the blood was deposited on the cloth **before** the image formed. If the image had formed first and blood was applied afterward (as would be the case if an artist painted bloodstains), the underlying fibrils should be colored. They are not.

This "blood-before-image" finding is a significant constraint on any forgery hypothesis: a medieval artist would logically add blood marks **after** drawing the body image, not before — and blood applied atop a pre-existing image would in any case not prevent the underlying fibers from being image-colored.

**Key citation:** Heller & Adler, *Canadian Society of Forensic Science Journal*, Vol. 14, No. 3, 1981, pp. 81–103; discussed in Schwalbe & Rogers, *Analytica Chimica Acta*, Vol. 135, 1982, pp. 3–49.

## Part E: Key Findings — Other Analyses

### E1. X-Ray Fluorescence: Elemental Composition Findings

Morris, Schwalbe & London (1980) mapped elemental composition across the cloth at image areas, blood areas, scorch areas, water stain areas, and background linen. Measurable elements found:

- **Calcium (Ca):** Up to ~280 μg/cm², uniformly distributed across the cloth, not concentrated in image areas. Consistent with hard water used in processing linen; also consistent with limestone dust (calcium carbonate) from a burial environment — though the latter explanation is interpretive.
- **Iron (Fe):** ~6.8–17.5 μg/cm² in image and background areas; elevated to ~58 μg/cm² in blood areas. Iron levels in image areas were **not significantly different from background linen** — i.e., there is no elemental iron enrichment that would indicate applied iron-oxide pigment. The elevated iron in blood areas is consistent with the iron in hemoglobin.
- **Strontium (Sr):** ~4 μg/cm², uniformly distributed. Consistent with linen processed with hard water.

The instrument's detection limits were established for rubidium, yttrium, zirconium, and other elements of potential forensic interest; none were found. No heavy metals characteristic of artist's pigments (lead white, lead red, vermilion/mercury sulfide in large amounts, azurite/copper, malachite, manganese, etc.) were detected above background in image areas.

This is a strong negative result: if the image were painted with red ochre (iron oxide, Fe₂O₃) as McCrone claimed, the iron concentration in image areas should be substantially higher than in blank linen. The XRF data show no such enrichment.

**Key citation:** Morris, R.A., Schwalbe, L.A. & London, J.R., "X-Ray Fluorescence Investigation of the Shroud of Turin," *X-Ray Spectrometry*, Vol. 9, No. 2, 1980, pp. 40–47.

### E2. Infrared Spectroscopy Findings

Accetta and Baumgart's infrared reflectance spectroscopy (Applied Optics, 1980) found that most features of the Shroud were spectrally similar in the 3–5 μm and 8–14 μm bands: the reflectance spectra of image areas, non-image areas, and background linen were not substantially distinguishable. The conclusion was effectively **null** in terms of identifying a unique chemical species in image areas by mid-IR spectroscopy. This null result is itself informative: known organic pigments and biological staining agents (logwood, weld, saffron, malachite green, etc.) show characteristic IR absorption bands; their absence in image areas further argues against applied colorants.

### E3. Water Stains and the Chromatographic Non-Migration Test

The Shroud shows large water stains from water thrown on the box during the 1532 fire. These stains show **chromatographic diffusion rings** at their margins — the normal pattern of water evaporating and leaving dissolved/suspended material at its boundary. Critically, the body image color within the water-stained areas **shows no migration, no blurring, no change in intensity** at the stain margins. If the image were produced by a water-soluble dye, stain, or pigment in an aqueous medium, the image would have migrated to the water stain edges and been chromatographically separated. It did not. This rules out all water-soluble image-forming agents.

### E4. Pollen Samples

Pollen sampling is associated with **Max Frei-Sulzer** (Swiss criminologist and botanist), not with STURP's core agenda. Frei took his first sticky-tape pollen samples from the Shroud in 1973 during a Vatican-authorized commission; he took a second set of **27 sticky-tape samples** during the STURP access period in October 1978 (working alongside but not formally as a STURP member). From his samples, Frei ultimately identified pollens of **58 plant species**, including a significant proportion of species specific to the Middle East, Turkey, and the eastern Mediterranean. Of particular note: *Gundelia tournefortii* (a thorny thistle that flowers in March–May in the Jerusalem region).

After Frei's death in 1983, his pollen slides were acquired in 1994 by the **Council for Study of the Shroud of Turin (CSST)** and subsequently analyzed by botanist **Avinoam Danin** (Hebrew University of Jerusalem) and palynologist **Uri Baruch**. Danin identified the co-occurrence of *Gundelia tournefortii* and *Zygophyllum dumosum* as pointing specifically to the Jerusalem–Hebron zone.

**Critical reservations:**
- Frei was later implicated in the **Hitler Diaries forgery** (1983), damaging his credibility as a forensic scientist.
- When Danin brought the pollen slides to Prof. Dr. Thomas Litt (expert palynologist) in 2001, Litt concluded that **none of the pollen grains he examined could be identified to species level** with the available material — a serious methodological blow.
- Danin himself retracted his specific botanical findings in later years, stating that the pollen evidence did not withstand rigorous re-examination.
- No independent laboratory has replicated Frei's species identifications from the original slides.
- The pollen evidence must therefore be treated as **inconclusive and methodologically compromised**.

### E5. Scorch Marks: UV Fluorescence Distinguishes Shroud Image from Heat Scorches

The 1532 scorch marks **fluoresce** under UV, as do all experimentally produced contact-heat scorches on linen. The body image does **not** fluoresce. Both the STURP UV photography and subsequent spectroscopy confirm this distinction. This means:

1. The body image was not produced by a hot object contacting the cloth.
2. The image was not produced by the same chemical mechanism as the 1532 scorches.
3. The mechanism(s) that produced the 1532 scorch vs. the body image must be chemically distinct, even though both involve linen coloration.

## Part F: The McCrone Dispute

### F1. McCrone's Analysis and Conclusions

**Walter C. McCrone** (1916–2002) was one of the world's foremost microscopists and director of the McCrone Research Institute in Chicago. He was widely respected for forensic microscopy work (he had, for instance, identified the Vinland Map as a modern forgery via microscopy of its ink). Ray Rogers loaned McCrone 32 of the sticky-tape samples after STURP returned from Turin; McCrone began analysis on **Christmas Day 1978** and reported preliminary findings at the STURP conference in Santa Barbara on **25 March 1979**.

McCrone applied **polarized light microscopy** to the tape samples and identified:

1. **Red iron oxide (hematite, Fe₂O₃)** — the principal pigment in **red ochre** — distributed across the image areas of the tape samples, in his view at concentrations consistent with painting.
2. **Vermilion (mercuric sulfide, HgS)** — a medieval red pigment — in some blood areas, leading him to conclude the "blood" was highlighted with vermilion paint.
3. A **collagen or gelatin binder** (animal glue tempera medium) associated with the pigment particles.

McCrone's conclusion: The Shroud image was produced by **a dilute red ochre paint in a collagen tempera (gelatin) medium**, applied by a 14th-century artist, with blood areas highlighted using vermilion. He published this in three papers (all in *The Microscope*, the journal of the McCrone Research Institute):

- McCrone, W.C. & Skirius, C., "Light Microscopical Study of the Turin Shroud, I," *The Microscope*, Vol. 28, 1980, pp. 105–113.
- McCrone, W.C., "Light Microscopical Study of the Turin Shroud, II," *The Microscope*, Vol. 28, 1980, pp. 115–128.
- McCrone, W.C., "Microscopical Study of the Turin Shroud, III," *The Microscope*, Vol. 29, 1981, pp. 19–38. (Added scanning electron microscopy with energy dispersive X-ray analysis, electron microprobe, and X-ray diffraction confirming hematite.)

He also published one paper in a fully peer-reviewed journal: McCrone, W.C., "The Shroud of Turin: Blood or Artist's Pigment?" *Accounts of Chemical Research*, Vol. 23, No. 3, 1990, pp. 77–83. This is McCrone's only English-language peer-reviewed publication on the Shroud.

### F2. The Response from STURP Members

Heller, Adler, Schwalbe, and Rogers mounted a systematic response. Their objections operated at multiple levels:

**1. The iron oxide is real but not the image chromophore.**

Both sides agreed that iron oxide particles are present on the Shroud. The dispute was about whether this iron oxide accounts for the image color. The critical test is quantitative: Schwalbe and Rogers's XRF data show that iron concentrations in image areas (~7–18 μg/cm²) are **not significantly elevated** compared to background linen. If the image were painted with iron oxide, image areas should show a large iron excess above background. They do not. McCrone was examining individual particles under a microscope; STURP's XRF measured bulk elemental concentrations over larger areas. The XRF is the more appropriate tool for answering the question of whether iron oxide is present **in image-forming quantities**.

**2. The iron oxide's origin is non-painting.**

Schwalbe and Rogers proposed that the iron oxide found by McCrone came from two sources:
- **Retting:** Flax plants preferentially absorb iron from soils during growth; the retting (water-soaking) process of linen manufacture concentrates iron in the outer fiber layers. Ancient and medieval linens consistently show elevated iron in the outer fiber surface.
- **Contact with iron-containing materials:** STURP found evidence that painted copies of the Shroud had been laid directly on the original — a common medieval practice of "sanctifying" copies by contact. This could transfer pigment particles from painted copies onto the original cloth.
- **Oxidized blood products:** Iron from blood degradation products (hemosiderin, etc.) would be distributed unevenly across blood areas.

**3. The "blood" dissolves in proteases, not in acid — the opposite of iron oxide.**

Heller and Adler's decisive chemical test: materials in blood areas dissolved completely when treated with proteolytic enzymes (pronase, trypsin), leaving a colorless fibril. Iron oxide is completely resistant to proteases. Blood and its proteins dissolve readily. This test alone definitively distinguishes blood from iron oxide pigment in the sample material.

**4. The "vermilion" identification was contested.**

Schwalbe and Rogers found no evidence of mercury in XRF scans of blood areas at the concentrations McCrone's identification would require. McCrone's microscopy-based vermilion identification was not supported by bulk elemental analysis.

**5. McCrone's microscopy was applied incorrectly.**

The STURP critique held that McCrone was counting pigment particles in very small areas of tape samples and extrapolating — a method that could identify trace contamination as the primary image mechanism if not carefully controlled for background levels in non-image areas.

The four-member STURP response to McCrone's 1990 *Accounts of Chemical Research* paper (Adler, Jumper, Rogers, Schwalbe) was prepared as a letter to the editor of that journal but was ultimately not published in that venue; it is preserved in the STERA (Shroud of Turin Education and Research Association) archives. See: "FROM THE STERA ARCHIVES (7): Four STURP Members' Critique of McCrone's 1990 paper in Accounts of Chemical Research," Academia.edu, 2023.

### F3. McCrone's Departure from STURP

McCrone resigned from STURP in **June 1980**, after his findings were rejected at an internal STURP meeting. He and the other STURP scientists reached an irreconcilable impasse. McCrone accused his colleagues of suppressing inconvenient evidence; the others accused him of premature conclusions from insufficiently controlled microscopy. McCrone published his work primarily in *The Microscope*, his own institute's journal, of which he was editor — a peer-review arrangement that critics noted lacked independence. His 1990 *Accounts of Chemical Research* paper is the only one in an independent peer-reviewed journal in English.

McCrone's 1999 book, *Judgment Day for the Shroud of Turin* (Microscope Publications), summarizes his position. He never changed his conclusion and maintained until his death in 2002 that the Shroud was a medieval painting.

**Significance of the dispute:** The McCrone controversy is partly a dispute about method (microscopy of individual particles vs. bulk spectroscopic analysis) and partly about interpretation (how much iron oxide constitutes "paint"?). The majority of STURP scientists and subsequent independent researchers — particularly Heller and Adler's protease tests and the XRF bulk data — have not supported McCrone's paint hypothesis. No independent research team replicating STURP's chemical methods has confirmed McCrone's conclusion that iron oxide accounts for the image. However, McCrone's work has not been definitively refuted on the microscopic particle level; the debate is more precisely framed as: iron oxide particles are present in small quantities across the cloth, but they are insufficient in concentration to account for the visible image, and their distribution does not correlate with image intensity.

## Part G: STURP's Published Conclusions (1981)

### G1. The Official Summary of Conclusions

The Summary of STURP's Conclusions was written by John Heller in non-technical language and distributed at a press conference following STURP's final meeting in **October 1981** in New London, Connecticut. The full document is preserved at shroud.com/78conclu.htm. Key passages:

> "No pigments, paints, dyes or stains have been found on the fibrils. X-ray, fluorescence and microchemistry on the fibrils preclude the possibility of paint being used as a method for creating the image. Ultra Violet and infrared evaluation confirm these studies."

> "Computer image enhancement and analysis by a device known as a VP-8 image analyzer show that the image has unique, three-dimensional information encoded in it."

> "The blood stains are composed of hemoglobin and also give a positive test for serum albumin."

> "The image is an ongoing mystery and until it can be reproduced by some known means, it must remain unfalsified."

> "We can conclude for now that the Shroud image is that of a real human form of a scourged, crucified man. It is not the product of an artist."

On the image formation mechanism, the summary states:

> "The scientific consensus is that the image was produced by something which resulted in oxidation, dehydration and conjugation of the polysaccharide structure of the microfibrils of the linen itself. Some type of energy interacted with the cloth to produce the image. There are no chemical or physical methods known which can account for the totality of the image, nor can any combination of physical, chemical, biological or medical circumstances explain the image adequately."

**Note on internal dissent:** The statement "We can conclude for now that the Shroud image is that of a real human form of a scourged, crucified man" was not universally endorsed by all STURP members. Alan Adler, for instance, maintained there was no scientific evidence that the Shroud had definitively wrapped a body — the image was consistent with a body but did not prove one. The statement reflects the majority position of the group's leadership rather than a formal consensus vote.

### G3. Open Questions STURP Explicitly Left Unanswered

The 1981 Summary and subsequent papers explicitly identified the following as unresolved:

1. **The image formation mechanism**: "There are no chemical or physical methods known which can account for the totality of the image, nor can any combination of physical, chemical, biological or medical circumstances explain the image adequately." This is the central mystery STURP acknowledged without resolution.

2. **Whether a body was actually present**: The image is consistent with a body imprint, but STURP did not claim to have proven that a body was the cause — only that the image has properties consistent with body-shaped input.

3. **The age of the cloth**: STURP did not conduct radiocarbon dating (which required destructive sampling and was deferred to a subsequent authorization, eventually granted in 1988 to three independent labs).

4. **The identity of the individual**: STURP made no claims about whether the Shroud wrapped Jesus of Nazareth.

5. **The precise chemical mechanism of the image chromophore**: While STURP identified oxidized/dehydrated cellulose as responsible for the color, they could not specify the exact pathway (direct oxidation? Maillard reaction? corona discharge? radiation? vapors?).

6. **The pre-1532 history** and whether the water stain patterns provide dating information.

## Part H: Criticism of STURP

### H1. Religious and Institutional Bias

The most persistent criticism of STURP is that the project was not ideologically neutral.

- STURP's president John P. Jackson and several team leaders were active members of the **Holy Shroud Guild** — an organization whose stated purpose was to promote devotion to the Shroud. Critics, including Joe Nickell (*Inquest on the Shroud of Turin*, Prometheus Books, 1983, updated 1998) and Steven Schafersman (*Skeptical Inquirer*, Vol. VI, No. 3, Spring 1982), argued that scientists who were simultaneously advocates for the Shroud's authenticity could not conduct an unbiased investigation.
- The conditions under which non-STURP, skeptical scientists could examine the samples were extremely restricted. After McCrone's departure in 1980, **no independent skeptical microscopist was given access** to the sticky-tape samples. Schafersman wrote that STURP "kept the lid on evidence that conflicts with their pro-authenticity beliefs by disparaging or ignoring McCrone's analytical results" (*Skeptical Inquirer*, 1982).

**STURP's counter-argument:** Team members pointed to the range of religious affiliations (Heller's account lists atheists, agnostics, Jews, and various Christian denominations, including "six agnostics, two Mormons, three Jews, four Catholics and all the rest Protestants"). Most were, by their own accounts, not devotees of the Shroud before joining the project. The data and methods, they argued, were independently published and open to replication.

### H2. Absence of Key Expertise

Multiple critics noted that STURP lacked:
- **Art historians** and medievalists who might have identified the image technique from an art-historical perspective.
- **Textile experts and archaeologists** specialized in ancient and medieval cloth.
- **Forensic chemists with expertise in medieval pigments and binding media** (outside McCrone, who departed).

This gap meant STURP was optimized for physical science (spectroscopy, X-ray analysis, photography) and chemistry, but not for the historical and art-historical context needed to evaluate a purportedly medieval artifact. Joe Nickell and others contended that no competent art historian was consulted about medieval painting techniques.

### H3. Methodological Criticisms

- **Sample restriction:** Only sticky-tape surface lifts were taken; no cross-sections of fibers were prepared, which would have definitively resolved whether image color extends partway into the fiber or is strictly superficial. The ~200 nm depth estimate comes from indirect optical evidence, not from physical sectioning.
- **No control samples:** STURP did not systematically compare tape samples from the Shroud to tape samples from definitively medieval painted linens (known painted fabrics of the 13th–14th century) under the same methods. Without matched controls, some of McCrone's particle-counting arguments are harder to definitively refute.
- **Chain of custody of tape samples:** After Rogers provided samples to McCrone, the distribution and custody of tape samples became a matter of dispute. Some samples may have been studied non-blindly by scientists aware of their provenance and expected content.
- **Conflict between bulk XRF and individual particle microscopy:** These are genuinely complementary, not redundant, methods. McCrone's microscopy provides information at the single-particle level that XRF cannot, and vice versa. The failure to fully reconcile these levels of analysis is a methodological gap rather than a settled dispute.

### H4. STURP II Proposals Rejected

STURP submitted detailed proposals for a follow-up investigation in **1984, 1987, and 1988**, including a 177-page 1984 proposal addressed to Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger as Prefect of the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith, with 26 proposed analytical techniques. All proposals were rejected by Church authorities without substantive reply. The 1988 radiocarbon dating was authorized instead, over STURP's objections about sample location and methodology. The refusal to allow STURP II has prevented independent replication and extended analysis.

### H5. Replication Attempts by Independent Teams

The primary challenge to STURP's most distinctive findings:

**McCrone/paint hypothesis:** Luigi Garlaschelli (University of Pavia) published a life-size reproduction of the Shroud image in 2009 using iron oxide (ochre) rubbed over a cloth-draped volunteer, then artificially aged with heat: Garlaschelli, L., "Life-Size Reproduction of the Shroud of Turin and its Image," *Journal of Imaging Science and Technology*, Vol. 54, No. 4, 2009. Garlaschelli claimed the result visually resembled the Shroud, including some 3D properties when analyzed by VP-8. STURP proponents (and independent analysis by Thibault Heimburger) responded that Garlaschelli's reproduction:
- Required hand-correction of the image ("dabbled on by hand").
- Did not replicate the superficiality — his image penetrated fibers, not just fiber surfaces.
- Did not replicate the half-tone fibril structure.
- Did not replicate the UV non-fluorescence.
- Required prior knowledge of what to produce (circular reasoning for a "medieval forgery").

**Blood chemistry:** The blood findings of Heller and Adler have not been systematically and independently replicated, primarily because no new sticky-tape samples have been made available to non-STURP researchers since 1978. The blood evidence therefore rests on STURP-affiliated scientists. This is a genuine limitation, noted by critics.

**3D properties:** The VP-8/3D encoding finding has been independently confirmed by multiple researchers using digital image analysis — including researchers with no STURP affiliation — who reproduced the 3D relief from Enrie (1931) and Miller (1978) photographs of the Shroud. The 3D encoding is therefore the most robustly replicated STURP finding.

**Image superficiality:** Di Lazzaro, P. et al. (ENEA, Italy), "Superficial and Subsurface Modifications of Linen by Short Laser Pulses," *Applied Optics*, Vol. 51, No. 36, 2012, demonstrated that deep ultraviolet (UV) laser pulses can produce superficial coloration of linen fibrils spectrally similar to the Shroud image. This independently validates the superficiality constraint established by STURP. However, Di Lazzaro et al. make no claim that a medieval laser existed; the experiment was conducted to characterize the physical constraints on image formation.

### I2. What STURP Could Not Determine

- **How the image was formed.** This remains scientifically open in 2026.
- **Whether the Shroud is the burial cloth of Jesus of Nazareth.** STURP explicitly declined to make this claim.
- **The date of the cloth.** Left to subsequent radiocarbon dating.
- **Whether the image-forming process required a body or could be produced artificially** given sufficient knowledge of its constraints.

### I3. The Strongest Ongoing Criticisms

1. **Access monopoly:** No independent, skeptical team has been allowed to examine the sticky-tape samples since McCrone in 1979.
2. **Bias:** Key organizers belonged to the Holy Shroud Guild.
3. **McCrone's findings have not been fully refuted at the particle level**, even if they have been successfully challenged at the bulk analytical level.
4. **No STURP II:** The 1984–1988 proposals for follow-up work were rejected; the science is frozen at the 1978 state.
5. **Publication venue for McCrone:** Most STURP papers were peer-reviewed; most McCrone papers were in his own journal — but this cuts both ways, raising questions about the peer review of both sides.
