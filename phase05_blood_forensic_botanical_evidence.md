## Part A: Blood Evidence — Chemistry

### A1. Heller & Adler: The Full Chemical Case for Blood

The definitive chemical case for the presence of whole blood on the Shroud was built by two STURP members working on 22 sticky-tape slides sampled directly from the cloth during the 1978 investigation:

- **John H. Heller** — hematologist and biophysicist, New England Institute.
- **Alan D. Adler** — porphyrin chemist, Western Connecticut State University.

**Primary publications:**
- Heller, J.H. & Adler, A.D. (1980). "Blood on the Shroud of Turin." *Applied Optics*, 19(16), 2742–2744.
- Heller, J.H. & Adler, A.D. (1981). "A Chemical Investigation of the Shroud of Turin." *Canadian Society of Forensic Science Journal*, 14(3), 81–103.

#### The Complete Battery of Tests and What Each Showed

**1. Microspectrophotometric transmission spectroscopy**
The most critical technique. Using a microspectrophotometer, Heller and Adler measured the absorption spectrum of single "blood particles" (coated fibers, shards, and globs) at wavelengths characteristic of hemoglobin and its breakdown products. They identified the Soret absorption band at approximately 410 nm — the hallmark of the porphyrin ring common to all heme compounds. They noted the material existed in the form of **acid methemoglobin**, the degraded form consistent with aged, denatured hemoglobin. This finding was consistent with very old blood, not with iron oxide pigment (which shows no Soret band).

**2. Hemochromogen test (pyridine hemochromogen / Takayama test)**
A specific chemical spot test for heme. When treated with pyridine and a reducing agent, heme compounds form the characteristic pink hemochromogen crystal (Fe²⁺-pyridine complex). The blood areas produced positive hemochromogen results; image-only areas (no blood) did not. This distinguished heme from iron oxide, which cannot form hemochromogen.

**3. Cyanomethemoglobin test (Drabkin's test)**
Treatment with potassium cyanide and ferricyanide converts methemoglobin to the stable cyanomethemoglobin complex, which has a characteristic absorption at 540 nm. Blood areas gave positive results; non-blood areas did not.

**4. Protease digestion**
Samples were treated with protease enzymes (protein-dissolving enzymes). The blood particles — fiber coatings, shards, and globs — were **completely dissolved** by protease without visible residue. This confirmed two things simultaneously: (a) the material is proteinaceous (consistent with blood, not mineral pigment), and (b) the iron associated with the particles was not iron oxide (Fe₂O₃, which would leave a visible rust residue after protein dissolution). McCrone's iron oxide hypothesis predicted an insoluble red residue; none was found.

**5. Fluorescamine test (fluorescent amine detection)**
Fluorescamine reacts with primary amines — the amino groups on amino acids and peptides released from protein degradation. The blood areas fluoresced strongly, confirming the presence of amino acids and protein fragments consistent with aged blood. This is incompatible with mineral pigments.

**6. Porphyrin fluorescence (chemical generation)**
Porphyrins fluoresce with a characteristic red emission under UV excitation. After chemical conversion of the heme to free porphyrin, the blood areas produced the expected porphyrin fluorescence. The image-only areas did not.

**7. Detection of bile pigments (bilirubin and biliverdin)**
Using both spectroscopic and chemical methods, Heller and Adler detected **bile pigments** — breakdown products of hemoglobin — in the blood areas. This finding was central to explaining the anomalous reddish color (see §A2 below).

**8. Albumin detection**
The UV-fluorescent **serum halos** surrounding many blood marks were tested for albumin. Results were positive, confirming the presence of blood serum (which is rich in albumin) alongside the whole blood clots. The halos represent clot retraction — the serum expelled as a fibrin clot tightens — a process that requires actual coagulating blood from a living or recently living organism. Paint cannot produce serum halos.

**9. Energy-Dispersive X-ray (EDX) / X-ray fluorescence analysis**
EDX confirmed **iron** was present in both blood areas and non-blood areas of the cloth. Crucially, the distribution was not confined to image or blood areas; iron was found throughout the cloth at roughly uniform levels, consistent with the natural iron content of linen (from water used during retting and washing) rather than added pigment. Walter McCrone had argued the iron was deliberately applied red ochre pigment; Heller and Adler's EDX data, and the protease results, contradicted this, as deliberate pigment would be concentrated in the image and blood areas.

**10. Hydrazine test**
A chemical test to distinguish iron in blood (in the form of heme) from iron in ferric oxide (red ochre). Hydrazine reduces heme compounds but does not react the same way with iron oxides. Blood areas responded in the manner expected for heme-bound iron. This provided yet another independent confirmation that the iron in blood areas was not red ochre.

**11. Protein precipitation tests (biuret, ninhydrin)**
General protein detection tests confirmed proteinaceous material in blood areas.

**12. Ultraviolet fluorescence imaging (not a chemical test, but part of their protocol)**
Under UV illumination, blood marks showed fluorescent halos — the serum rings — while the image itself did **not** fluoresce. This non-fluorescence of the image ruled out proteinaceous paint media (gelatin, egg tempera), which would fluoresce under UV. It also allowed STURP to establish the critical **temporal sequence**: serum halos surround the blood marks, meaning the blood was laid down **before** the image was formed. A forger painting blood over a pre-existing image would not produce underfluorescent serum halos beneath the image.

**Overall conclusion of Heller & Adler (1981):** "We conclude that the bloodstains are composed of whole blood of extraordinary high bilirubin content and that this accounts for their red color ... The chemical evidence ... clearly identifies the substance as blood, but we do not have sufficient data to determine the species."

**Note on iron oxide:** Heller and Adler acknowledged the presence of iron oxide particles on the cloth but argued these were (a) uniformly distributed, not concentrated in blood or image areas, (b) present in comparable amounts on other ancient Mediterranean linens, and (c) the result of natural contamination during the preparation of the linen, not deliberate painting. McCrone's identification of the same particles as artist's pigment represents a fundamentally different interpretive judgment on the same data.

### A2. The Bilirubin Argument: Mechanism, Evidence, and Independent Reception

**The problem it addresses:** Blood on linen normally darkens to a brown-black color over decades as hemoglobin oxidizes and degrades. The Shroud's blood marks are still distinctly **reddish** after centuries, which is anomalous and was cited by some as evidence of artificial pigment.

**Heller and Adler's explanation:** High concentrations of **bilirubin** — a yellow-orange bile pigment — stabilize the red coloration. The mechanism:

1. Under conditions of severe physiological stress (torture, prolonged hemorrhage, circulatory shock), massive **hemolysis** occurs: red blood cells are lysed, releasing hemoglobin into the plasma.
2. The reticuloendothelial system converts the released hemoglobin to **biliverdin** (green) and then to **bilirubin** (yellow-orange).
3. Serum bilirubin levels in such a patient could rise to many times normal levels.
4. When this bilirubin-rich blood dries on cloth, the bilirubin forms complexes with the remaining hemoglobin breakdown products, producing a color-stable reddish-brown compound that resists the normal darkening pathway.

Heller and Adler measured bilirubin spectroscopically and found it at levels they described as "extraordinarily high" in the blood areas.

**Independent reception:**

The bilirubin explanation has been accepted by some but challenged experimentally. A 2018 study by **Paolo Di Lazzaro and colleagues** (ENEA, Frascati) tested the hypothesis by preparing blood samples with elevated bilirubin (from jaundiced patients) and applying them to linen. Their finding: **elevated bilirubin alone did not preserve a reddish color** under normal storage conditions. The cloth darkened as expected. Only when subjected to **continuous ultraviolet radiation** (comparable to prolonged sunlight exposure) did the elevated-bilirubin samples retain higher red intensity compared to controls.

This critique suggests the bilirubin argument is insufficient on its own, and that some combination of bilirubin content plus UV exposure, or an entirely different mechanism, may be responsible. The Di Lazzaro group published these findings in their ongoing ENEA Shroud studies (see also: Crispino, D. et al. reviewing the reddish color hypotheses in *Medcrave: Journal of Hematology & Transfusion Sciences*, 2018).

**Hematological acceptance:** The bilirubin mechanism is physiologically plausible and consistent with known traumatic hemolysis. However, no independent hematology team has published a formal replication of Heller and Adler's bilirubin quantification. The 2015 review by **Piero Savarino** (*Blood Stains of the Turin Shroud 2015: Beyond Personal Hopes and Limitations of Techniques*, PubMed PMID 27001889) noted that technical limitations and the age of the samples make reliable quantification of bilirubin extremely difficult with current methods. The bilirubin explanation remains the most cited but not fully experimentally validated mechanism for the anomalous color.

### A3. Blood Type AB — Baima Bollone's Claim, Method, and Contestation

**The claim:** The blood on the Shroud is type AB on the ABO blood group system.

**Who made it:** **Prof. Pierluigi Baima Bollone**, forensic medicine professor at the University of Turin, and collaborators. In **1981** Baima Bollone announced that the blood was of human origin, and in **December 1982** he and colleagues announced type AB classification from immunofluorescence testing of a thread sample taken in 1978 from the side wound area.

**Method:** Baima Bollone used **serological ABO typing by immunofluorescence** — applying antisera raised against A and B antigens to the sample and detecting binding by fluorescence microscopy. He also extracted threads (as distinct from STURP's surface tape-lifts). The work was reported in conference proceedings and popular publications; a fully peer-reviewed account was never published in a standard hematology or forensic science journal.

**Why this is contested:**

1. **Aged blood and false-positive AB:** The most fundamental problem is that **aged, degraded blood frequently produces false AB results** by conventional ABO serology. The A and B antigens are glycoproteins on red blood cell surfaces; as cells degrade, these molecules denature, fragment, and are released into the surrounding medium. Degraded non-specific glycoproteins can bind both anti-A and anti-B antisera, producing an apparent AB result even when the original blood type was A, B, or O. Kearse (2012, immunological review, *shroud.com*) notes this problem explicitly. Critically, AB-positive typing has been reported for ancient Egyptian mummies of verified other blood types when retested with modern methods.

2. **Bacterial contamination:** A and B antigen-like molecules are expressed on the surfaces of many bacteria (which share structural epitopes with human ABO antigens). Heavily contaminated ancient textile samples — which the Shroud is — can test "AB" due to microbial A and B epitopes rather than human red cell antigens. Kearse (*Unanticipated Issues in Serological Analysis of Blood Species*, *Science Direct*) specifically flagged this problem.

3. **No peer-reviewed publication:** Baima Bollone's typing result has never been described in sufficient methodological detail in a peer-reviewed journal to allow replication or critical evaluation. The absence of full controls (how were the antisera validated? what were the cross-reactivity profiles?) makes independent assessment impossible.

4. **Independent assessment (Kearse 2019, *IJFSC*):** Kearse's paper "Inadequacies in Serological ABO Typing of Ancient Artifacts" (International Journal of Forensic Sciences, MedWin Publishers) used the Shroud as a case study and concluded: "A confident conclusion that the blood type is AB cannot be supported by available evidence ... A sample could test positive for AB without any red blood cells even being present."

**Summary:** The AB claim is weakly supported and probably reflects methodological artifact. It should not be treated as an established fact.

### A4. Is It Human Blood? Species Evidence and Its Limits

**What the evidence shows:**

Heller and Adler (1980, 1981) conducted immunological tests for human-specific proteins but did not publish a confident human-only identification. Their 1981 paper states explicitly: "We do not have sufficient data to determine the species."

Baima Bollone (1981) announced the blood was human by immunofluorescence. However, the species-specificity of the antisera used is the critical issue.

**The cross-reactivity problem:** Kelly P. Kearse's 2012 immunological review (*Blood on the Shroud of Turin: An Immunological Review*, available at shroud.com) examined all available serological evidence for human origin and reached a cautious conclusion:

- Most serological data support **primate** origin (consistent with human).
- Heller and Adler's own data showed **cross-reactivity** of their "human-specific" antibodies with rat and cat blood, undermining the species conclusion.
- Many "human-specific" reagents available in 1978–1981 were in fact **primate-specific**, not uniquely human.
- Current highly specific monoclonal antibodies were not applied to the Shroud blood.

**Kearse's conclusion:** "A confident conclusion that the blood is indisputably human is somewhat confounded by the cross-reactivity of many 'human-specific' reagents with blood [from other primates]. The empirical evidence supports the idea that the blood is primate, but strictly human origin cannot be confirmed with existing data."

**DNA evidence (see §A5):** More recent metagenomics data (Barcaccia et al. 2026 preprint) identified human mitochondrial DNA haplogroups from the dust samples, establishing that human DNA is present on the cloth — but this could derive from the many handlers over centuries and does not pinpoint a specific individual or era.

**Practical significance:** The species question is important for the forgery hypothesis. If it were shown definitively to be non-human blood (animal, for example), it would strongly support forgery. Current evidence does not allow a firm human-only conclusion using the methods available to STURP, but it is consistent with human blood.

### A5. The 2026 University of Padua Metagenomics Preprint

**Full citation:** Barcaccia, G. et al. (2026, preprint). "DNA Traces on the Shroud of Turin: Metagenomics of the 1978 Official Sample Collection." *bioRxiv*. DOI: 10.64898/2026.03.19.712852. Posted March 19 / v2 available. Not yet peer-reviewed.

**Principal investigator:** Prof. Gianni Barcaccia, plant genetics and genomics, University of Padova. This group had published a predecessor study in 2015 (*Scientific Reports*) identifying pollen, plant, and human mtDNA on the Shroud from the same 1978 vacuuming samples.

**What the study did:** Metagenomic analysis (shotgun sequencing) of dust particles vacuumed from the Shroud during the 1978 STURP investigation. The 2026 preprint is an expanded, deeper analysis of the same sample collection.

**Key findings:**

- **Human DNA:** ~40% of the human mitochondrial DNA haplogroups present traced to lineages characteristic of the **Indian subcontinent** (haplogroups M39, M56, R7, R8 — confirmed in the 2015 study and extended in 2026). Additional haplogroups from Europe, the Middle East, and East Africa were also present.
- **Plant DNA:** At least 19 plant species from the Mediterranean Basin were identified, plus species from Asia, the Middle East, and the **Americas**. Post-Columbian plants detected included **maize, tomatoes, potatoes, peppers, and peanuts** — plants that were introduced to Europe from the Americas only after 1492. Other detections included bananas, almonds, walnuts, and oranges.
- **Microbial DNA:** Bacteria from human skin, salt-tolerant archaeal communities, and fungal molds consistent with long-term textile storage.

**What it does not resolve — and why:**

The presence of DNA from Indian-subcontinent haplogroups, post-Columbian plants, and organisms from across the world is **entirely explained by cumulative contamination** from centuries of handlers, pilgrims, textile workers, researchers, and storage environments. Every person who has ever touched, breathed on, or worked near the Shroud could have deposited DNA. The 1978 vacuuming captured the **biological palimpsest of the Shroud's entire custodial history**, not evidence of its origin.

Post-Columbian plant DNA in particular is a contamination fingerprint: if the Shroud were a first-century Palestinian object, there would be no mechanism by which maize or tomato DNA reached it before 1492 — unless the DNA arrived via post-15th-century handlers, which is the simplest explanation.

**Expert critiques (Scientific American, April 2026):** Independent genomicists raised the following specific methodological concerns:
1. **Preliminary matching algorithms prone to false positives** — short environmental DNA fragments can match multiple species; the identification pipeline used by Barcaccia et al. requires validation steps.
2. **No ancient DNA authentication:** The study did not demonstrate that any of the DNA is genuinely ancient (e.g., via characteristic post-mortem damage patterns such as cytosine deamination at sequence ends, which distinguishes ancient from modern DNA).
3. **Absence of provenance control:** Without knowing the taphonomic history of each DNA fragment, the geographic inferences are not scientifically supportable.
4. **Pre-print status:** The paper had not undergone peer review at time of publication. If submitted to a journal, reviewers will require additional validation.

Cardinal/Archbishop Roberto Repole of Turin publicly expressed skepticism about the study's value.

**Bottom line:** The 2026 bioRxiv preprint adds no information about where or when the Shroud was made. It documents the biologically diverse contamination profile of a heavily handled ancient textile. It cannot distinguish a 1st-century Levantine from a 14th-century European origin.

## Part B: Forensic Pathology — Wound Analysis

### B1. Crown of Thorns Wounds

**What the Shroud shows:** The dorsal and frontal images display numerous small puncture wounds on the scalp and at the hairline — predominantly at the forehead/temples, the occipital region (back of the head), and the crown. Blood flows are visible on the forehead, including the well-known "3"-shaped (reversed epsilon) clot on the forehead, which is consistent with a forehead wound where blood pooled in a skin wrinkle or the supraorbital ridge and then flowed in two directions.

**Distribution — cap vs. crown debate:** The key forensic question is whether the wounds are distributed in a narrow circlet (like an artist's stereotypical thorny "crown") or cover the entire scalp (consistent with a thorny "cap" or helmet shape). The **forensic consensus** favors a **cap or helmet** distribution. Numerous wounds are present not only at the temples and forehead but also on the vertex (top of the skull) and nape. This distribution is incompatible with a simple laurel-wreath-style circlet; it requires a roughly hemispherical cluster of intertwined thorny branches pressed down over the entire head. Forensic pathologist **Robert Bucklin** (Chief Medical Examiner, Los Angeles) documented "multiple puncture wounds covering the scalp" in his 1982 autopsy analysis (*An Autopsy on the Man of the Shroud*, shroud.com).

More recently, a 2023 PMC paper (Di Lazzaro et al., *Some experiments and remarks regarding the possible formation of blood stains on the Turin Shroud: stains attributed to the crown of thorns*, PMC10772002) performed blood-flow simulations: applying blood along a circular scalp line (circlet model) best reproduced the frontal forehead stains; however, the presence of occipital and vertex stains is difficult to reproduce without a more encompassing structure. The paper did not definitively resolve the cap vs. crown question.

**Consistency with known thorns:** The thorn of *Ziziphus spina-christi* (Christ's thorn, native to the Middle East) produces thorns up to 2–3 cm long, consistent with the depth and spacing of the scalp punctures. No formal measurement of wound depth from the Shroud itself is possible without physical examination; estimates are derived from the wound-track visible on the image.

**Note:** The d'Arcis memorandum (1389) describes a "twofold image of one man" but makes no reference to wound details, so the historical record provides no baseline for comparing the wound pattern.

### B2. Scourge Wounds — Number, Pattern, Instrument

**Count:** Most forensic analyses cite approximately **120 individual scourge marks**, though counts vary between studies (range: ~80–130), depending on the image-enhancement method used and how overlapping marks are classified.

**Pattern:** The marks appear predominantly on the back (dorsal image), but also on the legs, buttocks, and (to a lesser extent) the chest and abdomen. The distribution is bilateral and covers a large surface area. The marks appear in **paired or triple groupings**, consistent with a multi-thonged instrument.

**The dumbbell shape (Type 1 marks):** The most diagnostically important mark type consists of two small round impressions (~0.8 mm diameter each) connected by a short linear trail. This "dumbbell" or figure-8 configuration is precisely the wound pattern expected from a Roman *flagrum* (*flagellum*) tipped with **plumbatae** — dumbbell-shaped lead or bone weights at the ends of leather straps. When the two-pronged tip strikes skin, each end of the dumbbell makes a separate contusion, with the connecting skin bridge between them forming the trail mark.

**Forensic confirmation:** Barbara Faccini (2008 Columbus Conference on the Turin Shroud; published paper: *Scourge Bloodstains on the Turin Shroud: an Evidence for Different Instruments Used*, also at institutojohnhenrynewmanufv.com) performed image processing and experimental replication. Using both a flexible rod bundle and a flagrum-type instrument, she reproduced stains morphologically similar to those on the Shroud. She identified **three distinct wound types**, suggesting at least two different instruments were used — consistent with Roman practice in which multiple executioners alternated with different flagra. The flagrum identification is accepted by the majority of forensic pathologists who have examined the Shroud.

**Forensic pathologist analyses:**
- Robert Bucklin (1982): "The posterior aspect of the body shows more than one hundred separate areas of dumbbell-shaped contusions, consistent with a scourging device with multiple strands tipped with weighted ends."
- Frederick Zugibe (*The Crucifixion of Jesus*, 2nd ed., 2005): concurred with the flagrum identification and the approximate count of 120, noting the bilateral symmetric distribution implies two individuals standing to the left and right of the victim.

**One caveat:** The scourge mark pattern is one of the strongest arguments for Roman practice consistency; however, it is also the detail that a medieval forger informed about Roman methods (or having access to descriptions in Eusebius, Jerome, or other patristic writers) could have incorporated deliberately.

### B3. The Wrist Nail Wound — Barbet vs. Zugibe

This is one of the most technically contested topics in Shroud forensic medicine.

**What the Shroud shows:** An oval bloodstain at the **lower palm / wrist junction** of the left wrist is visible. The thumbs are not visible in the frontal image (a fact requiring anatomical explanation). Two divergent blood-flow tracks run up the left forearm at different angles (~55° and ~65° from the wrist), indicating two distinct arm positions during blood flow.

#### Pierre Barbet's Position

**Pierre Barbet** (1884–1961), French surgeon, Hôpital Saint-Joseph, Paris. Key work: *A Doctor at Calvary: The Passion of Our Lord Jesus Christ as Described by a Surgeon* (1950; English translation, 1955).

Barbet conducted **cadaver experiments** (described in detail in *Doctor at Calvary*): he drove nails through the palms of freshly amputated hands and found they tore through the skin between the middle and ring fingers at a tensile load of approximately **88 pounds** — far less than the body weight that would need to be supported. He then drove nails through **Destot's Space** — the anatomical space at the center of the carpal bones bounded by the capitate, semi-lunar, triquetral, and hamate bones — and found the nail passed cleanly between the bones, displacing them without fracture. He reported the nail emerged through the dorsal (back) surface of the wrist approximately 1 cm above the point of entry.

On the **missing thumbs:** Barbet proposed that when a nail passes through Destot's Space, it would sever or compress the **median nerve** (which runs close to this location), triggering a reflex contraction of the flexor pollicis longus, pulling the thumb inward toward the palm and making it invisible in a dorsal view.

Barbet's hypothesis was enormously influential and dominated Shroud medicine for decades.

#### Frederick Zugibe's Critique and Alternative

**Frederick T. Zugibe** (1928–2013), Chief Medical Examiner, Rockland County, New York; Adjunct Associate Professor of Pathology, Columbia University College of Physicians and Surgeons.

Key works:
- Zugibe, F.T. (1984). "Death by Crucifixion." *Canadian Society of Forensic Science Journal*, 17(1).
- Zugibe, F.T. (2005). *The Crucifixion of Jesus: A Forensic Inquiry* (2nd ed.). Barricade Books.
- "Pierre Barbet Revisited" — available at shroud.com.

**Zugibe's anatomical critique of Barbet, point by point:**

1. **Palm nailing is biomechanically feasible:** Zugibe argued that Barbet's calculation was flawed. The nail need not be placed in the **center** of the palm; it can be placed through the bony space between the heads of the second and third metacarpals (the "M2–M3 space"), where firm bone support exists. With the wrists flexed upward and the body weight partly supported by the sedile (a small foot support or wooden peg often used in Roman crucifixion), the tensile force on each hand is substantially less than body weight. Zugibe used **over 100 live volunteers**, restrained to a cross with leather straps in positions approximating different crucifixion postures, to study respiratory mechanics, blood chemistry, and stress patterns.

2. **The median nerve critique:** Zugibe pointed out that Barbet's nail trajectory through Destot's Space actually **passes on the ulnar side of the palmaris longus tendon** — which means the nail would **miss the median nerve entirely**. Furthermore, even if the median nerve were damaged, paralysis of the thenar muscles (which the median nerve supplies) would cause the thumb to **extend outward** (under the pull of the extensor pollicis now unopposed), not flex inward. Barbet's mechanism is anatomically backward.

3. **Zugibe's explanation for the missing thumbs:** After death and during handling, the wrists naturally flex in the palmar direction, causing the relaxed thumbs to drop below the plane of the other fingers and become invisible in a dorsal view. No nerve injury is required.

4. **Nail entry point:** Zugibe proposed the nail entered at what he called the "M2–M3 space" in the **upper palm**, with a downward and slightly ulnar trajectory, so that the tip of the nail would exit through the **lower wrist / Destot's Space area** — producing the wrist-level wound visible on the Shroud without requiring a direct wrist entry. This reconciles the wound location visible on the Shroud with the traditional theological depiction of hand nailing.

**Where consensus lies:** Most contemporary forensic pathologists who have reviewed both positions find Zugibe's anatomical critique of Barbet's median-nerve argument compelling. The question of whether the entry was in the upper palm or in the wrist proper remains unsettled. A 2014 paper in *Legal Medicine* (ScienceDirect, "How was the Turin Shroud Man Crucified?" DOI: 10.1016/j.legalmed.2014.09.009) reviewed the biomechanics and concluded the wrist-entry hypothesis remained more consistent with the wound's visible location on the Shroud, while acknowledging Zugibe's anatomical corrections to Barbet's nerve argument.

### B4. The Side Wound — Dimensions, Dual-Fluid Outflow, and Medical Interpretation

**What the Shroud shows:** A large, elongated elliptical bloodstain on the right side of the chest, approximately **4 × 1.5 cm** at the wound site itself, with a large outflow approximately **6 cm wide and 15 cm long** running downward. The stain appears in the region of the **fifth or sixth right intercostal space**, anatomically consistent with a wound between ribs 5 and 6 at the anterior axillary line.

**Dual-fluid observation:** The stain shows two visually distinct components:
- A darker, denser inner flow consistent with **whole blood**.
- A lighter, more diffuse peripheral component consistent with **serum** or a watery fluid (pleural fluid or pericardial fluid).

This separation — whole blood surrounded by a lighter fluid — is consistent with a postmortem wound into a body in which the circulatory contents have begun to separate by gravity (red cells settling below plasma/serum).

**Medical interpretation:**
- Pierre Barbet and subsequent physicians proposed the spear entered the **right pleural cavity** and possibly the **pericardial sac**. A victim dying from crucifixion would be expected to develop **hemothorax** (blood in the pleural cavity) and potentially **hydrothorax** (watery pleural effusion from respiratory distress or cardiac failure). A spear entry through the fifth or sixth intercostal space on the right side at that angle would traverse the pleural cavity and potentially reach the right atrium or right ventricle of the heart.
- The **Gospel of John (19:34)** describes "blood and water" flowing from the wound. Barbet identified the "water" as pleural fluid or pericardial fluid (hydropericardium); later physicians added serum from hemolyzed blood as another candidate.
- A 2023 blood-flow simulation experiment (PMC10772002) replicated the dual-stain pattern by sequentially applying pleural effusion and then whole blood to cloth — reproducing a serum ring surrounding a blood stain, similar to what is seen on the Shroud.

**Consistency with a Roman lancea:** The Roman *lancea* was a short throwing spear with a leaf-shaped blade approximately 5–7 cm wide at its broadest point. The wound's dimensions (~4 cm × 1.5 cm) are **smaller** than the full width of a lancea blade, but if the spear was thrust at an angle and the wound was measured on the cloth after the edges had contracted, the dimensions are plausibly consistent. Robert Bucklin (1982) described the wound as "elliptical in shape with rather well-defined edges," consistent with a sharp, single-edged implement rather than a blunt instrument.

**Postmortem chest wound literature:** The medical literature is clear that postmortem spear wounds can produce slow outflow of separated blood components. In forensic pathology, postmortem wounds to the chest of a body that has been supine produce gravitational pooling; when a body is moved, pooled fluid can flow from existing wounds. The Shroud shows a dorsal (back) blood flow from the same wound consistent with the body having been moved from vertical (on the cross) to supine (laid in the tomb), with pooled right-side fluid draining from the wound in the new body position.

### B5. The Foot Wound(s)

**What is visible:** A large bloodstain at the right ankle/heel region in the dorsal image, consistent with a wound through the foot. The blood track runs up the calf slightly, consistent with a nail entry from above (with the foot fixed to the wood) and blood pooling around the nail site.

**What it suggests about nail placement:** The Shroud image suggests nailing through the **dorsal surface** (top) of the foot or ankle, with blood flowing toward the heel and up the leg. However — and this is important — this is at variance with the only definitively confirmed archaeological case of crucifixion nailing:

- The **Jehohanan skeleton** (excavated 1968, Jerusalem): the nail pierced the **calcaneus (heel bone) laterally**, driven through the **side** of the heel bone, with the foot bones alongside the cross upright rather than flat against it.
- A **2007 Roman crucifixion skeleton** found near Venice showed a similar lateral calcaneal wound.

Neither archaeological case shows dorsal (top-of-foot) nailing. The discrepancy between the Shroud image and the archaeological record has been raised by critics (Center for Inquiry) as a challenge to Shroud authenticity, since a medieval forger might have followed conventional artistic depictions of crucifixion (nails through the tops of feet) rather than the actual Roman practice of heel nailing.

**Defenders' response:** Multiple crucifixion methods existed; Roman practice was not standardized. Some crucifixions used ropes; some used a sedile (seat peg). The two archaeological cases are too small a sample to determine Roman standard practice. Frederick Zugibe and others have argued that dorsal foot nailing through the tarsals or metatarsals is biomechanically plausible.

### B6. The Shoulder Abrasion

**What the Shroud shows:** Broad abrasion and contusion marks visible on both scapular regions (shoulder blades) in the dorsal image. The marks are described by forensic pathologists as consistent with the rubbing and pressure of carrying a heavy rough-hewn wooden beam (the *patibulum*, the horizontal crossbar that the condemned normally carried to the crucifixion site, where it was affixed to a standing upright).

Robert Bucklin (1982) noted: "There is an abrasion/contusion in the region of both scapulae which is consistent with a pressure injury from carrying a heavy rough object across the back." More recent analysis has suggested the marks may reflect not only abrasion from carrying but also **damage from falling** (lacerations consistent with having fallen forward onto rough pavement while carrying a heavy beam, with the beam driving the scapular region into the ground).

**Forensic pathologist Sava Cerić et al. (2021, *New Light on the Sufferings and the Burial of the Turin Shroud Man*)** proposed that the shoulder trauma, combined with specific patterns of bruising, was consistent with a **brachial plexus injury** (damage to the network of nerves supplying the arm) resulting from a violent fall while carrying the beam. This remains a specialized hypothesis.

---

### B7. The Face — Swelling and Blunt Trauma

**What the Shroud shows:** Image analysis and 3D reconstruction have identified the following:

- **Right cheek swelling:** The right cheek/orbital area appears elevated compared to the left, with a large soft-tissue swelling consistent with blunt trauma. The swelling appears to involve both the superior and inferior orbital regions (upper and lower cheek).
- **Nasal deviation:** The distal (cartilaginous) third of the nose is displaced slightly to the right, consistent with a cartilage injury from a blow.
- **Mandibular displacement:** Advanced image processing has suggested left displacement of the mandible (jaw), possibly indicating temporomandibular joint damage from a lateral blow to the right side of the face.

These findings have been published in peer-reviewed literature: **Bevilacqua, M. et al. (2015).** "The Turin Shroud Face: The Evidence of Maxillo-Facial Trauma." *Folia Morphologica*, 74(2), 229–236. DOI: 10.5603/FM.2015.0033 (PMID 26050809). The authors used digital image processing and 3D reconstruction and concluded that the Shroud face shows evidence of blunt-force trauma to the right maxillofacial region, consistent with a blow from a blunt instrument (a stick, rod, or fist).

The Gospel accounts describe Jesus being struck on the face during his trial before the Sanhedrin and before Pilate (Matthew 26:67–68; John 18:22–23), which proponents argue is consistent with these findings.

## Part C: Blood-Flow Directionality — Borrini & Garlaschelli 2018

### C1. The Study in Full Detail

**Full citation:** Borrini, M. & Garlaschelli, L. (2019). "A BPA Approach to the Shroud of Turin." *Journal of Forensic Sciences*, 64(1), 137–143. DOI: 10.1111/1556-4029.13867. (Published online 10 July 2018; in print 2019.)

**Authors:**
- **Matteo Borrini** — Professor of Forensic Anthropology, Liverpool John Moores University (UK).
- **Luigi Garlaschelli** — Chemist, University of Pavia; known skeptic who in 2009 produced a full-size Shroud reproduction using medieval techniques (*Journal of Imaging Science and Technology*, 2010).

**Background — BPA (Blood Pattern Analysis):** BPA uses the geometry of dried blood drops and stains — their shape, directionality, angular distortion, and spatial relationships — to reconstruct the position and movement of a body (or blood source) at the time the blood was deposited. It is a standard forensic technique, widely used in crime scene reconstruction. However — as Borrini and Garlaschelli acknowledged — it has also been criticized as excessively subjective in some judicial applications.

**What they did:**

The study analyzed four distinct blood patterns:
1. The two short rivulets on the **back of the left hand** (wrist area).
2. The longer blood tracks running up the **forearms**.
3. The blood stain from the **side (lance) wound** on the front of the chest.
4. The alleged postmortem blood flow from the same lance wound onto the **back** (for a supine body).

Using **BPA geometric analysis** applied to published photographs of the Shroud, they determined the angle at which each drip or flow was deposited relative to the wrist/forearm/body axis. They then tested whether these angles were consistent with crucifixion postures.

**Experimental component:** They used:
- A **live human volunteer** (male, healthy) with a small blood-release point on the wrist, held in various arm positions.
- A **half-torso mannequin** with blood applied to simulate the wrist and lance wounds.
- Both **human blood** (donated, fresh) with and without added anticoagulant, and **synthetic blood** formulated to match human blood's flow properties.

**Key findings:**

*Wrist rivulets (back of left hand):*
The two short rivulets on the wrist were consistent with **arms held at approximately 45° from vertical** — a roughly diagonal upward angle. This is consistent with a person standing upright with arms partially raised, or potentially with a crucifixion position where arms are nailed at ~45°.

*Forearm flows:*
The longer forearm blood tracks required **nearly vertical arms** — arms pointing almost straight upward — in order for blood to flow in the observed direction along the forearm toward the elbow. This 45° vs. ~90° discrepancy between the two sets of marks on the same arm is the central finding: **no single arm position satisfies both blood patterns simultaneously**.

*Side wound (frontal):*
The blood flow from the chest wound was consistent with a **standing or upright position** — blood flowing vertically downward from a side wound, as would occur on a person hanging on a cross.

*Side wound (dorsal/postmortem outflow on the back):*
The belt of blood running across the lower back, which is interpreted as postmortem outflow from the lance wound when the body was laid supine, was described as "totally unrealistic" — the flow directions were inconsistent with the expected gravity-driven pattern for a supine body.

**Overall conclusion of Borrini & Garlaschelli:** "The BPA of the image of the Shroud of Turin indicates that the bloodstains are not consistent with a crucified position... At best, the image is inconsistent and...the cloth is not authentic."

**The retraction context:** The phys.org article (2018) notes that a **separate, supporting paper** — "Atomic Resolution Studies Detect New Biologic Evidences on the Turin Shroud" by Liberati et al. (*PLOS ONE*, 2017) — was retracted by PLOS ONE editors at approximately the same time, over concerns about data quality and conclusions. These two events were reported together in media, though the PLOS ONE retraction was independent of the Borrini-Garlaschelli paper (which remains published and unretracted).

### C2. Responses and Counter-Arguments

The Borrini-Garlaschelli paper generated a published formal commentary in the same journal:

**Primary formal response:**
Hermosilla, J.A. & Rucker, R.A. (2019). "Commentary On: Borrini M, Garlaschelli L. A BPA approach to the Shroud of Turin. *J Forensic Sci* DOI: 10.1111/1556-4029.13867." *Journal of Forensic Sciences*. PMID 30605574. (Also available at shroud.com/pdfs/Hermosilla EN.pdf)

**Borrini & Garlaschelli reply:**
Borrini, M. & Garlaschelli, L. (2019). "Authors' Response." *Journal of Forensic Sciences*. DOI: 10.1111/1556-4029.13941.

**Summary of the main counter-arguments:**

1. **The smooth-surface problem:** Critics pointed out that BPA angle calculations derived from drops on a **smooth plastic mannequin** surface do not translate directly to blood behavior on **damaged, hairy, dirty, dehydrated skin**. Blood flowing on abraded, sweaty skin, or clotted on prior dried-blood tracks, behaves differently from blood on clean plastic. The wrist wound on the Shroud man would be surrounded by dried blood, scourge wounds, and contaminated skin, all of which alter flow angle and speed.

2. **Anticoagulant in the blood bag:** Critics observed that Borrini and Garlaschelli used blood with **added anticoagulant** in at least some mannequin experiments. Anticoagulant prevents clotting and makes blood significantly more watery and free-flowing than the partially clotted blood in a trauma victim. This materially alters flow angles and drip behavior.

3. **Inappropriate subjects:** The volunteer subject was a **healthy, uninjured person** in a clean, temperature-controlled environment. The Shroud man's wrists had been punctured by a nail; the surrounding tissues would be lacerated, swollen, and covered in dried prior blood. The arm muscles of a crucified person would be in severe spasm or exhaustion. These differences make direct comparison to BPA from a volunteer invalid.

4. **Crucifixion positions are complex and dynamic:** A crucified person does not maintain a single static arm angle; they alternate between sagging (arms nearly vertical, body weight on wrists) and pushing up on the nailed feet to breathe (arms at a shallower angle, chest lifting). The two divergent blood-flow tracks on the forearm — which Borrini and Garlaschelli treated as incompatible — are precisely what would be expected from **blood deposited in two different phases** of crucifixion movement, a point made extensively in the earlier literature (Barbet 1950; Lavoie et al.).

5. **Legitimate BPA is still contested in courts:** Hermosilla and Rucker noted that BPA itself — as a forensic technique — has been seriously questioned in several high-profile criminal cases (including murder convictions overturned in part because BPA conclusions were unreliable). Applying it to determine the authenticity of a relic is an unusual application of a contested method.

6. **The back-stain argument:** Regarding the "totally unrealistic" postmortem dorsal blood flow, defenders argued that the Shroud shows blood was still flowing when the body was moved from the cross and carried to the tomb, then wrapped. The pattern of dorsal (back) staining may reflect the specific mechanics of wrapping the cloth while blood was still partially liquid — not simply passive gravity-driven flow in a stable supine corpse.

**Borrini and Garlaschelli's authors' response** (2019) maintained their original conclusions, arguing that the counter-arguments did not provide specific quantitative data invalidating their angle measurements, and that the methodological criticisms were speculative.

**Current status:** The Borrini-Garlaschelli paper is published, cited, and unretracted. The counter-arguments are substantively valid (especially points 1, 2, and 4) but have not produced new experimental data with better methodology to fully supersede the original study. The blood-flow angle question remains unresolved.

## Part D: Pollen Evidence

### D1. Max Frei — Sampling, Findings, and Geographic Claims

**Background:** Dr. **Max Frei-Sulzer** (1913–1983) was a Swiss criminologist with a doctorate in botany who founded and directed the City of Zurich Police Scientific Department (1948–1975). He was one of the leading forensic botanists in Europe and a recognized expert on Mediterranean flora.

**1973 sampling:** On November 24, 1973, Frei was given permission to take **12 sticky-tape samples** of surface dust from the Shroud during a limited examination in Turin. He pressed adhesive tape to the cloth surface at 12 different points and collected the adhering particles, including pollen grains.

**1978 sampling:** Frei was again granted access during the 1978 STURP investigation and took **27 additional sticky-tape samples** from the cloth. He analyzed these in his Zurich laboratory.

**Method:** Standard palynological (pollen analysis) technique: sticky-tape collection, mounting on microscope slides, light microscopy at high magnification (typically 400×–1000×), morphological identification by comparison with reference specimens from known plant species. Frei was particularly expert in Mediterranean pollen morphology.

**Plant species identified:** Frei reported identifying **58 distinct pollen types** (later revised to 57 in some accounts) from his combined 1973 and 1978 samples. The breakdown he claimed was approximately:

- **~45 species from the Jerusalem / Palestine area** (including plants of the Negev Desert, Dead Sea region, and Galilee)
- **~6 species from the eastern Middle East** broadly
- **~2 species found in Urfa / Şanlıurfa, Turkey** (ancient Edessa) — specifically designated as species #8 and #45 in his list
- **~1 species found exclusively in Istanbul / Constantinople** (species #18)
- The remainder from France and the broader European Mediterranean

Among the most frequently cited species were:
- *Gundelia tournefortii* (an Asteraceae thistle characteristic of the Middle East, particularly the Jerusalem area; some have proposed it as the plant used for the crown of thorns)
- *Zygophyllum dumosum* (a Zygophyllaceae shrub found specifically in the Israel-Jordan border zone, diagnostic for the Jerusalem-Hebron region)
- *Hordeum* sp. (barley)
- *Tamarix* sp. (tamarisk)
- Various halophyte species (salt-tolerant plants typical of the Dead Sea shore and Negev)

**Frei's geographic claim:** Because certain plant species (especially the halophytes and desert species) grow **exclusively or primarily in the Levant**, and one species was reported only from Istanbul and two only from Urfa/Edessa, Frei concluded that the Shroud had physically been present in Palestine, in Constantinople/Istanbul, and possibly in Edessa at some point in its history — precisely the three locations required by the Ian Wilson Mandylion hypothesis. Frei presented these results at conferences beginning in 1976 and in a 1982 paper in *Shroud Spectrum International*: Frei, M. (1982). "Nine Years of Palynological Studies on the Shroud." *Shroud Spectrum International*, 3, 2–7.

**Frei's death:** Max Frei died on **January 11, 1983**, before completing his final systematic report on the 1978 samples. His slides and materials passed to private hands (his family; later acquired by the **ASSIST** organization and eventually by the **Shroud of Turin Research Project** successor group). His work was never published in a peer-reviewed journal during his lifetime.

---

### D2. The Frei Credibility Problem — Hitler Diaries

In **April–May 1983**, just months after Frei's death, the Hitler Diaries scandal broke. The West German magazine *Stern* had purchased 60 volumes of purported Hitler diaries forged by **Konrad Kujau** for approximately 9.3 million Deutsche Marks. *Stern* had engaged multiple handwriting experts to authenticate them. **Max Frei-Sulzer was one of the authenticators** who had examined the diaries and provided an opinion.

The diaries were exposed as crude forgeries in May 1983 by the Bundesarchiv: the paper, ink, and binding materials were demonstrably modern (ink chloride measurements showed they had been written within the previous two years). The handwriting authentication was shown to have been performed against a **forged "control" sample** — Kujau had forged the reference sample as well, so the forged diaries matched the forged controls.

Frei's specific culpability in the Hitler Diaries affair has been debated:

- Joe Nickell (skeptical investigator, Center for Inquiry) described Frei as someone who "made a fool of himself authenticating the notorious Hitler Diaries" (1994 paper *Pollens on the 'Shroud': A Study in Deception*, Skeptical Inquirer), and noted that Frei was "found guilty and censured by the Police hierarchy in Switzerland" — though the degree to which this censure occurred is disputed in some Shroud-sympathetic sources.
- Defenders of Frei (including Werner Bulst, S.J.) argued that the handwriting experts were deceived by forged controls, that Frei's role was limited, and that his botanical expertise should be evaluated independently of his handwriting authentication failure.

**The relevance to Shroud research:** The Hitler Diaries affair established that Max Frei was capable of reaching false-positive authentication conclusions on items of significant historical importance. Combined with the methodological gaps in his Shroud pollen work (discussed below), this is treated by most mainstream scientists as a significant credibility problem — not proof that his pollen findings were fabricated, but a reason to require independent replication.

---

### D3. Uri Baruch's Re-Examination of Frei's Slides

**Uri Baruch** is an Israeli palynologist (pollen specialist) who worked with the Israel Nature and Parks Authority and collaborated with Avinoam Danin. He is an expert on the pollen flora of Israel and the Middle East.

In the late 1990s, Baruch examined 14 of the 27 slides from Frei's 1978 sampling (the full 1978 set of slides was available to researchers through the organization that had acquired them). He compared the pollen grain morphology in the slides with his own reference collections and with Danin's control specimens collected from Israeli flora.

**Key findings of Baruch's re-examination:**

- Of the 34 pollen types Frei had identified to species level, Baruch was able to confirm only **3** at the species level:
  1. *Gundelia tournefortii*
  2. *Ricinus communis* (castor oil plant)
  3. *Lomelosia (Scabiosa) prolifera*

- All of Frei's identifications that Baruch could check were **correct at the higher taxonomic level** (genus or family), but species-level confirmation was largely impossible due to the state of the samples.

- **A third of the grains Baruch examined remained unidentifiable.**

- Technical challenges: The sticky tape over the grains and the deterioration of grain wall structure (due to age and handling) prevented reliable species-level identification in most cases.

- **Anomalous distribution:** One tape contained far more pollen than all the other 25 tapes combined. This outlier tape came from the end of the sampling strip where Frei's gloved hands would have touched the adhesive, raising a contamination concern — gloves should have deposited no pollen, but the abundance of pollen at that location is unexplained.

**Subsequent assessment:** German palynologist **Thomas Litt** (University of Bonn) also examined Frei's slides and reported that waxes preserved on the pollen surfaces obscured the grain structure sufficiently to prevent precise identification at the genus level in many cases. This further undermined species-level identifications.

**Bottom line on Frei:** The geographic argument built on 58 species-level identifications rests on identifications that were mostly unconfirmed at species level by subsequent expert re-examination. *Gundelia tournefortii* and two or three other Near Eastern species are confirmed as present; the elaborate geographic story about Edessa and Constantinople rests on species that could not be confirmed.

### D4. Avinoam Danin — Flower and Plant Images on the Shroud

**Avinoam Danin** (1939–2015) was a professor of botany at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, specializing in the flora of Israel and Sinai. He was a leading authority on Israeli flora and co-authored the definitive multi-volume *Flora of Israel*.

**How Danin became involved:** Danin began collaborating with **Dr. Alan D. Whanger** (Professor Emeritus, Duke University Medical Center) and Mary Whanger in 1995. Whanger had developed the **Polarized Image Overlay Technique (PIOT)** — a method of comparing two images projected through polarizing filters onto a lenticular screen, allowing fine overlay and comparison of structural details. PIOT was published: Whanger, A.D. & Whanger, M. (1985). "Polarized Image Overlay Technique: A New Image Comparison Method and Its Applications." *Applied Optics*, 24(6), 766–772. In 1998, Uri Baruch joined the collaboration for pollen analysis.

**What Danin identified:**

Using enhanced photographs of the Shroud, Danin claimed to identify faint but structurally recognizable **floral and plant images** superimposed on or near the body image. He identified the following (among others):

1. **Gundelia tournefortii** — a large, globe-shaped Asteraceae thistle. Danin identified what he interpreted as a capitulum (flower head) of this species on the image near the shoulder and face area. This species had already been implicated by Frei's pollen work.
2. **Zygophyllum dumosum** — an Israeli-Jordanian shrub. Danin identified leaf images consistent with this species' distinctive two-lobed leaflets.
3. **Cistus creticus** (rock rose) — identified from petal images.
4. **Chrysanthemum coronarium** (crown daisy).
5. Additional species including *Capparis aegyptia*, *Picris asplenioides*, and others.

**The diagnostic geographic argument:** Danin argued that the combination of *Gundelia tournefortii* + *Zygophyllum dumosum* is only found together in a geographically **restricted zone**: bounded by lines connecting Jerusalem, Hebron, Madaba (Jordan), and Karak (Jordan). When *Cistus creticus* is added as a third species, the zone narrows further toward the **Jerusalem–Hebron corridor**. Danin concluded: "The combination of these plants is diagnostic for one region only, and that is Jerusalem."

He also noted that *Gundelia tournefortii* blooms only between **March and May** in this region, consistent with the Passion narrative's timing.

**Main publication:**
Danin, A., Whanger, A.D., Baruch, U., & Whanger, M. (1999). *Flora of the Shroud of Turin*. Missouri Botanical Garden Press, St. Louis. ISBN 0-915279-76-2.

**Additional paper:**
Danin, A. (2010). *Botany of the Shroud: The Story of Floral Images on the Shroud of Turin*. Danin Publishing, Jerusalem.

**Press release summary:** A 1999 EurekAlert/ScienceDaily press release by the Missouri Botanical Garden described the publication as presenting evidence that the Shroud "originated in Jerusalem area before the 8th century."

### D5. Peer Review Status and Critiques of Danin's Work

**Peer review question:**

The *Flora of the Shroud of Turin* was published by **Missouri Botanical Garden Press** — a peer-reviewed botanical press that publishes monographs and journal supplements (*Annals of the Missouri Botanical Garden*, *Novon*). Two distinguished American botanists — **Dr. Peter H. Raven** (former Director, Missouri Botanical Garden; President Emeritus) and **Dr. Michael G. Barbour** (University of California Davis) — reportedly expressed support for the botanical identifications.

The foundational PIOT technique was published in *Applied Optics*, a peer-reviewed journal (Whanger & Whanger 1985). However, the botanical *identifications from plant images on the Shroud* — as distinct from the pollen identifications — relied on PIOT, which is the more contested element.

**Key critiques:**

1. **Faint images and pareidolia:** The plant "images" on the Shroud are extremely faint, visible only in enhanced photographs with contrast manipulation. A **2011 study by Salvatore Lorusso** (University of Bologna) and colleagues applied modern digital image processing to two independent Shroud photographs and found **no images of flowers, coins, or other objects** beyond the body image. They concluded that the faint patterns identified by the Whangers and Danin were "only visible by incrementing the photographic contrast" and were more likely attributable to "yarn protuberances and alterations in the [1931] Enrie photographic negative."

2. **Independent non-replication:** No independent botanist — without prior commitment to the authenticity hypothesis — has published a confirmation of Danin's specific flower image identifications using the same photographic materials. The identifications rely on a subjective visual matching process that is inherently non-reproducible.

3. **Contamination alternative:** Even if the pollen identifications are accepted, pollen from the Jerusalem area on the Shroud does not necessarily indicate that the Shroud was made in Jerusalem. Pollen transport by pilgrims, visitors, or handlers who had traveled from Palestine is a plausible alternative. The Shroud was displayed publicly in Lirey and later Chambéry and Turin; any number of pilgrims returning from Jerusalem could have carried Levantine pollen on their clothing and deposited it on the cloth.

4. **Frei credibility contamination:** Danin's work was partly built on confirming Frei's pollen identifications. Given the methodological and credibility problems with Frei's work, Danin's confirmations of Near Eastern species are most credible where they rest on his own independent pollen examination (via Uri Baruch) rather than on Frei's identifications.

5. **The *XVI International Botanical Congress* (1999, St. Louis):** At the same meeting where Danin and colleagues presented their findings, attending botanists raised questions about whether the image resolution of Shroud photographs was sufficient to support species-level identification of faint floral impressions. No formal congress resolution endorsed or rejected the claims.

**Summary status:** Danin's identification of *Gundelia tournefortii* pollen (confirmed by Baruch) and *Zygophyllum dumosum* images is probably the most credible element of the botanical evidence. The broader claim of 28+ identifiable plant species from the Shroud images is not accepted as established by mainstream botanists outside the Shroud research community.

## Part E: Numismatic Evidence — Coins over the Eyes

### E1. Filas's 1980 Claim — What He Saw and How

**Francis L. Filas, S.J.** (1915–1985) was an American Jesuit theologian and professor at Loyola University Chicago. He had no specialist background in numismatics, forensic imaging, or ancient coinage.

**How the claim began:** Electronic image processing of the Shroud face was being developed in the late 1970s by Italian computer scientist Prof. Giovanni Tamburelli (University of Turin, SIP telecommunications research). Tamburelli's computer-enhanced images appeared to show faint detail near the eyebrow and eyelid regions. Filas examined these enhanced images and in **1980** published the claim that he could identify a small coin image over the **right eye** of the Shroud figure.

**What he claimed to see:**

Using enhanced photographs, Filas reported distinguishing:
- A **lituus** (a curved augur's staff or shepherd's crook) — a symbol used on specific Pontius Pilate coins.
- Four Greek letters — **UCAI** (or parts thereof) — near the outer edge of the supposed coin.

Filas and Italian numismatics collaborator **Mario Moroni** identified these as matching the inscription of a specific **Pontius Pilate lepton** (a small bronze coin, the "widow's mite" of the Gospel of Luke) minted in **29 AD**, bearing a lituus and the inscription **TIBEPIOY KAICAPOC** (of Tiberius Caesar) with a date indicator.

The coin is sometimes called a "lituus lepton" (the Julia lepton) or "lepton with lituus." Filas published his claim in: Filas, F.L. (1980). *The Dating of the Shroud of Turin from Coins of Pontius Pilate*. Cogan Productions, Youngstown.

A second, larger coin image was subsequently proposed over the **left eye**, possibly a "Julia lepton" with a simpulum (libation ladle) symbol. This was developed more extensively by Moroni and later by **Michael Marx**.

**The UCAI problem:** The coin Filas identified has the inscription TIBEPIOY KAICAPOC — so the letters visible near the eye region should spell (part of) "KAICAPOC," specifically "KAIC." Filas reported seeing "UCAI" — but the letter that should be a "K" appears as a "C" (a curved, not angular letter). Known lepton specimens uniformly show "K." Filas and Moroni later claimed that some leptons had a misspelled "C" instead of "K," and several coins with this apparent misspelling subsequently surfaced, though **biblical numismatist Mel Wacks** stated: "I know of no Pontius Pilate coin with the letters UCAI in the relative position of the Shroud coin image."

**Computer analysis at Virginia Tech:** The Spatial Data Analysis Laboratory at Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University performed computerized image enhancement and reported that the analysis "supports, though cautiously, the existence of the lituus lepton over the right eye and an outline of a coin over the left eye." This is one of the few independent technical analyses to offer any partial support, though it was cautious and acknowledged the interpretation was uncertain.

---

### E2. Why the Claim Is Rejected by Most Experts

**1. Image resolution insufficient for numismatic identification:**

The Shroud image is formed at the level of individual linen fibrils (200–600 nm depth); the surface weave structure has a resolution of approximately 0.5–1 mm per thread. A lepton coin is approximately **15–17 mm in diameter**. If placed over a closed eye (approximately 25–30 mm wide), the coin would appear in the image at approximately 15–17 pixels' worth of detail in a standard Shroud photograph. Identifying specific letter forms and symbols at this resolution requires image enhancement that is prone to **pareidolia** (the cognitive tendency to perceive meaningful patterns in ambiguous visual noise).

**2. Digital image processing and Lorusso 2011:**

Prof. **Salvatore Lorusso** (University of Bologna), Prof. Andrea Natali, and Dr. Cristina Nonfarmale published: "The Assessment of Artworks: A Comparison of Objective Methods of Analysis with Subjective Methods." In 2011, the same group processed Shroud photographs using modern digital techniques and found **no coin images** and **no flower images** beyond the body image. They concluded that all secondary images identified by various researchers were "only visible by incrementing the photographic contrast" — i.e., artifacts of contrast enhancement and not genuine features. Their conclusion was that the faint structures visible in heavily manipulated photographs correspond to "yarn protuberances and alterations in the Enrie photographic negative from 1931."

**3. Numismatic doubts:**

No specialist in ancient Judaean coinage has independently confirmed the lituus + UCAI identification from the Shroud photographs without reliance on Filas/Moroni's guided identification. L.Y. Rahmani, Director of the Jerusalem Museums and a leading scholar of Judaean burial practices, published an article in **Biblical Archaeologist** in 1980 in which he rejected, "without hesitation," any Jewish custom of placing coins on the eyelids of the dead. The Talmud and Jewish burial archaeology provide no support for such a practice; placing coins on eyes was a **pagan Greco-Roman custom** (as payment to Charon, the ferryman of the dead), not a first-century Palestinian Jewish practice, making it unlikely to appear in a Jewish burial.

**4. The pareidolia critique:**

Multiple imaging scientists who examined the Shroud as part of STURP or subsequently have stated that they could not identify coin images in the raw (unenhanced) photographs. The identification required progressively increasing contrast and digital manipulation. As contrast is increased in any ancient textile photograph, accidental patterns from weave structure, stains, and photographic grain become visible and can be interpreted as letters, shapes, or objects by an observer motivated to find them.

**5. The specific letter problem:**

Even within the Filas/Moroni framework, the four letters are read as "UCAI" — but the actual inscription should produce "KAIC." The "U" reading is problematic (Greek "K" is angular, not curved), and the substitution of "C" for "K" (if real) is a scribal error not attested on any confirmed Pilate lepton of this type known to numismatic catalogs.

---

### E3. Independent Confirmations and Denials

**Partial confirmation (cautious):**
- Virginia Tech Spatial Data Analysis Laboratory (early 1980s): cautious computerized analysis reported features "consistent with" coin outlines. (Not published in a peer-reviewed journal.)
- Tamburelli's computer processing showed structural regularities near the eyes, though Tamburelli himself did not make strong numismatic claims.
- Michael Marx and Mario Moroni extended the hypothesis, identifying a second coin type over the left eye.

**Denial / rejection:**
- **Antonio Lombatti** (Italian historian of religion): published "Doubts Concerning the Coins Over the Eyes" (shroud.com) arguing the claimed letter forms were not reproducible and the numismatic identification was unsupported. Lombatti's analysis is one of the most detailed critical reviews.
- **Mel Wacks** (biblical numismatist): stated no known Pilate lepton has the UCAI letter configuration in the position claimed.
- **L.Y. Rahmani** (Jerusalem Museums, 1980): rejected the burial custom as non-Jewish.
- **Lorusso et al. 2011**: no coins found in digitally processed images.
- **Most STURP members**: expressed skepticism. STURP's official summary report made no mention of coin images as a confirmed finding, treating secondary image claims (coins, flowers) as outside what could be scientifically established from their data.

**Consensus:** The coin-over-eyes hypothesis is not accepted by mainstream sindonologists or numismatists. It is classified as a secondary claim dependent on highly manipulated image processing. The most detailed evaluations (Lombatti; Lorusso 2011) have not confirmed it.
