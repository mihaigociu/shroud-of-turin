## I. Dating Studies (Non-Radiocarbon)

### A. Fanti et al. — Mechanical and Spectroscopic Dating

**Core publications:**
- Fanti, G. & Malfi, P. (2014). "Multi-parametric micro-mechanical dating of single fibers coming from ancient flax textiles." *Textile Research Journal*, 84(7). [Often cited in press as "2013"; the SAGE journal publication appeared in Vol. 84, 2014.]
- Fanti, G., Malfi, P., & Crosilla, F. (2015). "Mechanical and opto-chemical dating of the Turin Shroud." *MATEC Web of Conferences*, 36, 01001.

**Methods:**

*Mechanical:* Fanti designed a cycling-loads micromechanical testing machine to measure three fiber properties on individual flax fibers — Young's modulus (stiffness), tensile breaking strength, and loss factor (internal damping) — on the basis that these degrade over time following exponential decay laws as cellulose breaks down. A calibration dataset was built from linen samples spanning ~3,000 years of known age.

*Spectroscopic:* Fourier-transform infrared (FT-IR) and FT-Raman spectroscopy were used to detect molecular-level changes in cellulose and lignin chemistry over time — calibrated against known-age reference samples.

**Results:**
- Mechanical dating: ~400 AD ± 400 years (and ~260 AD from a second parameter)
- FT-IR: ~300 BC ± 400 years
- FT-Raman: ~200 BC ± 500 years
- Combined weighted mean: centered around the 1st century BC to 1st century AD

**Sample provenance:** A critical weakness. Fanti's fibers were not obtained through official Vatican/Turin channels — reportedly swept up from the Shroud during the 2002 restoration and provided informally. Chain of custody has never been independently certified.

**Validation status:**
- Calibration curves not independently validated by external textile labs working blind
- Sensitive to storage conditions of reference samples and individual fiber variation within a cloth
- The Shroud has survived two fires (1532 and earlier), extreme heat, and centuries of handling — mechanical and spectroscopic properties can be altered by factors other than chronological aging
- The loss factor showed a *linear* rather than exponential trend — an acknowledged internal inconsistency
- No independent laboratory has replicated these datings on officially sampled Shroud material
- The same group has produced multiple non-independent "1st-century" results across four distinct methods (mechanical, FT-IR, Raman, WAXS), raising confirmation-bias concerns

### B. De Caro et al. 2022 — WAXS Dating

**Citation:** De Caro, L.; Sibillano, T.; Lassandro, R.; Giannini, C.; & Fanti, G. (2022). "X-ray Dating of a Turin Shroud's Linen Sample." *Heritage*, 5(2), 860–870 (MDPI). DOI: 10.3390/heritage5020047.

**Method:** Wide-Angle X-ray Scattering (WAXS) measures the crystalline structure of cellulose in linen fibers, which degrades progressively with age. De Caro developed a calibration curve from known-age linen samples, including prominently a **Masada linen sample (55–74 AD)** as an anchor point.

**Sample:** A thread (~0.5 mm × 1 mm) from near the 1988 radiocarbon sampling corner. Not an officially sanctioned Vatican sample.

**Claimed result:** The Shroud sample's WAXS profile was "fully compatible" with the Masada sample (55–74 AD), placing the cloth in the 1st century AD — inconsistent with the 1988 medieval C14 date.

**Criticisms:**
1. **Environmental sensitivity:** Cellulose degradation depends on temperature and humidity over the entire storage history. A follow-up paper ("Long-term temperature effects on natural linen aging," *Information*, MDPI, 2022) acknowledged this but critics argue the Shroud's complex environmental history cannot be corrected for with the required precision.
2. **Sample provenance:** Chain of custody not certified; edge/corner material may not represent the main cloth.
3. **Researcher position:** All co-authors, including Fanti, are declared sindonologists. Between 2014 and 2022 this group developed four distinct dating methods all yielding 1st-century results.
4. **Non-standard method:** WAXS textile dating is not an established, peer-validated archaeological dating tool; not replicated by independent groups.
5. **Retraction context:** De Caro co-authored the 2017 PLOS ONE paper on Shroud blood that was **retracted in 2018** for inadequate controls and undisclosed competing interests (see §III-B below).
6. De Caro himself stated the "WAXS analysis should be performed by other laboratories to confirm the findings" — an invitation not yet taken up.

### C. Rogers 2005 — Vanillin Kinetics

**Citation:** Rogers, R.N. (2005). "Studies on the radiocarbon sample from the Shroud of Turin." *Thermochimica Acta*, 425(1–2), 189–194.

Rogers (Los Alamos physical chemist, STURP member) argued that the 1988 C14 sample area was chemically different from the main cloth, supporting the Benford-Marino invisible-reweave hypothesis:
- Pyrolysis-mass-spectrometry reportedly showed a cotton component and different chemical signature in Raes/C14 corner samples vs. main-cloth samples
- **Vanillin** (a lignin breakdown product) present in Raes-area fibers but **absent from the main Shroud**, implying the main cloth had aged beyond the vanillin-retention threshold — consistent with >1,300 years old

**Criticisms:**
- Skeptical re-examination found the Py-MS differences attributable to a long-chain aliphatic contaminant, not genuinely different textile composition (Bella, Garlaschelli & Samperi 2015, *Thermochimica Acta* 617)
- Vanillin loss rates in archaeological linen are poorly constrained; accuracy range of ~1,700 years vs. C14's ~150 years
- Rogers's sample access was informal and limited
- Directly contradicted by Freer-Waters & Jull 2026 (see §V)

### D. Casabianca et al. 2019 — Radiocarbon Raw Data Reanalysis

**Citation:** Casabianca, T.; Marinelli, E.; Pernagallo, G.; & Torrisi, B. (2019). "Radiocarbon Dating of the Turin Shroud: New Evidence from Raw Data." *Archaeometry*, 61(5), 1223–1231. DOI: 10.1111/arcm.12467.

**How the data were obtained:** Following a freedom-of-information request in 2017, the British Museum released the previously withheld raw sub-sample measurements from the three 1988 AMS laboratories.

**Key statistical findings:**
1. Some measurements reported to the British Museum were *modified* from original laboratory values before the Nature publication, using an unstated methodology.
2. **Statistical heterogeneity** in the Shroud sub-samples exceeds what is expected from stated measurement errors — chi-squared test gives only ~1.4% probability of arising by chance.
3. A **spatial gradient** was found: sub-samples from different positions along the C14 strip gave systematically different dates in a monotone pattern — consistent with uneven contamination rather than a homogeneous textile.

**Independent confirmation:**
- Walsh, B.J. & Schwalbe, L. (2020). "An instructive inter-laboratory comparison: The 1988 radiocarbon dating of the Shroud of Turin." *Journal of Archaeological Science: Reports*, 29, 102049. — Walsh & Schwalbe, not sindonologists, independently confirmed statistical heterogeneity in the Shroud sub-samples not seen in the control samples.

**Skeptical assessment:** The heterogeneity is real but does not by itself prove contamination or an incorrect date — it means the uncertainty is larger than originally reported. The central medieval date remains consistent with the data under wider error bars.

## II. Image Formation Studies

### A. Di Lazzaro et al. (ENEA) — UV Laser Work 2008–2012

**Citations:**
- Di Lazzaro, P. et al. (2008). "Coloring linens with excimer lasers to simulate the body image of the Turin Shroud." *Applied Optics*, 47(9), 1278–1285.
- Di Lazzaro, P.; Murra, D.; Nichelatti, E.; Santoni, A.; & Baldacchini, G. (2012). "Superficial and Shroud-like coloration of linen by short laser pulses in the vacuum ultraviolet." *Applied Optics*, 51(36), 8567–8578.

**What the experiments demonstrated:**

Using KrF excimer lasers (248 nm / deep UV), the team produced linen coloration reproducing several specific Shroud image features:
- Coloration confined to outermost fibers (correct superficiality)
- Correct yellow-straw hue
- No UV fluorescence (consistent with Shroud image, inconsistent with most pigments)
- Penetration depth of a few hundred nanometers — matching the ~200 nm observed by STURP

**The energy problem:** Explicitly quantified by the team: to color a full human body-sized linen area at the required fluence, approximately **34,000 billion watts (3.4 × 10¹³ W)** would be required in a brief pulse — far beyond any known natural or technologically feasible medieval process.

**Additional limits:**
- VUV radiation is absorbed by air within fractions of a millimeter — it cannot travel through an air gap from a body to cloth, requiring either direct contact (which conflicts with the image's lack of contact-distortion) or a source at the cloth surface itself
- ENEA experiments colored the primary cell wall; the Shroud image appears to involve only the *outermost* impurity layer — not fully reproduced
- Full-body geometry, 3D encoding, half-tone mechanism, and double superficiality were not demonstrated in these experiments

**The team's explicit framing:** "We are not in a position to affirm whether the Shroud image was formed by a supernatural phenomenon or not; our experiments simply show that intense VUV radiation can produce Shroud-like coloration on linen."

### B. Fanti & Maggiolo 2004 — Reverse-Face Image Discovery (Double Superficiality)

**Citation:** Fanti, G. & Maggiolo, R. (2004). "The double superficiality of the frontal image of the Turin Shroud." *Journal of Optics A: Pure and Applied Optics*, 6(6), 491–503.

**Context:** After the 2002 restoration removed the Holland backing cloth for the first time since 1534, the reverse face was photographed in high resolution — the first modern scientific photographs of the back. Fanti and Maggiolo applied digital image processing (Gaussian filters, Fourier-domain spatial frequency filtering) to these images.

**Finding:** A faint face image is present on the *reverse face* of the cloth, visible only after digital processing, corresponding positionally to the front facial image. Characteristics:
- Extremely faint; invisible to the naked eye
- Confined to the face region only (not the full body)
- Superficial — confined to surface fibers, not penetrating the cloth interior
- The front and reverse images are NOT exact mirror images — differences exist in detail

**Significance:** The frontal image exists on both surfaces of the cloth, with completely unaffected fiber cross-sections between them. This "double superficiality" is the hardest physical constraint in the field — no proposed image-formation mechanism (medieval forgery, rubbing, Maillard reaction, photography, corona discharge, or VUV laser) has been shown capable of producing two discrete, matched, superficial images on opposite faces of a woven cloth with no coloration in the interior.

### C. Moraes 2026 — 3D Digital Low-Relief Sculpture Hypothesis

**Citation:** Moraes, C. (2026). "Image formation on the holy Shroud — A digital 3D approach." *Archaeometry*. DOI: 10.1111/arcm.70030.

**Author:** Cicero Moraes — Brazilian 3D digital graphics specialist, not a textile scientist or archaeologist.

**Method:** Using open-source 3D software (MakeHuman, Blender, CloudCompare), Moraes created a virtual full 3D body and a virtual low-relief sculpture and simulated cloth draping over each. He argued the Shroud image geometry more closely matches draping over a **low-relief surface** than a full 3D body, because full-body draping introduces geometric distortions (the "Agamemnon mask effect") absent from the Shroud.

**What the paper does and does not show:**
- Addresses *only* image geometry — not superficiality, fiber chemistry, 3D encoding, blood-before-image, half-tone mechanism, or double superficiality
- Moraes explicitly stated: "I don't know how the image was printed or transferred"
- Used a body height of 180 cm (exceeding the accepted anthropometric range for the Shroud figure, ~173–177 cm) and mirrored anatomical orientation

**Peer response:**

Casabianca, T.; Marinelli, E.; & Piana, A. — "Commentary on 'Image Formation on the Holy Shroud — A Digital 3D Approach.'" *Archaeometry*. DOI: 10.1111/arcm.70109.

Critique: ambiguous research objectives; incorrect body height and reversed orientation; fallacious reasoning in extrapolating from geometric similarity to historical forgery; absence of essential image features in the analysis.

Moraes responded (DOI: 10.1111/arcm.70112), defending the paper's "strictly methodological" and geometric scope.

**Assessment:** Raises a legitimate geometric question; received disproportionate media attention relative to its actual scope.

## III. Blood and Biological Studies

### A. Borrini & Garlaschelli 2018 — BPA Study

**Citation:** Borrini, M. & Garlaschelli, L. (2018). "A BPA approach to the Shroud of Turin." *Journal of Forensic Sciences*, 63(4), 1136–1142. DOI: 10.1111/1556-4029.13867.

**Authors:** Mattia Borrini (forensic anthropologist, Liverpool John Moores University); Luigi Garlaschelli (Italian skeptical chemist).

**Method:** Bloodstain Pattern Analysis (BPA) — geometric reconstruction of bloodstain angles and trajectories from drop shape, using living volunteers and mannequins with real and artificial blood in different arm positions.

**Key findings:**
- Short wrist rivulets are geometrically consistent with arms at **~45° to vertical** — not the near-vertical position required for crucifixion
- Forearm bloodstains require **nearly vertical arm orientation** — inconsistent with the wrist data
- These two observations require *different arm angles* and cannot both be consistent with a single body position
- Back lance wound flows: described as "totally unrealistic" for a supine corpse

**Published criticisms (same journal):**
- Hermosilla, A.L. & Rucker, R. (2019). Commentary. *Journal of Forensic Sciences*, 64(1), 137–143.
- Bevilacqua, M. et al. (2019). Additional commentary.

Key objections: smooth plastic mannequin vs. battered, lacerated, swollen skin (fundamental surface difference); neither author is a forensic physician; BPA as a discipline has reliability issues even in criminal courts; crucifixion arm positions varied; body slumping and post-mortem repositioning were not modeled.

**Assessment:** First systematic BPA application; asks legitimate questions; methodology rests on idealized assumptions that significantly limit evidentiary weight.

### B. The Retracted 2017 PLOS ONE Paper

**Citation:** Carlino, E.; De Caro, L.; Giannini, C.; & Fanti, G. (2017). "Atomic resolution studies detect new biologic evidences on the Turin Shroud." *PLOS ONE*, 12(6), e0180487. **[Retracted July 2018]**

**What it claimed:** Electron microscopy and crystallographic analysis on a single Shroud fiber found nanoparticles of creatinine bound to iron oxide nanoparticles — argued as biomarkers of severe trauma, consistent with a person who underwent extreme physical violence before death.

**Why retracted:**
- Insufficient controls (no ink controls, no animal blood controls)
- Findings from a single 1-mm fiber cannot be generalized to the whole cloth
- Contamination could not be ruled out given the Shroud's handling history
- The sample was provided by STERA (Shroud of Turin Education and Research Association, an advocacy group) — not disclosed in the Competing Interests statement

Retraction was imposed over the authors' objections — indicating the editors were not satisfied with the authors' defense. De Caro's co-authorship on this retracted paper is relevant context for evaluating his subsequent work (the 2022 WAXS paper).

### C. Barcaccia et al. 2015 — DNA Study

**Citation:** Barcaccia, G.; Galla, G.; Achilli, A.; Olivieri, A.; & Torroni, A. (2015). "Uncovering the sources of DNA found on the Turin Shroud." *Scientific Reports*, 5, 14484. DOI: 10.1038/srep14484.

High-throughput sequencing of dust and pollen samples vacuumed from the Shroud in 1977–1978 and 1988. Found:
- **Plant DNA:** 19 taxa including species from Mediterranean, Central Europe, North Africa, Middle East, East Asia, and the **Americas**
- **Human mtDNA:** Haplogroups representing Europe, North/East Africa, Middle East, and India

The presence of American plant species (post-Columbian) in the 1977–1978 samples confirms extensive contamination. The authors themselves stated: "such diversity does not exclude a Medieval origin in Europe but it would be also compatible with the historic path followed by the Turin Shroud."

### D. 2026 Barcaccia & Achilli Metagenomics Preprint

**Citation:** Barcaccia, G.; Achilli, A.; et al. (2026). "DNA Traces on the Shroud of Turin: Metagenomics of the 1978 Official Sample Collection." *bioRxiv*. DOI: 10.64898/2026.03.19.712852. Posted March 31, 2026. [Not yet peer-reviewed as of April 2026.]

**Methods:** Full metagenomic shotgun sequencing of material from the 1978 official STURP sample collection.

**Key findings:**
- Human mtDNA haplogroups identified: K1a1b1a (matched sample collector's own genome), H2a2, H1b, H33 (prevalent in Near East / Druze population)
- ~40% of human genetic material traced to Indian lineages
- Rich microbiome including skin bacteria and high-salinity-adapted archaea (noted as consistent with Dead Sea environments)
- Non-human DNA: cats, dogs, cattle, pigs, carrots, wheat, **maize, peanuts, bananas** (all post-Columbian — obvious modern contamination)

**What the study cannot do:**
- Date the cloth
- Identify a specific individual
- Prove or disprove a Near Eastern or 1st-century origin
- Distinguish ancient from recent DNA without ancient DNA authentication methods (damage patterns, deamination analysis)

**Expert response (*Scientific American*):** "Metagenomics cannot determine whether the Shroud is older than the Middle Ages. That would require new radiocarbon dating." Media and Vatican News coverage significantly overstated the findings. The study represents a palimpsest of contamination accumulated over centuries of handling.

## IV. The 2002 Restoration and Reverse-Face Findings

The 2002 Holy See-authorized conservation (Flury-Lemberg & Tomedi, June 20 – July 23, 2002) removed the Holland backing cloth for the first time since 1534, exposing the reverse face and enabling the discovery of double superficiality (see §II-B).

**Key new information from 2002:**
- Full-resolution photographs of the reverse face — unavailable to STURP 1978
- Enabled Fanti & Maggiolo's 2004 reverse-face image discovery
- Removal of all 30 patches revealed the cloth's full burn damage pattern

**Controversy:** The work proceeded without an internationally agreed-upon scientific protocol. Critics argued that charred threads and 1534 patches in contact with the Shroud for 468 years were discarded, potentially losing irreplaceable evidence. No STURP-equivalent multi-laboratory examination accompanied the restoration.

## V. Freer-Waters & Jull 2026 — Textile Analysis of 1988 Sample Fragments

**Citation:** Freer-Waters, R. & Jull, A.J.T. (2026). "Analysis of textile fragments from the 1988 radiocarbon samples of the Turin Shroud." *npj Heritage Science*, 14. DOI: 10.1038/s40494-026-02530-7.

**Background:** The invisible-reweave hypothesis (Marino & Benford 2000; Rogers 2005) claims the 1988 C14 sample area was a medieval repair patch containing younger material. Freer-Waters is a textile conservator; Jull was the Arizona AMS laboratory director who performed the 1988 dating.

**What was analyzed:** Two fragments physically retained by the University of Arizona from the actual 1988 radiocarbon sampling, compared against control textiles distributed to the participating labs.

**Key findings:**
- The Arizona-retained fragments are **consistent with the main body of the Shroud** — same weave pattern and fiber characteristics
- **No evidence of contamination or anomalous textile composition**
- **Does not support the invisible-reweave hypothesis** for the Arizona sample area

**Published criticism:** Mark Oxley ("Evidence is Not Proof: A Response to Prof. Timothy Jull," shroud.com) argued the textile analysis addresses gross physical structure but not the fine chemical differences Rogers claimed (vanillin, dye coating, pyrolysis-MS). Also questioned whether the analyzed fragments' exact position within the C14 corner is identical to what Rogers examined.

**Assessment:** The strongest direct evidence to date that the 1988 Arizona sample was genuine Shroud linen, not a repair patch. Published in a Nature-portfolio journal. Institutional affiliations of the authors (Arizona lab) are noted by critics but do not undermine the physical textile findings.

## VII. Pollen and Botanical Studies (Post-2000)

**Danin et al. (1999–2002):** Avinoam Danin (Hebrew University) analyzed 313 pollen grains from Frei's original tapes and identified *Gundelia tournefortii* (a thistle blooming in Israel March–May) and species consistent with a Jerusalem spring provenance. Published "Flora of the Shroud of Turin" (Missouri Botanical Garden Press, 1999) with Whanger and Baruch.

**Litt (2001):** Palynologist Thomas Litt examined available pollen material with modern equipment and concluded **none of the grains could be identified to species level**; recommended suspending all conclusions from prior pollen studies until fresh collection with modern methods is possible.

**Boi (2016):** Italian palynologist Marzia Boi published in *Archaeometry* arguing the most abundant pollen on the Shroud belonged to *Helichrysum* (everlasting flower genus), consistent with anointing and embalming oils used in 1st-century Jewish burial practice.

**Barcaccia 2015/2026 DNA work** (see §III-C and §III-D): Plant DNA confirms extensive post-manufacture contamination from multiple continents including the Americas.

## VIII. What Has Changed in the Scientific Picture Since 1988

**What has materially changed:**

1. **The statistical heterogeneity of the 1988 C14 data is now documented in multiple peer-reviewed papers.** The 1989 *Nature* paper's "conclusive evidence" framing is no longer tenable in its strong form. The uncertainty is larger than originally reported, though the medieval date range remains the best-supported single estimate.

2. **The reverse-face image (Fanti & Maggiolo 2004) is a genuine new discovery** imposing constraints unrecognized before the 2002 restoration. No image-formation mechanism fully accounts for double superficiality.

3. **Rogers's repair-patch hypothesis (2005)**, while still widely cited by authenticity advocates, has been directly undermined by the 2026 Freer-Waters & Jull textile analysis of the actual 1988 Arizona material.

4. **UV laser physics (ENEA 2012)** showed some Shroud image features can be reproduced by specific radiative mechanisms, but the energy scales required (34 kJ in nanosecond pulses) are far beyond any known natural or medieval process.

5. **DNA evidence (2015, 2026)** confirms extensive multi-geographic contamination but cannot resolve age or origin.

**What has not changed:**

- The 1988 radiocarbon date of **1260–1390 CE** remains the only validated, directly measured age of the cloth. No peer-reviewed evidence has established that the samples were contaminated in a way that would shift the date by centuries.
- No alternative dating method has been independently replicated on certified Shroud material by laboratories without declared sindonologist affiliations.
- The image formation mechanism remains genuinely unexplained at the level of microscopic fiber chemistry.
- The Church has not authorized new systematic scientific access.
