# Phase 11 — The Skeptical and Mainstream Scientific Case Against Authenticity

> **Purpose:** A systematic, evidence-based presentation of the skeptical case: McCrone's chemical analysis, Nickell's artistic replication, Garlaschelli's 2009 reproduction, art-historical and historical arguments, the mainstream scientific consensus, and the Moraes 2026 3D-modeling study and its reception.

## 1. Walter McCrone's Chemical Analysis

### 1.1 Background and Sample Access

Walter C. McCrone (1916–2002) was a world-renowned microscopist, founder of the McCrone Research Institute in Chicago, and a recipient of the American Chemical Society's National Award in Analytical Chemistry (2000, partly for his Shroud work). He was not a founding member of STURP; he obtained access to the sticky-tape samples after the October 1978 Turin examination through STURP member Ray Rogers, who loaned him the tapes.

STURP collected 32 adhesive tape samples from the Shroud surface during the 1978 examination. Of these, 18 were taken from body-image or blood-image areas; 14 from non-image control areas. Each tape carried approximately 1,000 linen fibers. McCrone examined them at the McCrone Research Institute beginning in late 1978 and published his findings in a series of papers from 1980 onward.

### 1.2 Analytical Methods

McCrone's primary tool was **polarized light microscopy (PLM)**, an established forensic technique he had applied to questioned documents for decades. His procedure:

1. Examined fibers from all 32 tapes at high magnification under transmitted polarized light.
2. Characterized colored particles by: color, pleochroism, shape, size, crystallinity, refractive indices, and birefringence.
3. Confirmed elemental identity using **electron microprobe X-ray analysis** (energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy, EDS) to detect iron (Fe), mercury (Hg), and sulfur (S) in specific particle populations.
4. Ran **microchemical tests** for iron compounds, mercuric sulfide, and biological materials.

He explicitly did not use mass spectrometry, Raman spectroscopy, or X-ray fluorescence mapping — methods that STURP used on the cloth in situ.

### 1.3 Specific Findings

**On the body-image tapes:**
- Found billions of submicron-sized particles with optical properties (color, pleochroism, refractive indices, birefringence) **identical** to hydrous and anhydrous iron oxide (Fe₂O₃, collectively known as iron earth pigments / red ochre / hematite). Particle size: predominantly less than 1 micron (sub-micron).
- Red ochre was present on **20 of the body- and blood-image tapes**.
- The particles adhered to the outer surface of individual linen fibers.

**On the blood-image tapes:**
- Found a second, distinct pigment population: particles with properties matching **vermilion (mercuric sulfide, HgS)**, a bright red artists' pigment. Electron microprobe confirmed Fe, Hg, and S in blood-image areas.
- Vermilion was present on **11 blood-image tapes only**.
- Forensic tests for blood (protein, porphyrin, bilirubin assays) on these fibers were, in McCrone's hands, **uniformly negative**.

**On the non-image control tapes:**
- Both red ochre and vermilion were **absent** from all 12 non-image tape fibers examined.

**McCrone's interpretation:**
The distribution — pigment present in image areas, absent in non-image areas — was the key evidentiary point. He concluded that an artist had applied a dilute suspension of red ochre in a **collagen tempera (gelatin) medium**, a technique analogous to the grisaille employed by 14th-century European painters such as Simone Martini. The "blood" areas were additionally highlighted with vermilion, also in a collagen binder. He argued no blood was present and that the entire image was painted circa 1355.

### 1.4 Key Publications

- McCrone, W.C. & Skirius, C. (1980). Light microscopical study of the Turin "Shroud." *The Microscope*, 28(3–4), 105–113.
- McCrone, W.C. (1981). Light microscopical study of the Turin "Shroud" (II). *The Microscope*, 29, 19–38.
- McCrone, W.C. (1990). **The Shroud of Turin: Blood or Artist's Pigment?** *Accounts of Chemical Research*, 23(3), 77–83. DOI: 10.1021/ar00171a004. *(The most cited peer-reviewed version of his case.)*
- McCrone, W.C. (1999). ***Judgment Day for the Shroud of Turin***. Microscope Publications (Chicago). *(Book-length synthesis.)*

### 1.5 Key Arguments in *Judgment Day for the Shroud of Turin* (1999)

The book is McCrone's comprehensive statement. Its core arguments:

1. **The image is paint:** The submicron iron oxide particles are distributed exactly where paint would be applied — on image fibers, not on non-image fibers — and are embedded in a collagen (gelatin) medium consistent with medieval tempera technique.
2. **There is no blood:** His microchemical tests for blood constituents (hemoglobin, porphyrin) were negative on blood-area fibers; the red coloration derives from vermilion pigment, not hemoglobin.
3. **The historical record confirms medieval origin:** The complete absence of any documentation before 1356, Bishop Henri de Poitiers' investigation (see §5), and the d'Arcis memorandum of 1389 all corroborate a mid-14th-century creation.
4. **The negative image is not miraculous:** The artist, he argued, produced a negative image unknowingly: working by darkening the cloth wherever the body surface contacted it and grading the tone at contact areas — producing a natural photographic negative without understanding photographic principles.
5. **STURP's iron-oxide finding corroborates, not refutes:** McCrone noted that STURP's own X-ray fluorescence (XRF) data showed elevated iron in image areas — data that STURP members interpreted differently.

McCrone received the ACS National Award in Analytical Chemistry (2000) in part for this body of work, which the chemical community regarded as technically competent even if contested in its interpretation.

### 1.6 The Heller–Adler Rebuttal: Specific Technical Disagreements

STURP scientists **John Heller** (biophysicist) and **Alan Adler** (chemist, porphyrin specialist) examined the same tape samples and published their counter-findings:

- Heller, J.H. & Adler, A.D. (1980). Blood on the Shroud of Turin. *Applied Spectroscopy*, 34(3).
- Heller, J.H. & Adler, A.D. (1981). **A Chemical Investigation of the Shroud of Turin.** *Canadian Society of Forensic Science Journal*, 14(3), 81–103.

Their specific technical disagreements with McCrone:

**1. Iron purity argument.**
Heller and Adler measured the iron on the Shroud and found it to be approximately **99% chemically pure** — it contained essentially no manganese, nickel, or cobalt. Medieval iron-earth pigments (red ochre) from known 14th-century sources are contaminated with these transition metals at levels greater than 1%. The purity of the iron on the Shroud, they argued, was inconsistent with a painter's pigment and consistent with iron naturally present in linen (from water used in processing flax, or from blood).

**2. Iron distribution — no image correlation at microscopic level.**
STURP member Samuel Pellicori's independent measurements found that "the body image does not contain enough iron to contribute to its visibility." Heller himself found no statistically greater amounts of iron in image fibers compared to non-image control fibers at the sub-fiber level — casting doubt on iron oxide as the colorant.

**3. The particle dissolution test.**
Heller and Adler reported that the larger red particles on blood-area fibers — what McCrone called "vermilion" and "red ochre" — behaved differently in dissolution:
- True inorganic pigments (Fe₂O₃, HgS) dissolve in cold hydrochloric acid but do NOT dissolve in proteolytic enzymes.
- Particles from the "blood" areas dissolved in **proteolytic enzymes** (proteinase K, pepsin), which dissolve protein, but resisted HCl — the opposite of inorganic pigment behavior.
This, they argued, demonstrated that many of the "red particles" were **organic** (protein-based, i.e., blood-derived hemoglobin breakdown products), not inorganic pigments. McCrone's PLM could not distinguish these two populations.

**4. Blood chemistry identification.**
Adler applied microspectrophotometry to identify the specific absorption spectrum of **acid methemoglobin** — the form hemoglobin takes after great age and denaturation — in the blood-area fibers. The spectrum matched expected hemoglobin derivative spectra. They further confirmed:
- **Porphyrin fluorescence** (characteristic chemical signature of hemoglobin breakdown products)
- **Bilirubin** (a bile pigment produced by hemoglobin degradation, elevated in traumatic blood — consistent with a severely injured individual)
- **Albumin** (plasma protein)

Their Table 5 in the 1981 paper listed 12 independent tests all positive for blood constituents.

**5. No image-forming medium.**
STURP's chemical analysis found no evidence of an organic binding medium (gelatin, egg tempera, oil, gum arabic) in the image areas — which would be expected if the image were painted. McCrone's claim of a collagen tempera medium was not corroborated by other analytical methods.

On June 24, 1990, four STURP members — **Alan Adler, Eric Jumper, Ray Rogers, and Larry Schwalbe** — produced a formal written critique of McCrone's 1990 *Accounts of Chemical Research* paper, submitted as a letter-to-the-editor to that journal (editor Fred McLafferty). The publication status of this letter is uncertain from available records, but the document survives in the STERA (Shroud of Turin Education and Research Association) archives.

### 1.7 Was McCrone's Finding Independently Verified or Refuted?

The picture is mixed:

**Supporting McCrone:**
- Nickell (1983, 1998) accepted McCrone's iron oxide identification as corroborating the painting hypothesis.
- The ACS's recognition of McCrone's work (2000 award) signals that the analytical chemistry community did not find his methods technically defective.

**Challenging McCrone:**
- No subsequent independent laboratory has identified a collagen (gelatin) tempera binding medium in image fibers using modern analytical methods (IR spectroscopy, mass spectrometry, HPLC).
- Ray Rogers — who originally supplied McCrone with the tapes — later concluded publicly that McCrone had misidentified naturally occurring environmental iron oxide particles (from retting water) as artists' pigment. Rogers's 2005 *Thermochimica Acta* paper (which challenged the 1988 C14 dating) also cast doubt on a simple painting explanation for the image.
- A 2017 ScienceDirect paper (Borrini, Garlaschelli et al.) studying blood-transfer patterns did not challenge the presence of actual blood.
- Adler's identification of bilirubin-enriched, traumatic blood chemistry — pointing to a specific physiological signature of trauma — has not been refuted by any published chemical study.

**Modern analytical consensus:**
Current analytical chemistry does not support iron oxide as a sufficient image-formation mechanism. Even if iron oxide is present (all parties agree it is), the Shroud image's **extreme superficiality** (color confined to the outermost 200 nm of individual cellulose fibers, confirmed by STURP's Micro-FTIR and UV/visible spectroscopy) is inconsistent with deposited pigment, which would penetrate the fiber. Paint particles adhere to the surface at the micron scale, not the nanometer scale of the cellulose oxidation that forms the Shroud's image.

## 2. Joe Nickell's Case

### 2.1 *Inquest on the Shroud of Turin* (1983; updated 1998)

Joe Nickell (1944–2025) was a senior research fellow and "Investigative Files" columnist at the *Skeptical Inquirer*/Center for Inquiry. His 1983 book, updated in 1998 as *Inquest on the Shroud of Turin: Latest Scientific Findings* (Prometheus Books, Amherst, NY), is the most comprehensive popular-level skeptical treatment.

**Main arguments:**

1. **Historical impossibility:** The complete absence of any reliable documentation before 1356 (when the cloth appeared at Lirey in the possession of the French knight Geoffrey de Charny) is inexplicable if the cloth were the actual burial shroud of Jesus of Nazareth. A genuine first-century relic would have attracted documentation across twelve centuries.

2. **The d'Arcis memorandum:** Bishop Pierre d'Arcis of Troyes wrote in 1389 that his predecessor Bishop Henri de Poitiers had investigated the cloth circa 1355–1390 and established that it was "cunningly painted," with the truth "attested by the artist who had painted it." Nickell regards this as strong historical evidence of a contemporary medieval forgery (see §5 for detailed analysis).

3. **The artistic technique — bas-relief rubbing:** Nickell's signature contribution was demonstrating that a medieval rubbing technique could produce many of the Shroud's apparent "mysteries."
   - He wrapped a wet linen cloth over a **bas-relief sculpture** (a shallow-relief carving of a human figure) and allowed it to dry, molding it to the surface contours.
   - When dry, he applied **powdered pigment** (rather than wet paint) with a dauber — analogous to making a gravestone rubbing — to produce a negative image on the raised contact areas.
   - This technique: (a) produces a **negative image** without the artist intending one; (b) requires **no brushstrokes** (the daubing produces a diffuse, non-directional application); (c) results in image formation only on raised contact areas, somewhat analogous to cloth-draping.

4. **The image proportions are non-realistic:** Nickell noted that the body proportions are distorted in ways consistent with a bas-relief rather than an actual human being. The arms are disproportionately long (needed to cover the groin of a supine figure), the fingers are elongated on one hand, the forehead is too short, and the overall figure would represent a man of extraordinary height (approximately 6'2"–6'4") — exceptional for the 14th century.

5. **Corroboration from McCrone:** Nickell accepts McCrone's identification of red ochre particles as confirming a painting/rubbing origin, though the two differ in mechanism (Nickell's wet-cloth rubbing versus McCrone's direct painting).

**Bibliography note:**
- First edition: Nickell, J. (1983). *Inquest on the Shroud of Turin*. Prometheus Books, Buffalo, NY. ISBN 0-87975-194-4.
- Revised/expanded: Nickell, J. (1998). *Inquest on the Shroud of Turin: Latest Scientific Findings*. Prometheus Books, Amherst, NY. ISBN 1-57392-272-2.

### 2.2 How Nickell Addresses the VP-8 Three-Dimensional Finding

The VP-8 Image Analyzer finding (Jackson, Jumper, Mottern, 1976–1978) is one of the most-cited pro-authenticity arguments: when image intensity is mapped to vertical relief in the VP-8, the Shroud yields a three-dimensionally plausible human figure, whereas ordinary photographs produce distorted, non-3D results.

Nickell's response is indirect rather than direct:

1. **His bas-relief technique also encodes distance information:** When a cloth is draped over a bas-relief and pigment is applied by daubing, the quantity of pigment deposited correlates with the degree of cloth-surface contact, which in turn correlates with the height of the relief. The result is an image that has a partial distance-intensity relationship — hence some three-dimensional information.

2. **The VP-8 result from Nickell's reproduction was not considered decisive:** STURP researchers (Stevenson and Habermas, and later Jackson) noted that Nickell's image, when analyzed on a VP-8, shows a three-dimensional character that is "not truly three-dimensional" and is significantly distorted compared to the Shroud — the hands are embedded in the body and the legs show unnatural discontinuities. This is cited as a weakness of Nickell's model.

3. **Nickell's position on VP-8 in the 1998 edition:** He argues that the "three-dimensional" quality of the Shroud image is not unique to body-contact imprints — many photographs and drawings, when contrast-enhanced and displayed on a VP-8, produce some 3D-like relief. The claim that *only* a cloth draped over an actual body would produce the Shroud's specific VP-8 result is, in his view, overstated by proponents.

4. **He does not fully rebut the finding:** Nickell's 1998 update does not contain a detailed quantitative refutation of the Jackson et al. 3D analysis. The gap between Nickell's bas-relief reproduction's VP-8 result and the original Shroud's VP-8 result remains a point on which skeptics have not provided a complete answer.

### 2.3 How Nickell Addresses the "Blood Before Image" Finding

The STURP finding that there is **no image under the blood stains** — i.e., the image colorization stops at the edges of blood marks, implying the blood was deposited first and prevented image formation — is considered by STURP-aligned researchers to be strong evidence against a painting hypothesis.

Nickell's response:

1. **He contests that the "blood" is real blood:** Accepting McCrone's identification of the blood areas as containing vermilion tempera paint, Nickell argues that an artist who first painted "blood" and then applied the body image pigment on top could produce the "blood before image" appearance without any real blood being involved. The order would be: (1) paint blood marks in vermilion tempera; (2) apply body image pigment (red ochre) over the cloth, which does not adhere where the prior paint coat has altered the linen surface — creating the appearance of "no image under blood."

2. **This counter-argument has a significant weakness:** Adler and Heller's enzymatic dissolution test (see §1.6) showed that the red particles in blood areas dissolve in proteolytic enzymes — behavior inconsistent with paint, consistent with protein-based blood. Nickell's response to this specific test (the proteolytic dissolution result) is limited. In Center for Inquiry blog posts, he suggested the azobilirubin test could reflect paint additives, but he did not directly address the enzyme dissolution result in his published work.

3. **Serum halo evidence:** STURP's UV fluorescence photography revealed **serum halos** around the blood marks — a phenomenon caused by blood clot serum soaking into the cloth, leaving a fluorescent ring when the clot later detaches. This is a specific, known forensic characteristic of actual blood on cloth and is difficult to explain as a paint artifact. Nickell does not directly address the serum halo evidence in the 1998 edition.

## 3. Luigi Garlaschelli's 2009 Reproduction

### 3.1 Full Experimental Details

Luigi Garlaschelli, then a professor of organic chemistry at the University of Pavia, Italy, produced a life-size reproduction of the Shroud of Turin, announced in October 2009. The research was funded by the **Union of Rationalist Atheists and Agnostics (UAAR)** and published in 2010.

**Materials used:**
- Linen cloth of appropriate dimensions (approximately 4.4 × 1.1 m)
- **Ochre** (iron oxide pigment) with traces of **acid** (an acidified suspension)
- A bas-relief sculpture for the face area
- An oven for artificial aging

**Method (step-by-step):**
1. A human volunteer lay on a flat surface. The linen cloth was draped over the volunteer's body.
2. A pigment of **ochre with traces of acid** was rubbed over the cloth surface, following the contours of the body. The face was produced separately using a **bas-relief** of the face rather than a live face.
3. The cloth was then **artificially aged** by heating it in an oven and subsequently washing it. This aging/washing process removed the surface pigment from the cloth, leaving behind a chemical discoloration of the cellulose fibers — a faint, sepia-toned image.
4. Blood-like stains were separately added to simulate wound patterns.

The rationale for the acid: acidified iron oxide can catalyze the oxidative dehydration of cellulose (a Maillard-type reaction), producing a yellowish-brown discoloration of the fiber surface. After washing away loose pigment, only the chemical change to the cellulose remains.

**Exact journal reference:**
> Garlaschelli, L. (2010). Life-size Reproduction of the Shroud of Turin and its Image. *Journal of Imaging Science and Technology*, **54**(4), 040301-1–040301-14. DOI: 10.2352/J.ImagingSci.Technol.2010.54.4.040301

*(Received: November 30, 2009; accepted: March 24, 2010.)*

### 3.2 Properties Successfully Reproduced

- **Negative image character:** The resulting image was a photographic negative, consistent with the Shroud.
- **Sepia color:** The aged, washed image had a yellowish-brown tone similar to the Shroud.
- **Chemical discoloration (not pigment residue):** After washing, the image color derived from chemical discoloration of the linen cellulose rather than surface pigment particles — matching the STURP finding that the Shroud image is not a surface coating.
- **Half-tone character:** The image appeared as a diffuse, half-tone pattern without sharp boundaries.
- **Some 3D information:** The cloth had some areas of light and dark corresponding to body relief — encoding partial distance information.
- **Recognized as superior to prior attempts:** Fanti and Heimburger (in their critical letter, see §3.4) acknowledged that Garlaschelli's reproduction was "up to now the best Shroud-like image never produced."

### 3.3 Properties the Reproduction Failed to Match

**Superficiality (critical failure):**
The Shroud's image color is confined to a layer approximately **200 nm (0.2 μm)** thick — only the outermost surface of individual cellulose microfibrils. Garlaschelli's acid-etching process colors the **entire depth of the cellulose fiber**, not just its outermost surface. This is a fundamental mechanical difference that cannot be reconciled by washing alone.

**3D encoding fidelity:**
While Garlaschelli's cloth has some 3D character, independent analysis found it "nowhere near as sophisticated as the shroud." Specifically, under VP-8 analysis, the hands appeared embedded in the body surface and the legs showed unnatural discontinuities — distortions not present in the Shroud's VP-8 image.

**Blood-before-image sequence:**
The Shroud shows no image beneath the blood stains, demonstrating that blood was deposited before the image formed. Garlaschelli's method applied body-image rubbing first and blood afterward — the reverse sequence. He did not successfully replicate the blood-before-image relationship.

**Microscopic image character:**
At the fiber level, the Shroud's image fibers show a specific pattern of discoloration confined to individual fibrils with sharp boundaries between colored and uncolored fibrils. Garlaschelli's reproduction did not replicate this microscopic half-tone structure.

**The face produced by bas-relief vs. bas-relief over volunteer:**
Garlaschelli used a bas-relief specifically for the face (because pressing linen over a live face creates unacceptable distortions), while the body was made over a live volunteer. This is a methodological inconsistency — it is unclear whether any single medieval forger would have used two fundamentally different techniques for face and body.

### 3.4 Responses from STURP-Aligned Researchers

**Fanti, G. & Heimburger, T. (2011).** Letter to the Editor: Comments on "Life-Size Reproduction of the Shroud of Turin and Its Image" by L. Garlaschelli. *Journal of Imaging Science and Technology*, **55**(2). Published in the same journal as Garlaschelli's paper.

Key points from Fanti and Heimburger's critique:
- The reproduction fails to replicate the extreme superficiality of the image (200 nm fiber-surface depth).
- The blood-before-image sequence is not reproduced and cannot be reproduced by any technique that applies image coloring first.
- The VP-8 3D analysis of Garlaschelli's image shows systematic distortions absent from the Shroud.
- The microscopic half-tone structure of individual image fibrils is not replicated.
- They concluded: "the Shroud image was not produced by the technique proposed by Garlaschelli and still remains not reproducible."

Also relevant: Heimburger, T. (2009/2010). Comments on "Recent Experiment of Professor Luigi Garlaschelli." Archived at shroud.com. This detailed technical analysis itemized the specific physical properties that the reproduction failed to match.

## 4. The Art-Historical and Iconographic Argument

### 4.1 The Strongest Art-Historical Argument for Medieval Origin

The convergence of several art-historical observations constitutes a cumulative case for a 14th-century origin:

**1. Iconographic consistency with medieval Catholic Christ imagery:**
The body image's overall composition — long-haired, bearded man with arms crossed over the groin, wound marks consistent with narrative crucifixion accounts — matches the iconographic conventions of 14th-century Passion imagery in northern France and the Savoy. The spiky crown-of-thorns (as opposed to a smooth circlet) does not appear in Christian art until the 12th century, and was common in the 14th century.

**2. The "photographic negative" is not unique to body imprints:**
As noted above (§2.2), critics point out that many medieval drawing and rubbing techniques can produce negative-tonal images. The claim that no medieval forger could have understood negativity is weakened by Nickell's demonstration that the bas-relief rubbing technique produces a negative image as a natural consequence of the method, not as a conscious choice.

**3. The grisaille parallel (McCrone):**
McCrone specifically identified the image technique as analogous to **grisaille** — a 14th-century monochromatic painting technique used for simulating sculptural relief, employed by artists including Simone Martini (c.1284–1344). The use of dilute iron earth pigment in a collagen tempera to build up tonal gradations is consistent with documented 14th-century workshop practice.

**4. Anatomical anomalies consistent with artistic convention:**
Multiple anatomical features are inconsistent with a realistic corpse but consistent with medieval artistic conventions:
- The right arm and fingers are **elongated disproportionately** (needed to achieve the modest covering of the genitals without raising the shoulders — anatomically impossible in a truly supine corpse).
- The arms are **asymmetrical**.
- The **shoulders are too narrow** relative to the chest width.
- The **neck is too long**.
- The **forehead is too short**.
- The feet are depicted inconsistently: the frontal image shows feet angled upward, while the dorsal image shows the right sole resting flat — incompatible postures.
- The figure's implied height (~180 cm, approximately 6'2") would have been extraordinary for 14th-century Europe.

A 2024 paper (published online 2024, found in ResearchGate): *Unveiling Deception: A Comprehensive Analysis of the Shroud of Turin's Anatomical Anomalies and Artistic Liberties*, identifies these features as reflecting deliberate artistic choices to maintain modesty conventions — not features of a real human corpse.

**5. The William S.A. Dale art-historical position:**
Art historian William S.A. Dale (University of Western Ontario) argued on art-historical grounds that the Shroud is an icon rather than a burial shroud — one produced for liturgical use. Though Dale placed it in the Byzantine period (~11th century) rather than 14th-century France (making his analysis actually more inconvenient for the simplest forgery hypothesis), his work confirms that the image's style is art-historical in character rather than anatomically realistic. Notably, Casabianca et al. (2026, see §6) cited Dale against Moraes, pointing out that even Dale's art-historical dating undermines the specific claim of a 14th-century Champagne forgery.

**6. Emily Craig and Randall Bresee's dust-transfer experiment:**
Craig, E.A. & Bresee, R.R. (1994). Image formation and the Shroud of Turin. *Journal of Imaging Science and Technology*, 38(1), 59–67. They demonstrated that a **dust-transfer technique** using collagen dust (dry carbon-based pigment applied to paper and transferred to cloth by pressure) could produce:
- A reddish-brown image with lifelike tonal gradation
- A three-dimensional-appearing positive likeness
- No brushstrokes visible
This demonstrated that medieval artists had access to multiple techniques — not merely wet painting — capable of producing complex tonal images on cloth.

### 4.2 Are There Known Medieval Artists or Workshops That Could Have Produced It?

No specific medieval artist has been unambiguously identified as the creator. The d'Arcis memorandum states that Henri de Poitiers identified an artist who confessed, but **the name was not recorded** in any surviving document. This absence of a name is cited by both sides: skeptics say the failure to name the artist simply reflects incomplete record-keeping; proponents say it undermines the reliability of the d'Arcis claim.

From a technical capability standpoint, the consensus among art historians who accept a medieval origin is that 14th-century workshops possessed the necessary skills:
- Northern French and Burgundian artists of the mid-14th century had extensive experience with monochromatic devotional images.
- The grisaille technique (McCrone) was well-established by 1355.
- Bas-relief rubbing for textile decoration was a known practice.
- The production of large-format devotional images on linen was documented in late medieval France.

The argument that "no medieval forger could have produced it" is not established in the peer-reviewed art-historical literature. It is asserted by some Shroud proponents but has no systematic support from art historians specializing in medieval French textile art.

## 5. The Historical Argument

### 5.1 The d'Arcis Memorandum (c.1389)

**What it is:** A draft memorandum — actually preserved in two slightly differing draft versions — written circa 1389 by **Pierre d'Arcis**, Bishop of Troyes, addressed to **Pope Clement VII** (the Avignon antipope at the time of the Western Schism). The cloth was being publicly exhibited in Lirey, in d'Arcis's diocese, by the family of the dean of the Lirey collegiate church (the heirs of Geoffrey de Charny).

**What it says (key passage):**

> "...the said cloth had been cunningly painted, the truth being attested by the artist who had painted it, to wit, that it was a work of human skill and not miraculously wrought or bestowed."

D'Arcis attributes this investigation to his predecessor **Bishop Henri de Poitiers** (Bishop of Troyes c.1354–1370), who first encountered the Lirey exhibition. D'Arcis claims Henri discovered the fraud through diligent inquiry, obtained a confession from the artist, and suppressed the exhibition — only for it to be revived decades later.

**Strengths as evidence:**

1. It is the earliest surviving explicit statement from a named episcopal authority declaring the cloth a forgery.
2. It claims a **contemporary confession from the artist** — not a later inference.
3. The timing is right: Henri de Poitiers would have investigated the cloth approximately 1355–1370, precisely when it first appeared.
4. D'Arcis was in direct ecclesiastical jurisdiction over the Lirey church — he had personal and institutional reasons to investigate, and access to local testimony.
5. It is corroborated in timing by the Nicole Oresme document (see §5.3).

**Weaknesses and proponent responses:**

1. **The artist is unnamed.** D'Arcis does not give the artist's name in any surviving draft. This weakens the evidentiary force, since we cannot verify the claim independently.
2. **The drafts lack a date, formal signature, and polished Latin** — suggesting they were working drafts rather than a finalized official communication to Rome. Proponents argue this reduces their evidentiary weight as official records.
3. **D'Arcis had institutional and financial motives.** The exhibition attracted pilgrims and offerings to the Lirey church, potentially at the expense of d'Arcis's own see. Proponents argue his complaint was partly motivated by jurisdictional competition.
4. **The Pope's response was dismissive.** Clement VII did not declare the cloth a forgery; instead he issued a bull permitting its exhibition under the condition that it be announced as a "representation" (*figura*) of the Shroud rather than the actual shroud. Proponents argue that if the forgery was so clear, the pope would have acted more decisively.
5. **Ian Wilson and others argue the d'Arcis/Henri investigation concerned a *different* cloth** — a painted replica rather than the cloth now in Turin. This hypothesis is largely rejected by mainstream historians but remains in circulation.
6. **Hearsay:** D'Arcis was writing about events his predecessor Henri allegedly witnessed decades earlier. He was not personally present at Henri's investigation. The claim rests on d'Arcis's account of what Henri reportedly found, with no direct corroboration from Henri's own surviving records.

**Scholarly assessment:** Most historians of medieval relics treat the d'Arcis memorandum as significant but not conclusive in isolation. It is meaningful corroborating evidence for a 14th-century origin, particularly when combined with the Nicole Oresme text, the radiocarbon dating, and the absence of pre-14th-century documentation. It is not sufficient on its own, given the absence of the artist's name and the indirect evidentiary chain.

### 5.2 Nicole Oresme's *Problemata* (c.1370–1382) — The Newly Rediscovered Evidence

**The discovery:** Belgian-French researcher **Nicolas Sarzeaud** (Université Catholique de Louvain / Villa Médicis, Rome), working with historians **Alain Boureau** and **Béatrice Delaurenti**, identified a passage in Nicole Oresme's *Problemata* that explicitly denounces the Lirey Shroud as a fraud.

**Publication:** Sarzeaud, N. (2025). A New Document on the Appearance of the Shroud of Turin from Nicole Oresme: Fighting False Relics and False Rumours in the Fourteenth Century. *Journal of Medieval History*, **51**(5), 604–621. Published online August 28, 2025.

**Nicole Oresme** (c.1320–1382) was one of the most important French scholars of the 14th century — mathematician, physicist, philosopher, Bishop of Lisieux, and advisor to King Charles V. He is not a figure of marginal credibility.

**What the passage says:**
In the *Problemata*, a collection of questions on unexplained natural phenomena (mirabilia) composed between approximately 1370 and his death in 1382, Oresme warns against false clerical testimony and cites the Lirey Shroud as a "patent" (*clair*) example of clerical fraud:

> "I do not need to believe anyone who claims: 'Someone performed such miracle for me', because many clergymen thus deceive others, in order to elicit offerings for their churches. This is clearly the case for a church in Champagne, where it was said that there was the Shroud of the Lord Jesus Christ."

**Significance:**
1. This is now the **oldest known written rejection** of the Lirey cloth, predating the d'Arcis memorandum (c.1389) by approximately 10–20 years.
2. Oresme was writing contemporaneously with the cloth's first appearance — he was alive when it first appeared and describes it as a current example of clerical fraud.
3. His status as a philosopher and royal counselor gives the passage exceptional intellectual authority for the period.
4. The passage shows that the forgery was not only alleged by interested local bishops but was apparent to independent, internationally significant French intellectuals of the day.

**Note on the *Problemata* dating:** The document is dated to 1355–1382 based on manuscript scholarship. Sarzeaud's analysis suggests the relevant passage was written c.1370, after the initial suppression of the exhibition by Henri de Poitiers but before the d'Arcis letter.

### 5.3 The Absence of Pre-14th-Century Documentation

**The skeptical argument:**
A burial cloth that had enveloped the body of Jesus of Nazareth — were it genuine — would be one of the most significant objects in Christendom. The complete absence of any certain reference to such a cloth in any Christian, Jewish, or pagan source before the 14th century requires explanation.

**Proponents' main counter-argument — the Ian Wilson / Mandylion hypothesis:**
Ian Wilson (1978, *The Shroud of Turin*, Doubleday) proposed that the Shroud is identical with the **Mandylion of Edessa** — a famous early Christian image of Christ's face that was brought to Constantinople in 944. Wilson hypothesized that the Mandylion was the Shroud folded in fourths (*tetradiplon*) so that only the face was visible.

**Why this hypothesis is rejected by most scholars:**
1. **The Mandylion descriptions don't match:** All early descriptions of the Mandylion describe it as a facial image only — not a full-body image. No surviving description mentions wounds, a body, or crucifixion marks.
2. **Different dimensions:** The Mandylion is consistently described as a small cloth or tile (Greek: *keramion*); the Shroud is 4.4 × 1.1 m.
3. **Independent Mandylion tradition:** The Mandylion has its own documentary tradition (from Edessa, described as produced miraculously during Jesus' lifetime) that is wholly incompatible with a burial shroud.
4. **Andrea Nicolotti** (University of Turin), the leading specialist on the Mandylion, concluded in his 2014 monograph (*From the Mandylion of Edessa to the Shroud of Turin*, Gorgias Press) that "the Mandylion and the Shroud are two distinct objects" and that the identification rests on an accumulation of special pleading rather than documentary evidence.

**The skeptical weight of the documentation gap:**
Historians of medieval relics (e.g., Caroline Walker Bynum, Benedicta Ward) note that the 14th century was a period of extensive relic fabrication in France, particularly in the aftermath of the Fourth Crusade's dispersal of Constantinople's relic treasures. The sudden appearance of a highly specific and dramatically effective devotional object in the possession of a French knight with Crusader family connections is entirely consistent with the pattern of 14th-century relic forgery.

### 5.4 Bishop Henri de Poitiers: Did He Identify a Forger?

As discussed in §5.1, the key claim is from d'Arcis, not from Henri's own surviving records. What the historical record *actually* says:

- **Henri de Poitiers** investigated the Lirey exhibition, probably c.1355–1360.
- He reportedly suppressed the exhibition (the cloth was withdrawn from public display for some decades after his investigation).
- D'Arcis (writing ~30 years later) states that Henri "discovered the fraud and how the said cloth had been cunningly painted, the truth being attested by the artist who had painted it."
- **No document from Henri himself survives** that describes this investigation, names an artist, or declares the cloth a forgery.
- **The artist's name is not given** in the d'Arcis memorandum.

The inference that Henri identified a specific forger is based entirely on d'Arcis's retrospective account. This is important for assessing the d'Arcis memorandum: it is hearsay about a prior event, not a first-hand report.

## 6. The Moraes 2026 Study and the Casabianca Commentary

### 6.1 Moraes (2026) — Full Study Details

**Citation:**
> Moraes, C. (2026). Image Formation on the Holy Shroud — A Digital 3D Approach. *Archaeometry*, **68**(1), 84–93. DOI: 10.1111/arcm.70030

*(Preprint posted on SSRN: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5003510. Published in Archaeometry volume 68.)*

**Author:** Cicero Moraes is a Brazilian 3D graphics designer and forensic artist who has published forensic facial reconstruction work; he is not a material scientist or archaeologist by training. He used free, open-source 3D software: **MakeHuman** (parametric body modeling), **Blender** (fabric dynamics simulation), and **CloudCompare** (3D point cloud comparison).

**Research question:** Does the shape of the body imprinted on the Shroud correspond better to (a) a full three-dimensional human body, or (b) a low-relief sculpture (a shallow, flat surface with raised anatomical features)?

**Methodology:**

1. Moraes constructed a parametric 3D digital model of a human male body using MakeHuman, calibrated to a height of 180 cm.
2. He ran a **fabric dynamics simulation** in Blender to model how a linen cloth would drape over (a) the full 3D body and (b) a low-relief (bas-relief) version of the same figure.
3. He generated **contact area maps** showing which parts of the draped cloth would contact the underlying form and at what angle/intensity.
4. He compared the contact area maps from both models against the Shroud image intensity data derived from a 1931 photograph (the Enrie photograph).

**Key finding — the "Agamemnon Mask Effect":**
When a cloth drapes over a full 3D human body, the cloth wraps around the sides of the body, stretching and distorting the contact image laterally — producing a wider, distorted figure. Moraes named this distortion the **"Agamemnon Mask Effect"** by analogy with the gold funerary mask from Mycenae, which appears wide and distorted because it was hammered over a three-dimensional face.

By contrast, when the cloth drapes over a low-relief form, the cloth lies nearly flat over the raised areas with minimal lateral distortion. The contact area map from the low-relief model matches the proportions of the Shroud image more closely than the full-body model.

**Conclusion by Moraes:** The Shroud image is more consistent with having been produced over a **low-relief sculpture** than over a real human body. This supports a medieval artistic origin rather than an imprint of an actual corpse.

**Significance:** This is a new computational formalization of a hypothesis that had been discussed qualitatively since at least the 1980s (cf. Nickell's bas-relief rubbing). Moraes provides a quantitative, replicable digital simulation.

### 6.2 Casabianca et al. Commentary (2026)

**Citation:**
> Casabianca, T., Marinelli, E., & Piana, A. (2026). Commentary on the Article "Image Formation on the Holy Shroud — A Digital 3D Approach" by Cicero Moraes. *Archaeometry*. DOI: 10.1111/arcm.70109

**Authors:** Tristan Casabianca is a French independent researcher who has published on the Shroud in *Archaeometry*, *The Heythrop Journal*, and *Theology and Science*. Emanuela Marinelli is an Italian sindonologist aligned with the authenticity position. Alessandro Piana is a physicist.

**Key criticisms:**

**Methodological flaws:**
1. The simulation modeled only the **frontal image**, ignoring the dorsal (back) image and the relationship between the two.
2. The body model had **reversed anatomical orientation** — left and right hands/feet were swapped relative to the accepted reading of the Shroud.
3. The height of 180 cm is outside the accepted anthropometric estimate for the Shroud figure (generally cited as 173–177 cm), and Moraes does not justify this choice.
4. The simulation used **cotton** rather than linen in the fabric dynamics model, despite the Shroud being linen — which has different mechanical properties.
5. The comparison relied on a **single 1931 photograph** (the Enrie photograph) despite the availability of modern high-resolution multispectral imaging data.
6. **No quantitative metric of similarity** was defined — Moraes described the low-relief model as "more compatible" with the Shroud without providing a specific measurable comparison standard.

**Physical/scientific omissions:**
1. The **extreme superficiality** of the Shroud image (~200 nm depth on individual cellulose fibrils) is not explained by any contact-transfer mechanism — whether from a body or a bas-relief. The commentary argues that this physical fact is decisive and Moraes's model does not address it.
2. The **multiple independent confirmations of the presence of blood** (spectroscopic, chemical, enzymatic) are incompatible with any medieval artistic technique involving iron oxide rubbing, and Moraes's paper does not engage with this evidence.

**Art-historical problem:**
The commentary notes that Moraes draws on the art-historical work of **William S.A. Dale**, who — contrary to Moraes's use of him — argued that the Shroud was **not** produced in 14th-century France but was a Byzantine icon produced at least 200 years earlier and 2,000 km away in the Byzantine sphere. Moraes uses Dale to support a low-relief origin while ignoring that Dale's own dating undermines the specific 14th-century Champagne forgery narrative.

**Prior art ignored:**
The commentary notes that variants of the bas-relief hypothesis had already been examined and rejected in the early 1980s (specifically by STURP researchers), and that the problem of cloth-draping distortion had been analyzed as early as 1902 by the French scientist Paul Vignon.

**Fallacy of composition:**
Casabianca et al. argue that Moraes's explanatory strategy commits a fallacy of composition — drawing elements from different historical periods and geographical contexts (a Byzantine-style image, a Champagne church, a 14th-century forger) and combining them without evidence that these elements ever coexisted.

### 6.3 Moraes's Response

**Citation:**
> Moraes, C. (2025/2026). Response to the Comments of Casabianca et al. (2025). *Archaeometry*. DOI: 10.1111/arcm.70112

Moraes maintains his core finding — that the low-relief contact pattern better matches the Shroud image than a full 3D body — and disputes the specific technical objections. The exchange was ongoing as of early 2026 in the Archaeometry correspondence pages.

## 7. The Mainstream Scientific Consensus

### 7.1 The 1989 *Nature* Paper — Still the Benchmark

**Citation:**
> Damon, P.E., Donahue, D.J., Gore, B.H., et al. (1989). Radiocarbon dating of the Shroud of Turin. *Nature*, **337**(6208), 611–615. DOI: 10.1038/337611a0

**Results:** Three AMS radiocarbon laboratories tested samples from a single corner of the Shroud in 1988:
- University of Arizona: conventional radiocarbon age 646 ± 31 BP
- ETH Zurich: 676 ± 24 BP
- University of Oxford: 750 ± 30 BP

**Calibrated calendar age range (95% confidence): AD 1260–1390.**

*Nature*'s summary statement: "These results provide **conclusive evidence** that the linen of the Shroud of Turin is mediaeval."

This range coincides exactly with the cloth's first certain documented appearance (Lirey, ~1355–1356), and with the d'Arcis/Henri de Poitiers timeline.

### 7.2 What Scientific and Skeptical Organizations Say

**Center for Inquiry / Skeptical Inquirer (CSI):**
The Committee for Skeptical Inquiry (CSI, formerly CSICOP) has maintained through multiple publications and blog posts (authored largely by Nickell until his death in 2025) that the Shroud is a proven medieval fabrication, the evidence converging from: the C14 date, the d'Arcis confession, McCrone's pigment identification, Nickell's artistic replication, and the absence of pre-14th-century documentation. The CSI position is that "scholarship and science have proven the Turin 'shroud' a fake."

**Mainstream archaeology:**
The consensus in mainstream archaeology and medieval history is that the Shroud is a 14th-century devotional object. This is the default working assumption in professional literature on medieval relics (see, e.g., the treatment in Robert Bartlett's *Why Can the Dead Do Such Great Things?* [Princeton UP, 2013] and in general medieval history textbooks).

**Nature (1989):**
The *Nature* paper's "conclusive evidence" language — from a peer-reviewed study in the world's highest-impact general science journal — remains the most widely cited single statement of scientific consensus.

### 7.3 A Recent Systematic Review

**Citation:**
> Casabianca, T. (2024). Systematic Evaluation of Recent Research on the Shroud of Turin. *Theology and Science*, **23**(1). DOI: 10.1080/14746700.2024.2436781

This paper reviews four decades of research using epistemological tools including argument mapping and Bayesian analysis. Its conclusion — that the evidence provides "a warrant for the proponents' belief in the authenticity hypothesis" — is notable for coming from a peer-reviewed academic journal, though Casabianca is explicitly aligned with the authenticity position and is not a neutral reviewer.

For a more straightforwardly skeptical review, the Damon et al. 1989 paper itself serves as the scientific summary, and the Wikipedia article "Radiocarbon dating of the Shroud of Turin" (regularly reviewed by scientists) summarizes the consensus that "no radiocarbon dating expert has asserted that the dating is substantially unreliable" and that alternative theories "have been disproved by scientists using actual Shroud material."

### 7.4 The Catholic Church's Official Position

**What the Church says:**
The Catholic Church has never declared the Shroud authentic, and has explicitly declined to do so. The official position is custodial: the Diocese of Turin holds the cloth as a devotional object. Key statements:

- **Pope John Paul II (1998):** "Since it is not a matter of faith, the Church has no specific competence to pronounce on these questions. She entrusts to scientists the task of continuing to investigate."
- **Pope Francis:** Consistently used the word "**icon**" (rather than "relic") when referencing the Shroud — a deliberate theological distinction. An icon is a devotional representation; a relic is a physical remnant of a holy person. Francis's language implicitly acknowledges uncertainty about authenticity.
- The Church's authorization of the 1988 C14 testing was itself a signal of openness to scientific determination, not a dogmatic commitment to authenticity.

**How skeptics interpret the Church's caution:**
The Church's refusal to declare the Shroud authentic is interpreted by skeptics as institutional prudence in the face of scientific evidence pointing to medieval origin. If the Church had strong confidence in authenticity, it would have little reason to defer to science and disclaim competence. The term "icon" — adopted by the most recent popes — is seen as a quiet acknowledgment of the medieval-artifact hypothesis.

## 8. Summary: The Convergent Skeptical Case

The mainstream skeptical position rests on multiple independent lines of evidence, each pointing to a 14th-century origin:

| Evidence | Strength | Key citation |
|---|---|---|
| Radiocarbon dating (1260–1390 AD) | Very strong — three independent labs, peer-reviewed in *Nature* | Damon et al., *Nature* 337 (1989) |
| D'Arcis memorandum (artist confessed) | Moderate — hearsay, artist unnamed, but corroborated | D'Arcis, c.1389 |
| Nicole Oresme's *Problemata* (patent fraud) | Moderate — independent, contemporary source | Sarzeaud, *Journal of Medieval History* 51(5), 2025 |
| Absence of pre-14th-century documentation | Strong — inexplicable if genuine | Wilson Mandylion hypothesis fails scholarly scrutiny |
| McCrone: iron oxide pigment in image areas | Disputed — but never definitively refuted on its own terms | McCrone, *Accounts of Chemical Research* 23 (1990) |
| Garlaschelli reproduction: key properties replicated | Partial — superficiality and blood-sequence not reproduced | Garlaschelli, *JIST* 54(4), 2010 |
| Nickell's bas-relief rubbing replication | Partial — negative image and some 3D quality reproduced | Nickell, *Inquest* (1998) |
| Moraes 3D modeling: low-relief more consistent | New quantitative support for artistic origin | Moraes, *Archaeometry* 68(1), 2026 |
| Art-historical anomalies | Moderate — consistent with medieval conventions | Craig & Bresee, *JIST* 38(1), 1994 |

**The key unresolved tension:** The skeptical case is strong on historical and chemical grounds but does not yet offer a single complete mechanistic explanation for *all* observed image properties simultaneously — particularly the 200 nm superficiality, the blood-before-image sequence, and the high-fidelity VP-8 three-dimensional encoding. The STURP conclusion that the image mechanism "remains unknown" is accurate. However, the impossibility of explaining all image properties by current proposed mechanisms does not logically require a supernatural explanation — it may simply reflect an incomplete understanding of what a 14th-century forger actually did, or of natural cloth-aging processes.
