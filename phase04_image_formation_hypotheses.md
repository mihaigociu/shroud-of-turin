# Phase 4 — Image Formation Hypotheses: Systematic Evidence-Based Study

> Part of the [Shroud of Turin Research Plan](shroud_of_turin_research_plan.md). Researched: 2026-04-22.

---

## Preamble: The Constraints Every Hypothesis Must Satisfy

Before evaluating individual hypotheses, it is worth setting out the empirical constraints established by STURP (1978) and subsequent research. Any credible mechanism must account for all of them — not just the ones it finds convenient.

### C1. Extreme Superficiality

The body image is confined to the outermost surface of the linen. STURP fiber analysis (Rogers; Jumper et al.) established that discoloration is limited to the **uppermost 200–600 nm** of individual linen fibers — approximately the thickness of one or two cell-wall layers of a single flax fiber. The image involves no penetration into the fiber interior and no color change in threads below the surface layer. This is the single most restrictive constraint for any proposed mechanism.

- **Primary source:** Jumper, E.J. et al. (1984). "A comprehensive examination of the various stains and images on the Shroud of Turin." *Archaeological Chemistry III*, ACS Advances in Chemistry Series 205, pp. 447–476.
- **Also:** Rogers, R.N. and Arnoldi, A. (2002). "The Shroud of Turin: An Amino-Carbonyl Reaction (Maillard Reaction) May Explain the Image Mechanism." *Thermochimica Acta*, 389(1–2), 26–29. (Separately discusses superficiality.)

### C2. No Pigment, Dye, or Organic Binder in Image Fibers

Extensive microspectrophotometry, X-ray fluorescence (XRF), and microscopy by McCrone (see below), by Adler, and by STURP members found no paint vehicle (oil, gum, egg, gesso) and no continuous pigment coating in the image area. The image is a property of the linen fiber surface itself — not a coating applied to it.

- **Source:** Heller, J.H. and Adler, A.D. (1981). "A Chemical Investigation of the Shroud of Turin." *Canadian Society of Forensic Science Journal*, 14(3), 81–103.

### C3. Three-Dimensional Spatial Encoding

As reported in Phase 1 (Jackson, Jumper, and Mottern, 1976; published Jackson et al., *Applied Optics* 23(14), 1984), the image encodes a monotonic relationship between image darkness and estimated cloth-to-body distance. The VP-8 Image Analyzer converts brightness to altitude and produces an anatomically correct 3D relief. This property is absent from ordinary photographs and paintings.

### C4. Negative Photographic Character

The image is a tonal negative: light areas correspond to body recessions, dark areas to body prominences. This was unrecognized until Secondo Pia's 1898 photograph.

### C5. "Blood Before Image" — Serum Halos and Undisturbed Blood

STURP fluorescence studies showed that under UV light the blood marks are surrounded by **serum halos** — rings of fluorescent albumin extending several mm beyond the dark clot center. This is what a real retracted blood clot leaves on cloth. Crucially, wherever blood was present, the underlying linen fibers show **no image discoloration**. The body image did not form under the blood areas. This has two implications: (a) the blood was deposited first, before the image-forming mechanism acted; (b) the image-forming mechanism was blocked by the blood coating.

- **Source:** Adler, A.D. (1999). "Updating Previous Studies on the Shroud of Turin." In: Berard, A. (ed.), *History, Science, Theology and the Shroud*. Symposium Proceedings, St. Louis. Available via shroud.com.

### C6. No Directionality

Microscopic examination found no brushstroke directionality, no capillary wicking patterns consistent with a liquid applied by brush or sponge.

### C7. Anatomical and Medical Accuracy

The wound pattern is consistent with Roman crucifixion in medically verifiable ways that were either unknown in the 14th century or contrary to medieval artistic convention: nail wounds at the wrists (not palms); two-angle blood flows on the arms consistent with alternating respiratory postures; thoracic wound with blood-serum separation; no visible thumbs (consistent with median nerve damage); scourge marks consistent with two Roman flagrum wielders.

## Hypothesis 1: Medieval Artistic Forgery (Paint or Pigment)

### Background

The oldest recorded challenge to the Shroud is Bishop Pierre d'Arcis's 1389 memorandum to Pope Clement VII claiming his predecessor Bishop Henri de Poitiers (~1354–1370) "found the truth by the artist who had painted it" (cited *subtiliter depicta* — "cunningly painted"). No named artist, no surviving record of the investigation. The memorandum itself is an unsigned draft (Bibliothèque Nationale de Paris).

### 1a. Walter McCrone's Iron Oxide + Vermilion Argument

**Who:** Walter C. McCrone (1916–2002), director of McCrone Associates (Chicago), widely regarded as the world's leading microscopist at the time. Elected a Fellow of the Royal Microscopical Society and the Royal Society of Chemistry.

**What he examined:** Sticky-tape samples taken by STURP member Roger Gilbert from 32 locations across the Shroud during the 1978 investigation. McCrone received these samples for independent analysis.

**Methods:**
- **Polarized Light Microscopy (PLM):** Identified crystal shapes, refractive indices, and birefringence of particles.
- **Microspectrophotometry:** Measured visible-wavelength absorption spectra of individual particles.
- **X-ray Diffraction:** Confirmed mineralogical identity.

**What he found:**

1. **Iron oxide (Fe₂O₃, hematite/ochre):** McCrone found red iron oxide particles distributed throughout the image area. He identified these as consistent with red ochre (hematite), a common pigment in medieval painting. He argued the entire image was formed by dilute iron oxide suspended in a collagen-tempera medium (gelatin), applied in thin washes.

2. **Vermilion (mercuric sulphide, HgS):** In the blood areas, McCrone identified vermilion particles mixed with the iron oxide. Vermilion was the standard red pigment for depicting blood in medieval art.

3. **Collagen tempera binder:** He proposed a proteinaceous binding medium (derived from collagen, possibly hide glue), which would have disintegrated over centuries, leaving the pigment particles embedded in or atop the fibers.

**Key publications:**
- McCrone, W.C. and Skirius, C. (1980). "Light Microscopical Study of the Turin Shroud, I." *The Microscope*, 28(3–4), 105–113.
- McCrone, W.C. (1980). "Light Microscopical Study of the Turin Shroud, II." *The Microscope*, 28(3–4), 115–128.
- McCrone, W.C. (1981). "The Shroud of Turin: Blood or Artist's Pigment?" *Accounts of Chemical Research*, 23(3), 77–83.
- McCrone, W.C. (1999). *Judgment Day for the Shroud of Turin*. Microscope Publications, Chicago.

**STURP's rebuttal:**

STURP members John Heller (hematologist) and Alan Adler (porphyrin specialist) re-examined the same sticky-tape samples and reached different conclusions:

- The iron oxide particles are **not concentrated in the image** — they are distributed across the entire cloth including non-image areas, at densities consistent with **natural iron-rich water** absorbed through flax fiber retting (linen manufacturing involves biological rotting of flax in ponds or rivers, which can deposit iron minerals in cell walls). This is background iron, not applied pigment.
- The density of iron particles in image vs. non-image areas was found **not statistically different** by Adler's analysis.
- When the iron is removed (by chemical sequestration), the image **remains** — proving the image is not formed by the iron oxide particles.
- No paint binder (oil, gum, egg, collagen) was detected in the image fibers by Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) at levels required for a painted image.
- Heller and Adler confirmed the presence of **blood** (porphyrins, protein, albumin, bilirubin) in the red areas, not just vermilion.

**Source:** Heller, J.H. and Adler, A.D. (1981). "A Chemical Investigation of the Shroud of Turin." *Canadian Society of Forensic Science Journal*, 14(3), 81–103.

**Unresolved disagreement:** The McCrone vs. STURP dispute was never resolved through joint re-examination under agreed protocols. McCrone was excluded from the STURP final report. The dispute is, at bottom, methodological: McCrone trusted optical microscopy as definitive; STURP members argued that trace pigments from centuries of touch-ups or contamination (all artworks in medieval churches received occasional restoration) would be present without being the cause of the image.

**What the hypothesis explains:** The negative character (could be intentional artistic inversion, though this would be extraordinarily foresighted for 1350), the general body image. The 1988 C14 date (1260–1390 AD) is consistent.

**What it fails to explain:**
- The 200–600 nm image superficiality: brush-applied paint cannot be limited to single fiber-surface cell walls without penetrating fiber interiors. No known painting technique does this.
- The absence of a detectable paint vehicle or binder in the image fibers when tested by FTIR and chemical methods.
- The 3D spatial encoding: no painter producing a devotional image in 1350 would systematically encode cloth-to-body distances as darkness gradients — this information has no visual value and would only be discoverable by tools invented 500+ years later.
- The "blood before image" constraint: any artist would paint the image first, then add blood.
- The no-directionality finding.

**Current scientific status:** McCrone's specific claim — that iron oxide particles form the image — is rejected by most scientists who have examined the samples directly, including non-STURP researchers. However, some form of artistic forgery remains the default hypothesis for those who accept the C14 date as definitive and interpret the remaining anomalies as unsolved technical questions.

### 1b. Luigi Garlaschelli's 2009 Reproduction

**Who:** Luigi Garlaschelli, professor of organic chemistry, University of Pavia. A long-standing scientific skeptic of Shroud authenticity. Funded in part by CICAP (Italian skeptics' organization). Collaboration with colleagues at the University of Pavia.

**Publication:** Garlaschelli, L. (2009). "Life-size Reproduction of the Shroud of Turin and its Image." *Journal of Imaging Science and Technology*, 53(3), 031908. DOI: 10.2352/J.ImagingSci.Technol.2009.53.3.031908.

**Exact technique:**

Step 1 — Creating the body image:
- A human volunteer was placed supine on a sheet of **pre-aged linen** (artificially aged by heating). Garlaschelli spread the cloth over and around the front of the subject.
- A **dry ferric oxide pigment** (red ochre, Fe₂O₃) was rubbed directly onto the linen over the subject's body using a daubing pad. The pigment was applied dry, with gentle pressure, allowing the cloth-to-body distance to modulate how much pigment transferred (areas closest to the body received more pigment; raised areas received less; non-contact areas received none). This naturally encodes a rough distance-dependent gradient.
- The same process was repeated for the dorsal image.

Step 2 — Acid treatment (producing superficiality):
- The linen was then treated with a **dilute acid solution** (Garlaschelli used hydrochloric acid vapors and/or surface washing). This caused **acidic degradation** of the cellulose at the fiber surface, producing yellowish-brown discoloration **similar in color to the Shroud image**.
- After acid treatment, the **ferric oxide pigment was washed away** — leaving behind only the acid-induced discoloration on the fiber surface, which is chemically similar to aged cellulose.

Step 3 — Blood marks:
- Blood marks were added separately, using real blood or a blood analog, painted onto the already-formed image. (This violated the "blood before image" constraint — see below.)

Step 4 — Artificial aging:
- The cloth was further aged by washing and chemical treatment to simulate centuries of handling.

**Properties successfully matched:**

- **General appearance:** The resulting image, when photographed, shows a recognizable full-length body image in negative tonality, faint and brownish.
- **VP-8 / 3D: partially.** The image showed some 3D-encoding character on VP-8 analysis — not as clean or anatomically accurate as the Shroud but demonstrably non-flat. This was Garlaschelli's main claim: a simple rubbing technique encodes rough distance information.
- **Negative character:** Achieved — pigment transfer naturally inverts light/dark.
- **Gross superficiality:** The acid treatment confined discoloration largely to fiber surfaces, somewhat mimicking the Shroud's superficiality.
- **No paint binder:** The final result contains no organic paint vehicle — the image is chemically cellulose degradation.

**Properties it failed to replicate:**

1. **Sub-200 nm superficiality:** Garlaschelli's acid-treated fibers showed discoloration penetrating significantly deeper than 200–600 nm — into the fiber interior — when examined microscopically. The Shroud's image is confined to a far thinner layer.

2. **Image resolution:** The Shroud image shows fine anatomical detail — individual hairs, fine wrinkles on the face — at sub-millimeter scale. The rubbing technique produces a blurred, low-resolution contact impression; sharp features are lost because cloth does not conform with micron precision to facial contours.

3. **Non-contact areas:** The Shroud image contains information about body parts that were not in contact with the cloth — the sides of the nose, eye sockets, spaces between fingers. A rubbing technique cannot produce image in areas where the cloth did not touch.

4. **Wrap-around distortion absent from Shroud:** When cloth wraps a 3D body, a contact-print should show horizontal and vertical geometric distortions (the cloth stretches and drapes). The Shroud image lacks these expected wrap-around distortions — it looks like a perfect orthographic projection, not a contact impression. Garlaschelli's replica showed the expected distortions.

5. **Blood before image constraint:** Garlaschelli added blood marks after forming the image. The Shroud shows the opposite sequence: blood was deposited first, then the image formed around the blood, leaving un-imaged linen under the clots.

6. **Serum halos:** No serum halo fluorescence was reported in Garlaschelli's replica.

7. **Half-tone effect:** Under microscopy, the Shroud image is formed by discoloration of individual fibers in an **all-or-nothing pattern** — fibers are either fully colored or not colored at all; image intensity is controlled by the **number** of colored fibers per unit area, not by varying the degree of coloration of each fiber. This is a half-tone mechanism. Garlaschelli's rubbing produced variable-intensity coloring of individual fibers, not a half-tone.

**Expert reception:** The replication was widely covered in the popular press (Nature News, BBC) as demonstrating that the image could be produced by medieval methods. The sindonological community, including Adler's successor researcher Giulio Fanti and STURP alumni, criticized the failure to match the microscopic properties listed above. The $1 million Rolfe Challenge (which requires replicating all of the Shroud's known properties) was formally judged not to have been met by Garlaschelli's method.

**Current status:** Garlaschelli's experiment is the best-documented attempt at full-scale medieval replication. It demonstrates the possibility in principle of a low-tech route to a negative, roughly 3D-encoded image. It does not reproduce the full set of microscopic and chemical constraints, particularly superficiality depth and the half-tone mechanism.

### 1c. Other Proposed Medieval Techniques

**Direct rubbing with a brass bas-relief (Joe Nickell, 1978):**
Joe Nickell (*Inquest on the Shroud of Turin*, Prometheus Books, 1983, updated 1998) proposed that a medieval artist placed linen over a life-size bas-relief (carved or cast brass), dampened the cloth, and rubbed it with a pigment-charged daubing pad or "pounce." He produced replicas in the 1970s that showed a negative, somewhat 3D-encoded image. Nickell's replicas share the same failure modes as Garlaschelli's: wrap-around distortion, blurred resolution, depth of coloring in fibers, and no explanation of the blood-before-image sequence.

**Heated bas-relief / contact scorching (Vern Miller variant):**
Proposed that a heated metal bas-relief pressed onto linen could produce a scorch image. Scorches on linen from the 1532 Chambéry fire are visible on the cloth and have been studied spectrally. STURP FTIR analysis (Gilbert & Gilbert 1980; Jumper et al. 1984) found the image does not match the spectral signature of a heat scorch. Specifically, the Shroud image does not fluoresce under UV — heat-scorched areas do fluoresce, making the image-areas spectrally distinguishable from the 1532 fire scorch areas. Also, a scorch produces double-sided discoloration; the Shroud image is single-sided (see STURP finding on reverse-face imaging below).

**Key constraint revealed by 2002 restoration:** When the Holland backing was removed and the reverse face photographed by Mechthild Flury-Lemberg and Giulio Fanti (Fanti, G. and Maggiolo, R. (2004). "The double superficiality of the frontal image of the Turin Shroud." *Journal of Optics A: Pure and Applied Optics*, 6(6), 491–503), it was found that the frontal image is visible — very faintly — on the **reverse surface** as well. This reverse-side image is extremely faint, shows only the face and hands region, and — critically — there is **no coloring of the fibers in the interior of the cloth between the two surface layers**. This means there are two superficial discolorations: one on each face, with uncolored fibers in between. No known painting, printing, or simple scorching technique produces this property.

## Hypothesis 2: Direct Contact / Vaporograph (Body Vapor Hypothesis)

### Origin: Paul Vignon (c.1900–1939)

**Who:** Paul Vignon (1865–1943), French biologist and professor at the Institut Catholique de Paris. One of the first scientists to study the Shroud photographs systematically after Secondo Pia's 1898 discovery.

**The hypothesis:** Vignon proposed in his 1902 book (*Le Linceul du Christ*, translated as *The Shroud of Christ*, London: Constable, 1902) that the image resulted from a **chemical reaction between ammoniacal vapors** emanating from the body (from sweat and decomposing urea, which is hydrolyzed to ammonia) and a **light sensitizing coating on the linen** (he proposed an aloe/myrrh oil burial preparation, which he suggested could act as a sensitizer). In Vignon's model, ammonia vapors rising from the body's skin surface would react with the sensitized cloth, producing dark discoloration at points nearest the body (highest vapor concentration) and progressively less reaction at greater distances — explaining both the negative tonality and the 3D encoding.

**Vignon's supporting observations:**
- He and René Colson conducted laboratory experiments in which ammonia vapor rising from a surface produced progressive discolorations on linen coated with sensitizer.
- The image tones, he argued, varied in a way consistent with diffusing vapor concentration.

### Subsequent evaluation

**What the vapor hypothesis explains:**
- Negative tonality: naturally, as diffusing vapors deposit most at near-contact and least at distance.
- Rough 3D encoding: same mechanism — proximity determines concentration.
- No brush marks: vapor leaves no directionality.

**What it fails to explain:**

1. **Image resolution:** Diffusing ammonia vapors spread laterally and vertically. Simple diffusion physics predicts significant lateral spread ("halation"), producing a blurred image. The Shroud image shows fine anatomical detail. Gas diffusion cannot maintain the spatial coherence required to produce facial features at millimeter-scale resolution. This was analyzed quantitatively by **Jackson, Jumper, and Ercoline (1984)**: they modeled vapour diffusion and found it would predict image widths many times larger than observed.

2. **Non-contact areas:** While vapor can diffuse across air gaps better than contact-staining, it would still decrease strongly with distance. Non-contact areas of the Shroud (sides of nose, eye sockets, spaces between fingers) would need vapor to travel around obstacles — the predicted image there would be fainter and more diffuse than the Shroud shows.

3. **Image geometry / no wrap distortion:** Diffusing vapors from a 3D body would spread in all directions; the resulting image geometry would not be an accurate orthographic projection of the body. The Shroud image, when back-projected onto a 3D body model, fits accurately. Vapors would not produce this geometric accuracy.

4. **Superficiality:** Why would vapor-deposited chemical reaction products be confined to 200–600 nm? Vapors penetrate fabric; the reaction products would be expected throughout the thread, not just on the surface fiber.

5. **The half-tone mechanism:** Fiber-level microscopy shows the all-or-nothing half-tone pattern. A chemical reaction with vapor would vary gradually with concentration, not produce binary-state fibers.

**Laboratory replication attempts:**

Several researchers, building on Vignon's experiments, have attempted to produce satisfactory images by vapor. All have encountered the resolution/lateral-spread problem. John German (1994, unpublished) used urea solutions; Nickell summarized early replication failures. No vapor-deposition replica has been shown to match the Shroud's resolution and geometrical accuracy.

**Current status:** The vaporograph hypothesis is generally regarded as insufficient. Diffusion physics makes the requisite image resolution unachievable. The hypothesis is retained in some hybrid forms (e.g., Rogers's Maillard variant, below) with different proposed reactive species but it still must contend with the diffusion problem.

## Hypothesis 3: Maillard Reaction

### Rogers 2002 Proposal

**Who:** Raymond N. Rogers (1927–2005), physical chemist, Los Alamos National Laboratory (retired). STURP member since 1978. Initially sceptical of all non-natural hypotheses; an empirical, methodical scientist.

**Publication:** Rogers, R.N. and Arnoldi, A. (2002). "The Shroud of Turin: An Amino-Carbonyl Reaction (Maillard Reaction) May Explain the Image Mechanism." *Thermochimica Acta*, 389(1–2), 26–29.

**How the Maillard reaction works:**

The Maillard reaction is a well-known non-enzymatic browning reaction between **reducing sugars** (e.g., glucose, arabinose) and **amino acids or amines**. It occurs when heat, time, or alkaline conditions bring these two chemical classes together. Products — called **melanoidins** — are brown-to-yellow polymers, produced in a variety of configurations depending on the reactants.

Rogers proposed the following scenario:
1. The linen's surface had a thin coating of **starch and saccharides** (reducing sugars), residual from the **sizing** applied to linen during manufacture to smooth yarns for weaving, plus natural surface decomposition products.
2. The buried/wrapped body emitted **amines** (including ammonia from urea decomposition, putrescine from early tissue breakdown, and other volatile amines from sweat) — low-molecular-weight vapors.
3. These amines reacted with the reducing sugars **on the surface of the linen fibers** in a Maillard reaction, producing yellow-brown melanoidins.
4. The reaction would be most intense closest to the body (highest amine concentration) and progressively weaker at greater distances — encoding distance as density of reaction product.

**Why Rogers found this chemically plausible:**
- Linen sizing (starch and gum arabic) is well-documented in ancient and medieval textile production.
- The image color (straw-yellow to sepia) is consistent with melanoidin pigments.
- Melanoidins form on the surface where the sugars are, not in the fiber interior — this naturally explains some degree of superficiality.
- STURP's FTIR spectra showed the image has spectral characteristics of oxidized/dehydrated cellulose, not fully inconsistent with surface melanoidins.

**Rogers further developed this in his 2002 *Thermochimica Acta* paper and in a subsequent 2004 talk at the International Symposium on Archaeometry:**
He proposed that the amine source was not necessarily the body itself (decomposition would take days; the burial was allegedly short) but rather the **skin surface chemistry** — amino acids and amines in sweat concentrating at body surfaces.

**What the Maillard hypothesis explains:**
- Chemical composition of the image (no paint, no foreign pigment) — Maillard products are endogenous modifications of the linen's own chemistry.
- Color (yellow-brown) — consistent with melanoidins.
- Some superficiality — if the reaction is limited to surface-sized fibers.
- Negative tonality and rough 3D encoding — amine concentration gradient with distance.

**What it struggles to explain:**

1. **Image resolution:** The same problem that defeats Vignon's vapor model. Amines diffuse; Maillard products would form wherever amines reached sufficient concentration. Amine diffusion away from the body surface would produce a blurred, halo-diffused image. Rogers acknowledged this as the principal weakness and did not fully resolve it.

2. **Non-contact areas:** Amines would need to diffuse from the body around curved surfaces and through air gaps. Fine feature resolution in non-contact areas remains unexplained.

3. **The half-tone mechanism at fiber level:** The all-or-nothing fiber coloring would not be expected from a continuously varying chemical reaction.

4. **The quantitative superficiality:** While Maillard reaction products form on the sugar-bearing surface, Rogers's own estimate suggested coloring might penetrate through several cell wall layers. Confining it to 200–600 nm requires that essentially no amines penetrate even marginally into the fiber — a very tight constraint on diffusion.

5. **Energy/kinetics:** The rates of Maillard reactions at room temperature, over hours (the proposed duration), are very slow. For the kind of fine, high-contrast imaging the Shroud shows to form over a day or two at room temperature, the amine concentrations would need to be very high — higher than plausible from skin chemistry. Rogers estimated these kinetics and acknowledged the difficulty.

**Experimental tests:**

Rogers performed bench experiments applying amine vapors to sized linen and producing brownish discolorations. He was able to produce yellowing consistent in gross color with the Shroud. He did not claim to reproduce the resolution or the 3D encoding. His experiments were published as supporting plausibility rather than constituting a full replication.

An independent evaluation by **Adler** (before his death in 2004) expressed skepticism about the resolution problem but credited Rogers for identifying a chemically sound pathway. Adler and Rogers remained colleagues with the shared STURP background despite divergent conclusions on this point.

**Current status:** Rogers's Maillard hypothesis is the most chemically sophisticated natural hypothesis. It is testable in principle: one could produce sized linen, expose it to controlled amine concentrations at defined distances, and measure the resulting image sharpness. No group has published a comprehensive, quantitative test as of April 2026. The resolution problem remains the key unresolved difficulty.

## Hypothesis 4: Proto-Photography / Optical Method (Nicholas Allen)

### Allen's Hypothesis (1993–1995)

**Who:** Nicholas P. L. Allen, professor at the Port Elizabeth Technikon (later Nelson Mandela University), South Africa. His hypothesis was developed in his doctoral research and presented in a series of papers from 1993 onward.

**Key publications:**
- Allen, N.P.L. (1993). "Is the Shroud of Turin the first recorded photograph?" *The South African Journal of Art History*, 11, 23–32.
- Allen, N.P.L. (1995). "Verification of the nature and causes of the photo-negative images on the Shroud of Lirey-Chambéry-Turin." *De Arte*, 51, 21–35.
- Allen, N.P.L. (1998). *The Turin Shroud and the Crystal Lens: Testament to a Lost Technology*. Port Elizabeth: Empowerment Technologies. (Book-length treatment.)

**The proposed technique:**

Allen proposed that the Shroud image is a **photographic negative** produced by a genuine photographic process in the 13th or 14th century, using the following sequence:

1. **A large-scale camera obscura**: A tent or chamber at least 5–6 meters long with a small aperture (possibly a large convex lens or crystal) at one end, into which a life-size human subject (a cadaver or living model) was placed in sunlight at one end.

2. **Silver salts**: The linen was pre-treated with a **silver salt sensitizer** — Allen proposed silver nitrate, silver sulfate, or silver chloride (using urine as a source of ammonia and silver-containing compounds). The sensitized cloth was placed as the focal plane of the camera obscura.

3. **UV light**: Medieval glass was transmissive to UV; Allen argued that with an appropriate aperture (possibly a rock crystal, quartz, or large convex lens of clear glass), sufficient UV radiation would reach the sensitized linen to produce silver-reduction reactions in proportion to light intensity — exactly as in modern photography.

4. **Fixing**: The image would be fixed by washing, removing unexposed silver. Allen claimed the required chemistry is entirely achievable with 13th–14th century materials.

**Claimed advantages:**
- **Explains negative tonality**: Photography naturally produces a negative — lighter areas of the subject (flesh in sunlight) expose more silver salt; darker areas (hair, shadow) expose less.
- **Explains 3D encoding**: A 3D subject photographed in a camera obscura projects onto the focal plane with distance-dependent contrast — closer surfaces are brighter and thus expose more. The resulting image encodes depth information in tonal gradients.
- **Explains anatomical accuracy**: A photograph of a real body would by definition be anatomically accurate.
- **No paint or pigment**: If the image is a silver-based photographic print that subsequently lost most of the silver, the chemical signature of the image fiber would be degraded cellulose, not paint.

**Did Allen produce a replica?**

Yes. Allen successfully produced full-scale photographic images on linen using only materials plausibly available in the 14th century (silver-salt sensitized linen, crystal lens, camera obscura, cadaver or mannequin in natural light). His replicas show:
- Full-body negative image on linen
- Rough 3D encoding
- Anatomical accuracy from a real subject

The replicas were published in his 1995 *De Arte* paper with photographic documentation.

**Objections:**

1. **No silver detected in the Shroud image fibers**: If the image were a silver-salt photograph, silver or silver compounds should be detectable in the image fibers by XRF or electron microscopy. STURP's XRF analysis (Morris, Schwalbe, and London, 1980) found no anomalous silver in the image areas. Allen's response: the silver was largely washed away during the fixing step or has been converted to silver sulfide over centuries (invisible to some detection methods). Critics consider this post-hoc and insufficient — with centuries of handling and no further chemistry, some detectable silver should remain.

2. **No other photochemical traces detected**: No silver sulfide, no silver halide, no photographic developing agent residues have been identified.

3. **14th-century optics**: Allen's requirement for a large (>10 cm) high-quality lens capable of transmitting UV at a focal length of several meters. Medieval lenses existed (they appear in illuminated manuscripts from c.1290) but were small, low-quality, and typically lead glass (which blocks UV). A quartz or crystal lens of sufficient aperture and quality is conceivable but would require exceptional craftsmanship. Allen argued rock crystal could serve — rock crystal transmits UV — but no surviving lens of the required size and quality from the medieval period is known.

4. **Exposure time problem**: Silver salts sensitive enough to produce a full-body image on linen in direct sunlight without modern darkroom chemistry would require either very high silver-salt concentration or very long exposure (minutes to hours). A cadaver held motionless for this period is possible; a living subject is not. Allen used cadavers in his experiments.

5. **Superficiality**: A photographic silver image on linen would penetrate through the thread to some extent as silver ions diffuse. Why would the image be confined to 200–600 nm? Allen did not fully address this.

6. **Double superficiality**: The faint image on the reverse face of the cloth (discovered 2002) would not be expected from a single-face photographic exposure.

7. **Concealment problem**: Producing a life-size human photograph in the medieval period would require infrastructure and knowledge far beyond anything documented. The absence of any other example, any written record, or any workshop evidence is striking.

**Current status**: Allen's hypothesis is largely rejected by the scientific community for the lack of silver or photochemical residues in the image fibers. His replication demonstrates that medieval photography was in principle possible (a genuinely interesting result), but does not establish that the Shroud is a photograph. The hypothesis is not considered seriously in mainstream sindonological reviews as of 2020s.

## Hypothesis 5: Corona Discharge / Electrostatic Field

### Origin and Proponents

**Oswald Scheuermann** (German engineer) proposed in the 1980s that a corona discharge or electrostatic field could produce a Shroud-like image. Later developed by **Fanti, Lattarulo, and Marinelli** (2010).

**Key publication:** Fanti, G., Lattarulo, F., and Marinelli, O. (2010). "A New Electromagnetic Theory for the Formation of the Image on the Turin Shroud." In: Proceedings of the International Workshop on the Scientific Approach to the Acheiropoietos Images, ENEA Frascati, Italy, 4–6 May 2010. [Conference proceedings, not peer-reviewed journal.]

### Mechanism

In a corona discharge, a high-voltage electrostatic field ionizes surrounding air and produces a plasma that can chemically modify surfaces. The proposed scenario:

1. The body (or a bas-relief representation) acts as one electrode.
2. The cloth acts as the opposing surface.
3. A strong electrostatic field (possibly triggered by seismic activity, or in some "resurrection hypothesis" versions by an unexplained electromagnetic burst) produces a corona discharge.
4. The resulting ionized air/plasma contacts the linen and causes oxidative degradation of the cellulose surface — producing a yellowish-brown color.
5. Because the discharge is strongest where the field lines are most concentrated — i.e., at points of closest proximity to the body surface — the image density varies with cloth-to-body distance, encoding 3D information.

### Evidence supporting it

- **Laboratory demonstrations**: Various researchers have produced corona-discharge images on linen or paper that show some similarity to the Shroud's general appearance.
- **Explains superficiality**: Plasma-phase reactions would be expected to occur at the surface first, since charged particles are stopped by the first material they encounter.
- **Explains negative tonality and 3D encoding**: Field concentration at nearest surfaces is highest.

### Objections

1. **No verified energetic mechanism**: What would generate a sufficiently powerful electrostatic field around a buried human body? No plausible natural source is identified. Proposed seismic mechanisms are speculative.

2. **Directionality problem**: Corona discharge is omnidirectional from a body-shaped electrode. The Shroud image shows only dorsal and frontal views — why no lateral corona?

3. **Ozone and chemical traces**: Strong corona discharge produces ozone and leaves distinctive oxidation by-products. These have not been identified in the Shroud image areas.

4. **Double superficiality**: If a corona discharge were to cause image formation, it would tend to go through the cloth, not produce two separate surface images with un-imaged interior.

5. **Scale and uniformity**: Producing a uniform, fine-detail corona discharge image at the scale of a full human body with body-accurate detail would require precise control of the electrostatic field to an improbable degree.

6. **Laboratory replications**: Corona discharge images produced in the laboratory show some Shroud-like features (superficiality, negative) but do not reproduce the resolution, the half-tone fiber mechanism, or the double-superficiality.

**Key laboratory experiment:** Di Lazzaro et al. (ENEA Frascati) briefly tested corona discharge effects on linen as part of their broader radiation-hypothesis work and found the images were not as sharply defined as the Shroud image. However, they did not publish a full systematic comparison.

**Current status**: A minority hypothesis. Not supported by any physical or chemical evidence from the Shroud itself (no ozone traces, no corona-specific chemical signature). Largely developed within the sindonological community rather than peer-reviewed materials science. Taken more seriously in the context of the "resurrection hypothesis" (see Section 6) where the electrostatic mechanism is combined with a radiation burst.

## Hypothesis 6: Radiation Hypothesis — Proton/Neutron Flux, John Jackson's Cloth-Collapse Model, and the "Resurrection Hypothesis"

### 6a. John Jackson's "Cloth-Collapse" Model

**Who:** John P. Jackson, physicist, founder of the Turin Shroud Center of Colorado. Former USAF officer, original VP-8 discovery team (1976). Principal organizer of STURP.

**Publication:** Jackson, J.P. (1990). "Is the image on the Shroud due to a process heretofore unknown to modern science?" *Shroud Spectrum International*, 34, 2–29. (Not a peer-reviewed scientific journal.) A version of the argument appears in Jackson, J.P. (1984) and is discussed in Antonacci, M. (2000), *The Resurrection of the Turin Shroud*, M. Evans and Co.

**The model:**

Jackson proposed that the body emitted radiation (he did not specify the type precisely — he used the term "light" and later considered various forms of electromagnetic and particle radiation) during a brief, intense event consistent with a resurrection. In the cloth-collapse model:

1. The body somehow became **mechanically transparent** to the cloth — the cloth began to fall through the body's volumetric space.
2. As the cloth fell through or around the body, it was irradiated by whatever radiation the body was emitting.
3. The amount of irradiation received by any given point on the cloth was proportional to the **time that cloth element spent in proximity to the radiating body**, which in turn depends on the **original distance from the cloth to the body surface** (cloth elements farther from the body, e.g., above the nose, fell through more body-space and received more total radiation).
4. This model was designed to explain why the non-contact areas (like the eye sockets) are imaged — the cloth fell through those areas during the collapse and received radiation.
5. The image would encode **vertical distance** from the original body surface, not horizontal distance, which Jackson argued better fits the VP-8 data than simple cloth-to-body proximity.

**What this attempts to explain:**
- Non-contact areas: solved by cloth-collapse.
- 3D encoding: explained by fall-through distance.
- Superficiality: a brief, intense radiation burst would preferentially affect the surface of the linen first.

**Objections:**
1. Jackson provides no physical mechanism for bodily transparency or for the type of radiation emitted.
2. The model is non-falsifiable as stated — any radiation type could be invoked.
3. The 3D mapping predicted by the cloth-collapse differs subtly from that predicted by cloth-to-body proximity; Jackson argued his model fits better, but the comparison has not been formally settled.

### 6b. The "Resurrection Hypothesis" — Fanti and Others

**Key proponent:** Giulio Fanti, professor of mechanical measurement, University of Padua. A prolific sindonological researcher with declared belief in authenticity.

**Key publications:**
- Fanti, G. and Gaeta, S. (2013). *Il Mistero della Sindone*. Rizzoli, Milan. (Popular book.)
- Fanti, G. (2010). "Hypotheses regarding the formation of the body image on the Turin Shroud: A critical compendium." *Journal of Imaging Science and Technology*, 55(6), 060507. DOI: 10.2352/J.ImagingSci.Technol.2011.55.6.060507.
- Rucker, R.A. (2014). "Nuclear Hypothesis for the Shroud of Turin." Available at tinyurl.com/ShroudNuclear. (Conference paper, not peer-reviewed journal.)

**The proposed mechanism (varied by author):**

Fanti and others have proposed that the image was formed by a brief, intense burst of radiation — specifically **UV radiation**, **proton flux**, or **neutron flux** — emanating from within the body. Scenarios include:

- A burst of **vacuum ultraviolet (VUV) radiation** from the body's surface during a transformation event.
- A flux of **protons** or **neutrons** emitted from nuclear processes within the body's tissues during resurrection.
- Robert Rucker specifically proposed neutron emission: a neutron flux from the body would (a) expose the linen surface, producing image coloration; (b) irradiate the C-14 in the cloth, producing transmutation reactions that would shift the apparent C-14 date forward to a medieval date — thereby simultaneously explaining the image and the "anomalous" 1988 radiocarbon result.

**How it is evaluated scientifically:**

1. **Unfalsifiability**: The resurrection hypothesis is explicitly theological — it proposes a miraculous physical event. Science cannot test for the occurrence of miracles; it can test whether specific predicted physical consequences are found.

2. **The Rucker neutron-flux prediction and its refutation**: Rucker's model predicts that a neutron flux sufficient to form the Shroud image would also activate stable isotopes in the cloth into radioactive forms. Specifically, calcium-40 would be activated to calcium-41 (radioactive, half-life ~1.02 × 10⁵ years). If a medieval date is correct, there would be a detectable excess of Ca-41 in the Shroud. Thomas Phillips (Harvard) noted in 1989 (*Nature* 337, 594) that this could in principle be tested. As of April 2026, the Church has not authorized sampling for Ca-41 analysis, but the test remains in principle available.

3. **Energy scale problem**: The amount of radiation required to produce the Shroud image — superficial discoloration across ~2 m² of linen at nanometer depth — at conventional exposure rates would require an enormous integrated energy output from the body. This has been quantified by various researchers including Di Lazzaro (see Section 7) as far beyond any known biological or chemical process.

4. **Proton/neutron flux problems**: Proton or neutron fluxes at the energies required would cause far more dramatic radiation damage to the surrounding tomb, cloth, and material environment than is consistent with the evidence.

**Current scientific status:** The resurrection hypothesis is not regarded as a scientific hypothesis in the mainstream literature; it is a theological claim that makes some testable peripheral predictions (Ca-41 activation, neutron-flux traces in materials) that have not been falsified simply because the Church has not authorized the tests. Jackson's cloth-collapse model is taken more seriously as a geometric/physical model even by some who do not endorse the theological interpretation, because it attempts to account for the VP-8 3D data quantitatively.

## Hypothesis 7: UV Excimer Laser — Di Lazzaro et al. (ENEA)

### The Experiment

**Who:** Paolo Di Lazzaro, Daniele Murra, Antonino Santoni, Enrico Nichelatti, and Giuseppe Baldacchini. All staff researchers at the ENEA (Italian National Agency for New Technologies, Energy and Sustainable Economic Development) Research Centre at Frascati.

**Key publication:** Di Lazzaro, P., Murra, D., Santoni, A., Nichelatti, E., and Baldacchini, G. (2012). "Superficial and subsurface modifications of linen by short laser pulses in the vacuum ultraviolet." *Applied Optics*, 51(36), 8567–8578. DOI: 10.1364/AO.51.008567.

**Earlier related work:**
- Di Lazzaro, P. et al. (2010). "Deep ultraviolet radiation simulates the Turin Shroud image." *Journal of Imaging Science and Technology*, 54(4), 040302.
- Presentations at the International Workshop on the Scientific Approach to the Acheiropoietos Images (ENEA Frascati, 2010).

### What they did

Di Lazzaro's group used an **ArF excimer laser** (argon fluoride, wavelength 193 nm — deep ultraviolet/VUV) to irradiate small linen samples (threads and fabric pieces). Laser parameters:

- **Wavelength:** 193 nm (ArF excimer laser — vacuum ultraviolet / deep UV)
- **Pulse duration:** ~20 nanoseconds (ns) per pulse
- **Pulse energy:** varied from millijoules to ~100 mJ per pulse
- **Energy fluence at target:** varied from threshold upward; image-like coloration required fluences in the range of **~10–50 mJ/cm²** delivered per pulse
- **Spot size:** small (cm-scale) — full-body imaging would require large-scale beam scanning or many pulses

### What the irradiation produced

At appropriate fluences, VUV laser irradiation of linen produced:

1. **Yellowing/browning of the linen surface** that is **spectrally similar** to the Shroud image when measured by reflectance spectroscopy and FTIR.

2. **Extreme superficiality**: The 193 nm photons are so strongly absorbed by the cellulose surface that they deposit their energy within the outermost 200–400 nm of the fiber — exactly the range observed in the Shroud image. This is determined by the Beer-Lambert absorption depth of linen at 193 nm. This is the strongest result of the ENEA experiments: VUV radiation at this wavelength naturally produces the observed depth of coloration without any special tuning.

3. **Spectral match**: The FTIR and UV-visible reflectance spectra of the laser-yellowed linen were compared with STURP spectral data for the Shroud image fibers. The match was described as "good" — both show oxidized/conjugated cellulose signatures without paint-medium residues.

4. **No scorching or pyrolysis**: At just-above-threshold fluences, the coloration was produced without the fiber damage, cracking, or char associated with heat scorching.

5. **No fluorescence**: Like the Shroud image (but unlike the 1532 fire-scorch areas), the laser-colored areas did not fluoresce under UV — consistent with a non-pyrolytic mechanism.

### How similar to the Shroud?

Spectrally and in terms of superficiality depth: very similar. Chemically (conjugated/oxidized cellulose without binder): similar. The ENEA team used this to argue that VUV radiation is the class of physical agent most consistent with all the measured properties of the Shroud image.

Key statement by Di Lazzaro (2010): "The laser coloring closely reproduces many macroscopic and microscopic aspects of the Turin Shroud image — in particular the ultra-superficiality."

### What the experiment proves and what it does not prove

**What it proves:**
- That VUV radiation at ~193 nm can produce superficial linen coloration with spectral properties matching the Shroud image.
- That VUV radiation offers a physically plausible mechanism for the superficiality depth — a mechanistic explanation grounded in photophysics.
- That such coloration is not equivalent to a heat scorch (different spectral properties, different fluorescence behavior).
- That achieving Shroud-like coloration requires a specific, narrow range of VUV fluence: below threshold, no image; above threshold, fiber damage.

**What it does NOT prove:**
- That the Shroud image was actually formed by VUV radiation.
- That any natural source of VUV radiation existed or could have existed to act on the Shroud.
- That VUV radiation could produce a full-body image with the observed resolution and geometry — the ENEA experiments irradiated small samples, not full-body geometry.
- That the 3D encoding, negative character, and half-tone fiber mechanism can be reproduced by a VUV source.

### The "energy required" problem

Di Lazzaro and colleagues explicitly acknowledged this. To produce the Shroud image by VUV radiation over the full ~2 m² image area, the total energy required (at ~10–50 mJ/cm²) would be:

- At 10 mJ/cm², the front-body image area (~0.4 m² = 4,000 cm²) = **40 joules minimum for the front face area**.
- For the full body area and both faces: on the order of **hundreds of joules of VUV radiation**, delivered in nanosecond pulses.

Di Lazzaro wrote (2012): "A flash of VUV radiation... would require an instantaneous (nanosecond-scale) release of energy of about 34 × 10³ J [34 kilojoules] distributed over the surface of the body" — acknowledging this is "far beyond present-day technology" in terms of simultaneously illuminating a full body surface with nanosecond VUV pulses. Their KrF and ArF lasers irradiated samples of ~cm² area; scaling to full-body requires beam expansion or scanning that was not performed.

**Implication:** The ENEA team's work establishes what class of physical agent is needed to form the image (VUV/deep UV radiation) and that it is physically achievable in principle. It does not provide a source for that radiation and is explicitly agnostic on whether the source was natural, artificial, or miraculous. The team stated publicly: "We are not in a position to affirm whether the Shroud image was formed by a supernatural phenomenon or not; our experiments simply show that intense VUV radiation can produce Shroud-like coloration on linen."

### Comparison with other radiation proposals

The ENEA group also tested **longer-wavelength UV (248 nm KrF laser, 308 nm XeCl laser)**: these produced yellowing too, but the penetration depth was greater than the Shroud image, and the spectral properties did not match as well. They also tested **proton beams** (in collaboration with accelerator laboratories): proton irradiation of linen produced surface modification but with different spectral signatures. VUV at ~193 nm remained the best match.

## Hypothesis 8: Body-in-Linen Contact Variants — Scorching and Thermal Mechanisms

### 8a. Thermal Contact Scorching

**Proposal:** The image was formed by heat from a warm body (living or recently dead) being transmitted to the cloth by direct contact. Warm skin-to-linen contact at elevated temperatures (fever, or artificial heating of a model) scorches the cellulose in contact areas.

**Problems:**

1. **Spectral mismatch:** The 1532 Chambéry fire scorch marks are visible on the same Shroud and have been spectrally characterized. **Gilbert, R.L. and Gilbert, M.M. (1980).** "Ultraviolet-visible reflectance and fluorescence spectra of the Shroud of Turin." *Applied Optics*, 19(12), 1930–1936: the 1532 scorch areas fluoresce under UV; the body image areas do not. This is a definitive spectral distinction: the Shroud image is not a heat scorch.

2. **The body-heat problem:** A living or recently deceased body is at ~37°C. Even a febrile body at 40°C would not, over short periods, scorch linen. Producing a scorch requires temperatures of at least 150–200°C in contact with linen for seconds. A dead body in contact with linen for hours at body temperature produces no scorch.

3. **Superficiality:** Simple contact scorching would produce continuous scorch damage through the fiber cross-section, not a 200 nm surface layer.

4. **Non-contact areas cannot be explained.**

**Status:** Thermal scorching from a body is physically inconsistent with the evidence. The only version with any explanatory value is a heated metal bas-relief (Nickell's variant) — but this too fails the spectral fluorescence test, the superficiality test, and the directionality test.

### 8b. Thermal Bas-Relief (Heated Statue)

**Proposal (various skeptics, 19th-century suggestions):** A heated metal bas-relief of a man's body was pressed against the cloth, scorching it in proximity-dependent fashion.

**Problems:**
- UV fluorescence test: would produce scorch, not Shroud-type image.
- Superficiality: full-cross-section scorching expected.
- Resolution: contact from a bas-relief is mechanically imprecise; fine details would be lost.
- Double-sided image: would not produce a faint reverse image.

**Status:** Not consistent with the photophysical evidence.

## Synthesis: The Current Scientific State of the Problem

### What the Evidence Most Constrains

The following constraints together eliminate most hypotheses:

1. **Superficiality of 200–600 nm** eliminates all liquid-application methods (paint, dye, water-based pigment), all gas-diffusion methods that would be expected to penetrate fabric (vaporograph, Maillard variants at the original Rogers kinetics), and simple contact scorching.

2. **No paint binder / no pigment** eliminates McCrone's hypothesis and any painting technique.

3. **3D encoding without distortion** eliminates simple rubbing techniques (Garlaschelli, Nickell) because rubbing produces wrap-around geometric distortions not seen in the Shroud.

4. **Blood before image** eliminates any technique in which the forger/artist produces the body image and then adds blood (including all standard painting and rubbing techniques).

5. **Double superficiality** (front and reverse surface images with unaffected interior) is extremely difficult to explain by any single mechanism: paint, vapor, corona discharge, and most radiation hypotheses all struggle with it.

6. **Half-tone fiber mechanism** (all-or-nothing fiber coloring with image intensity controlled by fiber density, not fiber darkness gradient) is unexplained by chemical reaction mechanisms (which would produce continuous color gradients) and has not been explicitly tested by the ENEA laser group.

### Where the Remaining Uncertainty Lies

The ENEA VUV laser experiments remain the closest laboratory approach to satisfying all the constraints simultaneously — particularly superficiality and spectral properties. But they leave critical gaps:

- No natural or artificial medieval source of VUV radiation at the required scale and intensity is identified.
- The full-body imaging geometry (non-contact areas, 3D encoding, double superficiality) has not been reproduced at full scale.

The Maillard reaction remains the only purely naturalistic hypothesis with a plausible chemistry. It satisfies the "no pigment" constraint and partially explains superficiality. It fails on resolution, geometry, and the half-tone mechanism. Rogers's work would benefit from a quantitative test in which sized linen is exposed to controlled amine gradients at defined spatial scales and the resulting image sharpness is measured against the Shroud's known resolution.

Rogers himself, in one of his final papers before his death in 2005, wrote: "The image is the result of a chemical reaction between reducing sugars and amines from the body. The exact mechanism of image formation at the fiber level remains uncertain, but the chemistry is sound." He did not regard this as a complete explanation.

**The problem that no hypothesis fully solves**: the double superficiality (Fanti and Maggiolo 2004). No proposed mechanism — whether chemical, photographic, radiative, or mechanical — has been shown capable of producing two very thin surface images on opposite faces of a woven cloth, with entirely unaffected fibers in between, over a continuous body-shaped area. This constraint, discovered only after the 2002 restoration removed the Holland backing, effectively eliminates or severely challenges all pre-2004 hypotheses as they were originally stated.
